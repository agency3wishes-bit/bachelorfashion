## Research aim
DIRECT (recurring consistently across independent WebSearch renderings of the abstract/summary): The paper is framed as a review of the greenwashing research literature, undertaken because "public concern over greenwashing has grown over the last two decades" and academic research on the topic "has increased correspondingly." The stated purpose is to review and analyze the accumulated greenwashing literature "to evaluate trends and progress in the field" and to synthesize empirical and conceptual results, with an explicit further aim of drawing out "implications for the voluntary-mandatory transition of CSR" — i.e., using the review's findings to argue for a shift from purely voluntary CSR toward some degree of mandatory regulation as a remedy for greenwashing.

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT: The central organizing concept of the paper is the "grey zone" — the idea that "the voluntary idea of CSR appears to be a grey-zone that creates space for misleading 'green' communication." This grey zone is presented as the conceptual hinge between legitimate CSR communication and greenwashing: because voluntary CSR disclosure is unregulated and largely unverified, firms can occupy an ambiguous middle ground between genuine sustainability communication and misleading claims.
- DIRECT (recurring across independent search renderings): The review organizes the literature around three themes: "(i) the meanings and forms of greenwashing, (ii) its main consequences, and (iii) the recent conceptualization of corporate social responsibility as a form of regulation against greenwashing practices." This third theme is the most directly relevant to this thesis's coercive-pressure/regulation framing.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 2.1 (greenwashing concept)** and **RQ1**: The "grey zone" concept is directly usable to frame the conceptual boundary problem this thesis addresses — namely, that not all favorable environmental communication is greenwashing, and the ambiguity is structurally produced by voluntary (unverified, unaudited) CSR disclosure regimes. This supports the thesis's need to operationalize a working distinction between legitimate communication and greenwashing via document analysis rather than relying on an assumed bright line.
2.
