## Main findings
1. DIRECT (recurring across all independent WebSearch queries): CSR reporting became more comprehensive across the fashion industry sample over 2013-2016, interpreted by the authors as a response to meet stakeholders' expectations.
2. DIRECT (recurring across all independent WebSearch queries): Reporting of 16 CSR issues showed an overall rise (i.e., more issues addressed and/or addressed more extensively) over the period studied.
3. DIRECT (recurring across all independent WebSearch queries): Labor and environmental issues were the predominant themes across the reports throughout the period.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2 (B2 Fast-fashion model / literature review) and RQ1**: The finding that CSR reporting became more comprehensive and that 16 issues rose in coverage between 2013-2016 is usable as background evidence that both H&M and Inditex (both confirmed, at PARTIAL confidence, to be in the 12-company sample) were already, a decade before our 2022-2025 window, engaged in an industry-wide trend of expanding disclosure — a trend our thesis can position as a baseline against which to assess whether 2022-2025 institutional pressures produced substantive or merely symbolic/expanded disclosure.
2. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2 and RQ1 (institutional pressures / mimetic isomorphism)**: An industry-wide, near-simultaneous rise in CSR-issue coverage across 12 competing fashion companies (including H&M and Inditex) is consistent with — though not explicitly framed by the source itself as — mimetic and normative isomorphism among competitors responding to a shared "global policy agenda of sustainability"; this thesis may use the finding as empirical grounding for that argument while being explicit that the isomorphism/institutional-theory framing is our own interpretive lens, not the authors'.
3. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 3 (Methodology — design parallel) and Methodology generally**: The study's design (longitudinal, multi-year, multi-company content analysis of corporate sustainability/CSR reports coded against a fixed set of themes/issues) is a direct methodological precedent for our own comparative multi-year document analysis of H&M Group and Inditex reports (2022-2025); it can be cited to justify treating year-on-year, theme-level content analysis of sustainability reports as an established and validated approach in the fast-fashion CSR literature.
4. UNVERIFIED as thesis-usable claim: Any specific statement that certain themes were "omitted" or "de-emphasised" over 2013-2016 cannot currently be supported — no snippet confirms this — so the thesis must not claim the source shows selective omission without full-text confirmation; only the "which themes rose / were predominant" side of the focus question is currently evidenced.
5. UNVERIFIED as thesis-usable claim: No company-specific (H&M vs. Inditex) finding from this source can be cited; the thesis should describe this source's findings strictly at the aggregate 12-company/industry level.

## Use in thesis
- **Section 2.2 (B2 Fast-fashion model, literature review)**: Cite as evidence that CSR/sustainability reporting in the fast-fashion and broader apparel industry had already been expanding in comprehensiveness and thematic coverage (2013-2016), including specifically for H&M and Inditex, providing historical/background context for the thesis's 2022-2025 comparative period.
- **Section 3 (Methodology — design parallel)**: Cite as a validated precedent for using longitudinal, multi-company, theme-coded content analysis of corporate sustainability reports as a research design, supporting the methodological choice of directed qualitative content analysis applied comparatively across years and across the two case companies.
- Do not cite this source for: any institutional-theory terminology (coercive/normative/mimetic pressures, decoupling, substantive/symbolic response) as the authors' own framing — these are this thesis's interpretive additions, not confirmed constructs of the article; any claim about omitted/de-emphasised themes; or any H&M-specific or Inditex-specific findings.

## Connection to RQ
- **RQ1 (institutional pressures and corporate responses)**: Indirect, background support — the industry-wide rise in CSR reporting comprehensiveness (2013-2016) across a sample including H&M and Inditex is usable as pre-existing evidence of an industry norm of expanding disclosure, which this thesis's RQ1 analysis (2022-2025) can build on to ask whether the institutional pressures of the later period produced deeper/substantive change or continuation of a pre-existing symbolic/incremental trend. The source itself does not analyse institutional pressures or classify responses as substantive/symbolic/mixed — that classification is this thesis's own contribution.
- **RQ2 (competitive pressure and selective disclosure)**: Indirect support — near-simultaneous, industry-wide increases in reporting comprehensiveness across 12 competing companies is consistent with competitive/mimetic dynamics, but the source does not itself measure competitive pressure or selective disclosure; and the "what is omitted" half of RQ2's selective-disclosure logic is not evidenced by any located snippet (see Evidence gaps).
- **Methodology**: Direct design-parallel support (see "Use in thesis" above) for the comparative, multi-year, theme-coded document analysis approach.

## Limitations of the source
- Evidence base is limited to search-engine-synthesised summaries of the abstract/publisher page; no full text, methods section, coding scheme, or results tables were directly read.
- The 2013-2016 study period predates this thesis's 2022-2025 window by roughly a decade; findings describe an earlier phase of industry CSR reporting and cannot be assumed to generalise to the greenwashing-scrutiny environment (e.g., post-EU Green Claims/CSRD developments) relevant to 2022-2025.
- No confirmed evidence on the theoretical framework used by the authors (institutional theory, legitimacy theory, or stakeholder theory) — any theoretical alignment claimed in this thesis is the thesis's own interpretive framing, not a demonstrated feature of the source.
- No company-specific (H&M vs. Inditex) results were locatable; all findings are aggregate across 12 companies, limiting direct comparability to this thesis's two-case comparative design.
- The "which themes are omitted" side of the assigned focus question remains entirely unanswered by available evidence — a material gap for the specific research use this card was commissioned to serve.
