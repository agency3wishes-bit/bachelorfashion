## Research aim
DIRECT (consistent across multiple independent search results, and confirmed by the article-type label "COMMENTARY — Open Access" appearing in a search-result title for the Springer PDF): This is a commentary-type article (not an original empirical study), whose purpose is to lay out how negative externalities occurring at each step of the fast-fashion supply chain — from water-intensive cotton cultivation, through untreated-dye release into water sources, to low wages and poor working conditions for garment workers — cumulatively constitute what the authors frame as a "global environmental justice dilemma." ## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT: **Environmental (in)justice** as the organizing lens — the claim that environmental and health burdens of fast fashion are not evenly distributed but are disproportionately borne by specific (marginalized, LMIC) populations.
- DIRECT: **Global North–Global South burden-shifting** — fast-fashion *consumption* is concentrated in the Global North, while the associated chemically intensive manufacturing and textile-waste disposal are increasingly outsourced to the Global South, where environmental regulation is comparatively weak or less stringently enforced.
- PARTIAL: The article is positioned as public-health/environmental-health scholarship (published in *Environmental Health*, an occupational/environmental-health journal) rather than a management/IB or CSR-communication piece; it does not appear, at snippet level, to draw explicitly on institutional theory, legitimacy theory, decoupling, or selective disclosure — these are concepts this thesis will need to bring in from other, management-theory sources, using Bick et al. only as an empirical/contextual anchor for what the underlying environmental problem is.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Fast fashion — defined by the widespread sale of inexpensive, quickly-produced clothing — has become a dominant apparel business model and has driven a sharp rise in garment consumption.
2. DIRECT: The environmental and social costs run across the supply chain: water-intensive cotton cultivation; release of untreated dyes/toxicants into local water sources; and low wages and poor working conditions for garment workers.
3. DIRECT: An estimated 85% of clothing consumed in the U.S. ends up in landfills, equating to roughly 80 pounds of textile waste per person per year.
4. DIRECT: Roughly 90% of clothing is produced in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs), and an estimated 40 million workers face hazardous conditions in LMIC garment assembly.
5. DIRECT: Fast-fashion consumption is concentrated in the Global North, while chemically intensive manufacturing lines and textile-waste disposal are increasingly outsourced to the Global South, where environmental regulation is comparatively lacking — so marginalized communities in the Global South disproportionately bear the environmental consequences (a "disproportionate burden," per search-synthesized phrasing).
6. PARTIAL: Health outcomes reported in LMIC manufacturing/disposal contexts reportedly include lung disease, cancer, endocrine disruption, adverse reproductive/fetal outcomes, and injury/death — this list appears consistently in search-engine synthesis but exact sourcing/attribution within the article (i.e., which specific claims are the authors' own synthesis versus citations to specific prior studies) cannot be confirmed without full-text access.
7. PARTIAL: Textile-waste volumes are cited in the range of tens of millions of tons dumped globally (one search result cites 92 million tons in 2015 with a projection toward 148 million tons by 2030), with US-specific landfill figures also cited (approximately 11.3 million tons/year) — these figures appear across search snippets discussing the article's supply-chain/waste argument but could not be confirmed as appearing verbatim, with the same numbers, inside the Bick et al. text itself versus being drawn from adjacent secondary sources citing similar industry statistics.
