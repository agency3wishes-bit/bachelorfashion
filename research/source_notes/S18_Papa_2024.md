## What it says for OUR topic
1. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.4 (Legitimacy & decoupling) and RQ1 (institutional pressures/corporate responses)**: The finding that mandatory ESG/sustainability regulation coincides with improved qualitative ("talk") disclosure but not with matching improvement in real environmental KPIs (resource/energy use) is directly usable as comparative evidence for a "symbolic" or "mixed" corporate response to coercive institutional pressure — i.e., regulation may induce disclosure-level compliance (legitimacy-seeking) without necessarily changing underlying environmental practice. This supports the thesis's core theoretical expectation that coercive pressure does not automatically yield substantive responses.
2. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.4 and RQ1**: The apparent unevenness across environmental sub-dimensions (carbon emissions vs. resource/energy use) is usable to argue that decoupling is not monolithic — companies (or in this thesis's context, H&M Group and Inditex) may decouple selectively, showing closer talk-action alignment on the most regulator/investor-salient metric (carbon) while decoupling more on less-scrutinized dimensions (resource use, water, chemicals, textile waste). This is a transferable analytical logic for RQ1's substantive/symbolic/mixed response coding, even though this source's own empirical setting (Polish listed companies generally, not fast fashion specifically) differs from this thesis's case companies.
3. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.4 and RQ2 (competitive pressure/selective disclosure)**: The talk-vs-KPI gap operationalization is a usable methodological precedent for this thesis's own document-analysis approach to detecting selective disclosure — i.e., contrasting narrative/qualitative claims in sustainability reports against available quantitative indicators as a proxy for identifying selective-disclosure patterns. This supports the thesis's methodological framing for RQ2, though it is not itself evidence about competitive pressure specifically (UNVERIFIED whether the article discusses competitive/mimetic pressure at all).
4. UNVERIFIED — maps to **RQ1 (coercive pressure test)**: This thesis's working question "does ESG regulation reduce or shift decoupling?" cannot yet be answered from this source at more than a descriptive level; the source appears to show decoupling persisting under regulation for certain KPIs, which if confirmed would support a "regulation does not eliminate decoupling, only shifts its locus" argument — but this causal/comparative claim needs full-text verification before being used as a firm proposition in the thesis.
