## Research aim
DIRECT (snippet-level, consistent and near-verbatim across multiple independent search results): "This article focuses on trustworthiness based on a review of previous studies, [the authors'] own experiences, and methodological textbooks. Trustworthiness was described for the main qualitative content analysis phases from data collection to reporting of the results." One secondary source additionally frames the article's guiding question as: "What is essential for researchers attempting to improve the trustworthiness of a content analysis study in each phase?" The paper is explicitly positioned as addressing a gap — that "few articles have examined the trustworthiness of content analysis use in nursing science studies" — and its stated purpose is to scrutinize trustworthiness across every phase of the analysis process, not only to report it after the fact.

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (snippet-level, recurring across independent sources): The article treats **credibility, dependability, confirmability, transferability, and authenticity** as the five terms most commonly used to describe the trustworthiness of qualitative research, and states that "the current consensus is that credibility, transferability, dependability, confirmability, and authenticity are the five most relevant terms for determining the trustworthiness of research."
- DIRECT (snippet-level): Trustworthiness is organized around three sequential **phases of qualitative content analysis**: **preparation, organization, and reporting**. The article "offers a checklist to improve the trustworthiness of a qualitative content analysis study at each of three phases: the preparation, organization, and reporting phases."
- DIRECT (snippet-level): In the **preparation phase**, the process "consists of collecting suitable data for content analysis, making sense of the data, and selecting the unit of analysis." The **unit of analysis** is highlighted as a recurring weak point: it "has often been inadequately described in previous scientific articles, making it difficult to evaluate how successful the analysis was."
- DIRECT (snippet-level): The **organization phase** is explicitly differentiated by analytic approach:
  - In **inductive** content analysis, organization includes "open coding, creating categories, and abstraction."
  - In **deductive** content analysis, organization involves "categorization matrix development, whereby all the data are reviewed for content and coded for correspondence to or exemplification of the identified categories." (This deductive/categorization-matrix description is the closest documented analogue to directed content analysis relevant to this thesis, though the article's snippets use the term "deductive" rather than "directed"; see Evidence gaps.)
- PARTIAL: The **reporting phase** is described only at a general level in available snippets — as the endpoint of the process where results and trustworthiness of the analysis are communicated — without further detail on specific reporting techniques being independently verifiable at snippet level.

## Method and data of the article
DIRECT (snippet-level): This is a methodological/discussion article, not an empirical study — it has no primary dataset, sample, or case; it is a review-based, textbook-and-experience-informed discussion of trustworthiness. It is framed within nursing science as the originating disciplinary context (the article "examined the trustworthiness of content analysis use in nursing science studies"), consistent with its authors' health-sciences affiliations. ## Main findings
1. DIRECT: The trustworthiness of qualitative content analysis should be evaluated separately, and specifically, at each of three phases of the research process — preparation, organization, and reporting — rather than assessed only as a single global judgment at the end of a study.
2. DIRECT: Five terms — credibility, dependability, confirmability, transferability, and authenticity — represent the current consensus vocabulary for discussing trustworthiness in qualitative research generally, and the article maps these onto content analysis specifically.
3. DIRECT: In the preparation phase, careful and explicit selection and description of the unit of analysis is presented as essential to trustworthiness; inadequate description of the unit of analysis is flagged as a common and evaluable weakness in published content-analysis studies.
4. DIRECT: The organization phase differs by analytic strategy — inductive content analysis proceeds via open coding, category creation, and abstraction, while deductive content analysis proceeds via development and application of a categorization matrix against which all data are reviewed and coded for correspondence.
5. DIRECT: The article provides a practical checklist, organized by the preparation/organization/reporting structure, intended to help researchers improve and evaluate the trustworthiness of a content-analysis study at each phase.
6. PARTIAL: Specific checklist items beyond the general phase headings, and the precise content of the reporting-phase guidance, could not be independently confirmed at snippet level and require full-text access.

## Limitations of the source
- This is a generalist, discipline-agnostic (originally nursing/health-science-oriented) methods discussion, not a business/management or corporate-communication-specific methodology source; it must be paired with IB/management-specific document-analysis or directed-content-analysis methods literature (e.g., Hsieh & Shannon on directed content analysis, if included elsewhere in the corpus) for full methodological justification in a business thesis.
- No evidence at abstract/snippet level that the article discusses corporate reporting, sustainability disclosure, greenwashing, or business/organizational documents as an application context; its disciplinary frame is nursing/health science, and no claim of sector relevance to fast fashion or CSR reporting should be made.
- The article's snippet-level content uses "deductive" content analysis terminology rather than "directed" content analysis (Hsieh & Shannon's term, commonly used for theory-driven a priori coding); while conceptually closely related (both involve a priori categories/matrix applied to data), the exact terminological alignment with this thesis's "directed qualitative content analysis" label should be checked against full text and, ideally, corroborated with a source that uses "directed content analysis" terminology explicitly.
- Full text was not accessible via direct fetch (SAGE Journals, a personal-website PDF mirror, ResearchGate, Semantic Scholar, and the SAGE Perspectives blog post all returned HTTP 403 or could not be retrieved), so all findings rest on search-engine-indexed abstract/snippet renderings and secondary summaries (including a 2025 SAGE Perspectives retrospective blog post on the article's citation impact) rather than a verified reading of the full article or exact page locations of each claim.
