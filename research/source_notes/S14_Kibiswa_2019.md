## Research aim
Kibiswa's article is a "How To" methods paper (published in *The Qualitative Report*'s How-To Article series) that sets out to systematize and operationalize **Directed (deductive) Qualitative Content Analysis (DQlCA)** as a distinct analytic tool, illustrated through its application to conflict-analysis research. The stated purpose is to give qualitative researchers — particularly those working in conflict/peace studies but by extension other social-science fields — a structured, replicable procedure for content analysis that is explicitly theory-driven rather than purely inductive. ## Method and data of the article
The paper is itself a methodological/conceptual contribution (not an empirical study with its own primary data set in the conventional sense); it proposes and walks through a procedural framework. PARTIAL: Multiple independent search snippets converge on the same structure: DQlCA is organized into **three main phases** — preparation, organization (data analysis/coding), and reporting — instantiated as an **eight-step procedure**:
- Preparation phase: (1) developing the study's theoretical framework and operational definitions, (2) determining the unit of analysis and sampling the materials to be analyzed, (3) getting a sense of the whole data.
- Analysis/organization phase: (4) coding and organizing the data using a theory-derived coding tool/scheme, (5) making connections between codes, interpreting them and drawing conclusions, (6) verifying the plausibility of interpretations and discussing trustworthiness.
- Reporting phase: (7) producing an appropriate outline for detailed presentation of the research, (8) producing a "thick description" of the research process and findings.
(PARTIAL — this step breakdown is reconstructed consistently across several AI-generated search summaries citing the source, but I have not seen it as a single verbatim block of article text; step numbering/wording should be checked against the PDF.)

## Main findings

- Purpose of DQlCA: it is used "to test, to corroborate the pertinence of the theory/ies guiding the study, or to extend the application of the theory/ies to contexts/cultures other than those in which that/those theory/ies was/were developed." - The coding tool/coding scheme displays: theory-based categories/themes with their codes, theory-based subcategories/subthemes with their subcodes, operational definitions of each (sub)category to identify matching text passages, and the alphanumerical codes assigned to text segments. PARTIAL.
