## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (converging across at least three independent search queries in near-identical wording): the paper defines greenwashing as **the intersection of two firm behaviors: poor environmental performance and positive communication about that environmental performance** — i.e., greenwashing is conceptualized not as a single act but as a state located at the crossing point of two independently variable dimensions, often glossed in secondary literature as "Talk" (quality/positivity of environmental communication) and "Walk" (quality of actual environmental performance), where greenwashing is high positive Talk combined with low/poor Walk.
- DIRECT: the paper organizes the drivers of greenwashing into **three levels: external, organizational, and individual**.
- DIRECT: **external drivers** are further split into (a) non-market/institutional actors — regulators and NGOs — and (b) market actors — consumers, investors, and competitors. The current regulatory environment (described in the paper as, at the time of writing, comparatively weak in the U.S. context, with highly uncertain enforcement) is identified as a key external driver making greenwashing more likely when coercive oversight is limited or its enforcement uncertain.
- DIRECT: **individual-level drivers** are specifically psychological, including narrow (decision) framing, hyperbolic (intertemporal) discounting, and optimistic bias — psychological tendencies the paper argues become more salient, and have a greater distorting effect on managerial decision-making, under conditions of uncertainty and limited or imperfect information (bounded rationality).
- PARTIAL (recurring in search summaries but not independently corroborated at verbatim/page level in this round): **organizational-level drivers** are described as including firm structures, internal processes/procedures, and misaligned incentive systems that make greenwashing more or less likely at the firm level, and the paper offers corresponding managerial recommendations — altering firm structures, instituting processes and controls, and providing incentives/training — as levers to counteract organizational- and individual-level drivers. The precise operational content of the organizational-driver category (e.g., specific named factors such as decentralization, incentive-pay structure, or reporting lines) is not confirmed at snippet level beyond this general characterization.

## Main findings
- DIRECT: greenwashing is best understood as the co-occurrence of poor environmental performance and positive environmental communication, rather than as a single discrete act of lying.
- DIRECT: the drivers of greenwashing operate at three distinct, non-mutually-exclusive levels — external (institutional and market), organizational, and individual (psychological) — implying that greenwashing cannot be fully explained by external regulatory or market pressure alone, since organizational and individual-level factors independently shape whether and how a firm engages in it.
- DIRECT: weak or uncertainly enforced regulation is identified as a key external/institutional condition that increases the likelihood of greenwashing, because it lowers the expected cost of decoupling communication from performance.
- PARTIAL: managerial and policy recommendations following from the driver taxonomy are reported (via search synthesis) as including firm-level structural/process/incentive changes and improved training to counter organizational and individual drivers, alongside external policy recommendations aimed at strengthening the regulatory and market-monitoring environment — the exact list of recommendations was not independently confirmed verbatim in this round.

## What it says for OUR topic
- CLAIM 1 (DIRECT): Delmas & Burbano's **external/institutional versus market driver taxonomy** is a distinct contribution not covered by the existing §2.1 definitions already in the corpus. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) and de Freitas Netto et al. (2020) define *what* greenwashing is (a mismatch between communication and performance, differentiated by level of analysis and communicative form), but neither source, per the existing source notes for S01 and S07, provides a structured account of *why* firms engage in it, differentiated by driver level (external/organizational/individual). Delmas & Burbano's taxonomy fills that gap directly, without duplicating the existing definitional content. → Maps to thesis §2.1, as a complement to (not a restatement of) the existing definitions.
- CLAIM 2 (DIRECT): the "poor performance + positive communication" two-dimensional framing gives the thesis's §2.1 definitional discussion an additional, independently sourced formulation of the mismatch concept, phrased in terms of two continuously variable dimensions (performance quality, communication positivity) rather than a binary present/absent judgment — this nuances, without contradicting, the existing convergence described in §2.1's first paragraph.
- CLAIM 3 (DIRECT, maps to RQ1 and §2.3): the external/institutional driver category (regulators, NGOs) maps closely onto coercive and normative institutional pressure respectively, giving the thesis an additional, independent citation for treating regulatory pressure (coercive) and NGO/societal-expectation pressure (normative) as distinct external drivers of greenwashing-relevant behavior, complementary to (not overlapping with) DiMaggio & Powell's (1983) general institutional-isomorphism mechanisms and Latif et al.'s (2020) empirical operationalization of the same triad.
- CLAIM 4 (DIRECT, maps to RQ1/RQ2 and §2.5): the external/market driver category (consumers, investors, and competitors) is directly relevant to RQ2's focus on competitive pressure — Delmas & Burbano identify competitors explicitly as one of the three market-level external drivers of greenwashing, alongside consumers and investors, giving the thesis's competitive-pressure discussion (currently anchored mainly on Lee & Lee, 2022) an additional, independent theoretical source establishing competitive dynamics as a recognized driver category in the greenwashing literature generally, not only in the disclosure-withholding literature specifically.
- No usable claim for the specific selective-disclosure mechanism (RQ2's core construct): the driver taxonomy identifies competitive pressure as a general driver of greenwashing but, per the evidence gathered in this round, does not itself define or study selective disclosure as a distinct behavioral pattern — that remains the province of Nemes et al. (2022), Bernini & La Rosa (2024), Lee & Lee (2022), and now Marquis, Toffel & Zhou (2016, S33).

## Limitations of the source
- A conceptual/framework-building CMR article rather than an empirical study of firms, disclosures, or the fast-fashion sector; it provides no case-level or fast-fashion-specific evidence and cannot substitute for the corpus's empirical sources on H&M Group or Inditex.
