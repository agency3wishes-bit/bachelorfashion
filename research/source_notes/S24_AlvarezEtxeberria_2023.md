## Method and data of the article
DIRECT/PARTIAL — Longitudinal document/disclosure analysis of a sample of 120 companies over an eight-year period, studying non-financial disclosure behavior in Spain around the transposition of EU Directive 2014/95/EU into Spanish national law (Ley 11/2018). The design explicitly compares two periods — before the law (an "expectations period," when firms anticipate forthcoming mandatory disclosure regulation) and after the law's enactment — and disaggregates results by (a) sector/activity (specifically environmentally sensitive vs. other sectors) and (b) firms' pre-existing reporting "leadership" level (i.e., whether a firm was already a high- or low-level non-financial reporter before the law). Exact variables, coding scheme, statistical method (e.g., panel regression vs. content-analysis-based scoring), and precise sample composition (industries, listed status) were not found in available search snippets and would require full-text confirmation.

## Main findings
- DIRECT — "Before the law, a trend to increase disclosure was observed, especially among environmentally sensible sectors and low-level reporting firms, while afterwards, the trend was reversed except for leading companies, highlighting the prevalence of the mimetic and normative isomorphism." (Consistent, recurring wording across independent WebSearch queries, attributed to the MDPI abstract/summary.)
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — In the anticipatory (pre-law) period, firms in environmentally sensitive sectors and firms that were previously low-level (lagging) reporters were the ones that most increased their non-financial disclosure — consistent with mimetic isomorphism (imitating better-reporting peers to reduce perceived legitimacy/regulatory risk) and/or anticipatory positioning ahead of expected coercive regulation.
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — After the law's enactment, the previously observed disclosure-increase trend reversed for most firms, except for already-leading companies, which continued on their trajectory — interpreted by the authors as evidence that once coercive (legal) isomorphism took over as the operative pressure, the earlier mimetic anticipatory dynamic dissipated for laggards, while normative/leadership-driven disclosure persisted among leaders.
- DIRECT — Contribution statement: "This work deepens understanding of the adoption processes of coercive norms based on mimetic behaviors and coercive isomorphisms and helps in predicting the effect of a given norm after its announcement and approval, supporting more efficient designs for future legislation." (Recurring wording across independent WebSearch snippets.)

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT/PARTIAL — Provides a directly on-point empirical account of mimetic isomorphism operating in anticipation of new non-financial-disclosure regulation (firms increasing disclosure before a law is even enacted, especially laggards imitating leaders/environmentally sensitive peers), which is exactly the mechanism this thesis's RQ1 needs to characterize institutional pressure typologies (coercive/normative/mimetic) affecting H&M and Inditex, particularly around the EU's evolving CSRD/non-financial reporting regime in the 2022–2025 window. Maps to: 2.3 (institutional pressures), RQ1.
2. DIRECT/PARTIAL — The finding that the disclosure-increase trend "reversed" for most firms once the law was actually enacted, except for already-leading firms, is usable evidence for a broader institutional-theory point in this thesis: that anticipatory mimetic behavior and post-enactment coercive compliance are analytically distinct phases, and that firm response (leader vs. laggard) mediates whether disclosure continues to expand or plateaus once compliance becomes mandatory. This supports the thesis's substantive/symbolic/mixed corporate-response typology (RQ1), by suggesting that "leading" firms' continued disclosure growth may reflect more substantive institutionalization, while laggards' reversal after the law suggests disclosure behavior driven by anticipatory/symbolic competitive positioning rather than durable commitment. Maps to: 2.3, RQ1 (this specific substantive/symbolic mapping is our own theoretical extension, not a claim made by the source — see Limitations).
3. DIRECT/PARTIAL — Directly supports RQ2's competitive-pressure/selective-disclosure-convergence framing: the paper shows firms disclosing more when peers (or sector norms) create competitive/legitimacy pressure to keep pace, i.e., peer-driven disclosure convergence in advance of regulation — precisely the "imitate peers" dynamic RQ2 investigates for H&M vs. Inditex. Maps to: 2.5 (competitive pressure / selective disclosure), RQ2.
4. PARTIAL — As a methodological precedent, the paper's design (comparing disclosure behavior across a pre-regulation "expectations" period and a post-regulation period, disaggregated by sector and prior reporting leadership) offers a structural analogy for this thesis's comparative multiple-case design, which likewise needs to track H&M's and Inditex's disclosure behavior across a regulatory-transition window (2022–2025, spanning CSRD's phase-in). Maps to: Methodology (3.4).
5. UNVERIFIED — No evidence found that the paper discusses the fast-fashion sector specifically, or H&M/Inditex by name; its "environmentally sensitive sectors" finding is a general cross-sector category, not confirmed to include or specifically discuss apparel/textiles. Any fast-fashion-specific claim attributed to this source must be flagged as our own extrapolation.

## Connection to RQ
- RQ1 (institutional pressures / corporate responses): strong, direct relevance. The source's central finding — mimetic isomorphism operating in anticipation of coercive regulation, followed by a reversion to (or persistence of, for leaders) more limited disclosure growth once the law takes effect — is directly usable as an institutional-theory-grounded account of how anticipatory mimetic pressure and eventual coercive pressure interact, and as suggestive (though not proven for our specific firms) grounding for distinguishing substantive vs. symbolic/mixed corporate responses by leader vs. laggard status.
- RQ2 (competitive pressure / selective disclosure): strong, direct relevance. The paper's core phenomenon — firms increasing disclosure in step with sector peers/leaders ahead of regulation — is a directly on-point empirical precedent for RQ2's peer-driven, competitive-pressure account of disclosure convergence, even though the source does not use the term "selective disclosure" itself.
