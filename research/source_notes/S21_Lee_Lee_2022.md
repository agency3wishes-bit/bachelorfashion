## Research aim
DIRECT (recurring consistently across independent WebSearch-rendered summaries of the abstract): the study investigates the relationship between the degree of **industry (product-market) competition** and the level of **managers' voluntary disclosure** of greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions information in South Korean listed firms, and additionally examines how **corporate governance structure** moderates that relationship. PARTIAL: one summary frames the underlying mechanism as "fiercer competition within an industry increases the incentive for managers to perform strategic actions to improve their competition status," which is consistent with a proprietary-cost framing of disclosure choice, but this exact framing/wording is not confirmed as a verbatim sentence from the article itself.

## Theory / key concepts
- PARTIAL (recurring across independent search summaries, but expressed as paraphrase rather than confirmed verbatim theory-naming): the study's causal logic is that of the **proprietary cost hypothesis** of voluntary disclosure — as industry competition intensifies, disclosing GHG emissions information carries a higher proprietary cost (risk of revealing competitively sensitive information to rivals), so managers reduce voluntary disclosure to protect/maximize firm value. The term "proprietary cost" recurs across multiple independent search renderings of the findings, which increases confidence that this is the article's own framing, but it was not retrieved as a directly quoted sentence.
- UNVERIFIED: whether the article explicitly invokes agency theory, stakeholder theory, or signaling theory alongside/against the proprietary cost logic could not be confirmed at snippet level; governance is treated as a moderating mechanism but the specific theoretical justification given for *why* governance would weaken (rather than strengthen) the competition-disclosure link was not surfaced in search results.
- UNVERIFIED: the specific operationalization of "corporate governance structure" (e.g., board independence, ownership concentration, foreign ownership, a composite governance index) is not confirmed at snippet level for this thesis's purposes — search results for related but distinct Korean GHG-disclosure studies (e.g., foreign-ownership and carbon-disclosure papers) surfaced in the same search space, and must not be conflated with this article's own governance measure without full-text confirmation.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT (consistent, near-identical wording recurring across three independent WebSearch result summaries): there is a **negative relationship between the degree of industry competition and the level of voluntary disclosure of greenhouse gas emissions information** — i.e., managers in more competitive industries disclose less GHG information.
2. DIRECT (same recurrence pattern): the mechanism given is that **managers reduce disclosure to protect/maximize firm value because higher industry competition raises the proprietary cost of disclosing GHG emissions information** to rivals.
3. DIRECT (recurring consistently): **corporate governance structure weakens (moderates downward) the negative relationship between industry competition and voluntary disclosure** — i.e., stronger governance attenuates the competition-driven reduction in disclosure, implying governance can partially counteract competitive-pressure-driven non-disclosure.
4. UNVERIFIED: whether the paper reports a symmetric/opposite effect (e.g., governance directly increasing baseline disclosure independent of competition) was not confirmed at snippet level; only the moderation of the competition-disclosure link is confirmed.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 2.5 and RQ2 (competitive pressure and selective-disclosure patterns)**: This source is directly usable as core empirical evidence that product-market/industry competition is associated with **reduced**, not increased, voluntary environmental (GHG) disclosure, via a proprietary-cost mechanism. This directly supports RQ2's premise that competitive pressure shapes selective-disclosure behavior, and gives the thesis a concrete, testable directional hypothesis (competition → less/selective disclosure) to compare against H&M Group's and Inditex's actual reporting patterns in the 2022-2025 period.
2. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 2.5 and RQ2**: The finding that corporate governance **weakens** the competition-disclosure relationship is usable to nuance RQ2: selective disclosure driven by competitive pressure is not uniform across firms but is conditioned by internal governance quality, which is relevant to comparing H&M Group and Inditex as two firms with potentially different governance/ownership structures (e.g., family control at Inditex vs. more dispersed ownership at H&M) that could differentially moderate competitive pressure's effect on disclosure.
3. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.5 and RQ1 (institutional pressures/corporate responses)**: While this source is not framed in institutional-theory language, the proprietary-cost/competitive-pressure mechanism can be read as a **mimetic/competitive institutional pressure** analogue in the thesis's own framework — i.e., rivalry-driven disclosure behavior is a form of institutional pressure distinct from coercive (regulatory) or normative (professional/NGO) pressure, and this source supplies empirical grounding for treating "competitive pressure" as an analytically distinct pressure type feeding into RQ1's typology, alongside its primary role in RQ2. This mapping is the thesis's own theoretical extension, not the article's own framing, hence tagged PARTIAL.
4. PARTIAL — maps to **Methodology / section 2.5**: The proprietary-cost logic offers a plausible explanation for **selective disclosure** (disclosing some information, e.g., favorable metrics or narrative claims, while withholding other information, e.g., detailed Scope 3 data or supply-chain specifics) under competitive threat, which is conceptually close to but not identical to this thesis's decoupling/selective-disclosure framework (Legitimacy Theory); the source's disclosure measure is a quantity/extent measure (voluntary disclosure level) rather than a qualitative content-selectivity measure, so the transfer to "selective disclosure of specific content types" (this thesis's operationalization) is an interpretive extension requiring careful framing rather than a direct empirical match.

## Use in thesis
- **Section 2.5 (literature review, competitive pressure and selective disclosure)**: Cite as core empirical evidence for the proprietary-cost hypothesis — that industry/product-market competition is associated with reduced voluntary environmental (GHG) disclosure — to ground RQ2's argument that competitive rivalry (e.g., between H&M Group and Inditex as direct fast-fashion competitors) can plausibly produce selective or reduced disclosure rather than fuller transparency, counter to a naive assumption that competition drives a "race to the top" in sustainability reporting.
- **Section 2.5 / RQ1 framing**: Cite (with appropriate caveats about this being an extension of the source's own framing) as supporting evidence that competitive dynamics function as a distinct pressure type worth considering alongside coercive/normative/mimetic institutional pressures when explaining corporate environmental-communication behavior.
- **Methodology / discussion**: Use to caution that a governance-moderation effect exists in prior literature — i.e., that the relationship between competitive pressure and disclosure is not fixed but conditioned by firm-level governance characteristics — which supports the thesis's comparative case-study design (two firms with different governance/ownership profiles) as a useful way to probe whether governance differences plausibly explain any observed divergence in H&M Group's vs. Inditex's disclosure/selective-disclosure patterns.

## Connection to RQ
- RQ2 (primary anchor): This is the core empirical anchor for RQ2's premise that competitive pressure shapes disclosure behavior, providing a directional finding (competition reduces voluntary GHG disclosure via proprietary cost) directly relevant to explaining selective-disclosure patterns between two direct competitors in fast fashion.
- RQ1 (secondary/extension): Supplies a rationale, requiring the thesis's own theoretical bridging, for treating competitive/mimetic pressure as a factor shaping corporate response type (substantive/symbolic/mixed), since reduced disclosure under competitive pressure could itself be read as a symbolic-leaning or minimal-compliance response.
- Governance moderation finding is relevant to both RQs as a reason to expect **firm-level heterogeneity** in how competitive pressure translates into disclosure behavior, supporting the comparative (H&M vs. Inditex) design rather than expecting uniform effects.

## Limitations of the source
- Empirical context is South Korean listed firms and general GHG-emissions voluntary disclosure — not the fast-fashion/apparel sector specifically, and not framed around greenwashing, Institutional Theory, or Legitimacy Theory; all thesis-relevant connections require interpretive bridging by the thesis author rather than direct transfer of the source's own claims.
- The disclosure measure is a quantitative/extent-based "level of voluntary disclosure," which differs from this thesis's qualitative, content-selectivity-based conception of "selective disclosure" (i.e., which topics/metrics are emphasized vs. omitted); equating the two must be done carefully and explicitly flagged as an analytical extension in the thesis text.
- All evidence gathered is abstract/snippet-level via WebSearch; no full text was read (all direct WebFetch attempts returned HTTP 403), so sample size, time period, exact governance and competition operationalizations, and precise statistical results (coefficients, significance levels) remain unconfirmed.
- Cannot rule out that the "corporate governance structure" moderator in this study is a narrow, Korea-specific construct (e.g., a governance index tied to Korean corporate law/chaebol structures) with limited conceptual transfer to the European/Spanish (Inditex) and Swedish (H&M) governance contexts relevant to this thesis; this should be checked against full text before over-generalizing.
