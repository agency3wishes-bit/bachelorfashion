## Research aim
The article investigates stakeholder support for policy instruments — mandatory versus voluntary/incentive-based — that could contribute to a transition towards a circular and sustainable fashion system in Europe. It sits within an "envisioning/backcasting" exercise that engaged a wide range of fashion-system stakeholders to construct and validate scenario dimensions for reaching a circular fashion system, of the kind subsequently articulated in the EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles. ## Theory / key concepts
- Policy-mix framing: mandatory regulation vs. voluntary/incentive-based instruments as complementary (not substitute) tools. [PARTIAL]
- "30 aspects of a circular fashion system," derived from screening visions/strategies published by supranational bodies (e.g., EU), NGOs, and sectoral organizations — used as the scenario/backcasting framework. [PARTIAL]
- Explanatory mechanism offered for stakeholder support of mandatory rules: a "prisoner's dilemma" logic — individual firms in global value chains cannot unilaterally adopt costly circularity measures without losing competitiveness, hence stakeholders (including industry) call for government intervention to create a "level playing field." [PARTIAL — phrase recurred verbatim across independent search summaries, suggesting genuine abstract wording, but not confirmed against raw HTML]
- References to concrete regulatory instruments under discussion at the time: Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR), the OECD Due Diligence Guidance (2018), and the revision of the Ecodesign Directive into the Sustainable Products Initiative (binding product requirements for durability, reusability, repairability, fibre-to-fibre recyclability, footprint reduction, microplastics). [PARTIAL]
- No explicit institutional-theory or legitimacy-theory vocabulary (coercive/normative/mimetic, decoupling) identified in the retrieved material — the paper is a policy-stakeholder-support study, not an institutional-theory study. Its relevance to our framework is analogical/contextual: it documents anticipated coercive pressure (regulation) and industry's own diagnosis of a mimetic/competitive trap (first-mover disadvantage), which we can use as external corroboration, not as a theory source. ## Method and data of the article
- Mixed-method: online survey plus focus groups, conducted as part of a stakeholder "envisioning exercise" (part of the EU-funded SCIRT project; a companion deliverable "Vision and roadmap towards a circular fashion system," SCIRT D1.4, Oct 2022, appears to share the same empirical exercise). [PARTIAL]
- Survey: distributed via newsletters, social media, and direct mailing sent out by sector federations and research institutes to a broad range of fashion-system stakeholders. [PARTIAL]
- Fieldwork window: survey collected February–May 2022; focus groups held at "User Board" meetings on 11–12 May 2022. [PARTIAL]
- Sample: 382 total survey responses; 61 responses collected by a single major fashion brand among its own employees were excluded to avoid bias, leaving 321 respondents for analysis. [PARTIAL — figure appeared in one WebSearch synthesis only; not cross-confirmed by a second independent source]
- Analysis: stakeholder-support ratings/rankings across the 30 identified circularity aspects, comparing mandatory-regulation options against voluntary/incentive-based options, disaggregated by stakeholder type (implied, but the precise brand-vs-retailer breakdown was not retrievable at snippet level). [PARTIAL]

## Main findings
- Broad, cross-stakeholder support (including, apparently, industry actors) for government intervention and mandatory regulation as part of the policy mix for circular fashion. [PARTIAL]
- Support for mandatory regulation was found to be "high among all types of stakeholders," not confined to NGOs/civil society. [PARTIAL]
- Mandatory regulation is framed by the authors as "a necessary but not a sufficient condition for a transition towards a circular fashion system" — i.e., regulation must be paired with supporting measures such as economic incentives and awareness-building. [PARTIAL — this exact phrase recurred identically across two independent WebSearch queries, which is suggestive of verbatim abstract language, but I have not personally viewed the raw page to confirm word-for-word accuracy or exact context]
- Proposed explanation for industry support of mandatory rules: firms face a "prisoner's dilemma" in global value chains — no single company can afford to move first on costly circularity measures without a competitive penalty, so a level playing field via regulation is preferred over unilateral voluntary action. [PARTIAL]
- No data on H&M or Inditex specifically were found; the study is sector-wide/stakeholder-wide (EU fashion system), not company-specific. [DIRECT — absence confirmed by repeated searches returning no company-specific mentions]

## What it says for OUR topic
- **Thesis section 2.2 / 2.3 (fast-fashion model; institutional pressures) — RQ1 / Methodology**: This source offers third-party, non-brand-authored evidence that the fast-fashion sector as a whole anticipated intensifying regulatory (coercive) pressure in the 2022 window — precisely the start of our 2022–2025 study period — around what became the EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (published March 2022) and adjacent instruments (Ecodesign/Sustainable Products Initiative, EPR, due-diligence). This is useful as background/triangulation evidence that coercive institutional pressure was a live, anticipated reality for the sector during our study period, independent of what H&M/Inditex say in their own reports. [PARTIAL]
- **RQ1 (institutional pressures typology)**: The "prisoner's dilemma" / "level playing field" finding is a fashion-sector-specific articulation of a mimetic-competitive dynamic — firms will not act unilaterally for fear of competitive disadvantage, and therefore call for coercive regulation to level the field. This can support an argument in our RQ1 analysis that industry stakeholders themselves frame regulation as solving a collective-action/mimetic problem, which is conceptually adjacent to (but not identical with) DiMaggio & Powell's mimetic-pressure category and can be cited as corroborating context, not as the theoretical source. [PARTIAL]
- **RQ2 (competitive pressure / selective disclosure)**: Indirectly relevant — a sector that acknowledges a first-mover competitive penalty for going beyond compliance is a plausible structural condition under which selective disclosure (highlighting compliant/voluntary initiatives while downplaying systemic overproduction) becomes rational. However, the source itself does not discuss disclosure or communication practices — this connection is an inference we would be making, not a finding of the article. [UNVERIFIED as applied to RQ2 — this is our own interpretive bridge, not the paper's claim]
- **Section 1.2 (study-period context)**: Confirms that stakeholder-level debate about mandatory vs. voluntary circular-fashion regulation was active in the exact Feb–May 2022 window, just before/around the EU Textile Strategy's publication — useful as a contextual anchor for "why 2022" as the start of our study period.
