## Theory / key concepts
- PARTIAL: Qualitative document analysis (QDA) is characterised (per search-engine-rendered summary, not a confirmed verbatim quote) as "a systematic approach to extracting meaning from documentary evidence," and as "an iterative and introspective procedure wherein the researcher iterates between concept formulation, sampling, data collection, analysis, and interpretation." This description could reflect the paper's own framing or a search-generated paraphrase drawing on document-analysis literature generally (e.g., Bowen 2009); it is not confirmed as the paper's own original definition versus a citation of prior work.
- DIRECT/PARTIAL: The paper's substantive contribution, recurring across independent summaries, is a discussion of using **multiple sources or types of documents for triangulation** as a means of enhancing the **credibility** of qualitative research — i.e., triangulation across document types/sources is treated as a specific rigour technique within QDA.
- PARTIAL: The paper argues for rigour via explicitness — naming researcher assumptions and being transparent about the procedures used in the QDA process — positioned as a lesson learnt from the case study rather than a purely theoretical claim.

## Main findings
1. PARTIAL: Using multiple sources or types of documents for triangulation is presented as a practical technique that enhances the credibility of qualitative document analysis findings.
2. PARTIAL: Rigour in QDA is achieved substantially through explicitness — naming assumptions and being transparent/systematic about the procedures followed during concept formulation, sampling, data collection, analysis, and interpretation.
3. PARTIAL: The paper explicitly frames itself as offering practical guidance ("lessons learnt") for novice researchers on how to apply thematic-analysis-based QDA credibly, implying the existence of concrete strengths/limitations/pitfalls content beyond what is captured at abstract level.
4. UNVERIFIED: Specific named techniques beyond triangulation and explicitness (e.g., a formal audit trail, peer debriefing, member checking, negative case analysis) could not be confirmed as being discussed in this particular article from the available snippets.
