## Research aim
DIRECT/PARTIAL — The article is a systematic literature review (SLR) that, per consistently repeated search-snippet language across independent queries, presents "a rigorous content analysis of 88 studies" and "delineates strategic typologies of greenwashing in sustainability reporting" while "examin[ing] content-analysis-based measurement approaches used to detect it." This framing (aim, scope, corpus size of 88 studies) recurred verbatim or near-verbatim across at least three independent WebSearch queries, which increases confidence it reflects actual abstract wording, but it was never confirmed by directly rendering the MDPI abstract page itself (blocked, see Evidence gaps).

Institutional Theory is not named in any retrieved snippet as an explicit theoretical lens of the review itself.
- PARTIAL — "Several studies adopt a four-quadrant matrix with varying conceptual dimensions, while others classify strategic responses to institutional pressures along a passive–active continuum" (recurring phrasing, two independent WebSearch calls, near-identical wording). This is the closest snippet-level evidence linking the review's synthesis to "institutional pressures" and to a spectrum of corporate response types (passive vs. active), which is conceptually adjacent to our substantive/symbolic/mixed response typology (RQ1) even though it is not phrased in Institutional Theory's canonical coercive/normative/mimetic vocabulary.
- PARTIAL — Decoupling is explicitly named as one greenwashing mechanism: "decoupling between symbol and substance in ESG reporting" is listed among the manifestations catalogued by the review, alongside "disclosure manipulation," "strategic disclosure," and "strategic partnerships," with "fourteen unique manifestations" identified in total (recurring wording, two independent searches).
- PARTIAL — One search summary offers a working definition apparently synthesized from/by the review: greenwashing as "the deliberate or unintended dissemination of misleading, incomplete, or overly favorable sustainability-related disclosures, manifested through selective presentation of information, concealment of negative performance, or the decoupling of reported claims from underlying organizational practices." This directly touches our "selective disclosure" concept (RQ2) but the wording is an AI-generated search summary, not a confirmed verbatim quotation from the article — treat as PARTIAL, not as a citable quote.

## Main findings
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — The review identifies "fourteen unique manifestations" of greenwashing, including disclosure manipulation, decoupling between symbol and substance in ESG reporting, strategic disclosure, and strategic partnerships (consistent wording across two independent WebSearch queries).
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — "The literature proposes a variety of innovative, content-analysis-based approaches aimed at capturing divergences between communicative claims and organizational realities — most notably, discrepancies between disclosure and measurable performance, and between symbolic and substantive sustainability actions, as well as the identification of selective or manipulative communication practices that may signal greenwashing" (near-verbatim, recurring across independent searches).
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — "Analytical techniques commonly focus on linguistic and visual cues in sustainability reports, including tone (sentiment and narrative framing), readability (both traditional readability indices and machine learning–based textual complexity measures), and visual content" (recurring snippet wording) — this is the most directly relevant methodological finding for our codebook, since it catalogues concrete content-analysis indicator families (tone/sentiment, readability/complexity, visual content) rather than abstract typologies alone.
- PARTIAL — Strategic responses to institutional pressures are said to be classified by some reviewed studies "along a passive–active continuum," and by others via "a four-quadrant matrix with varying conceptual dimensions" — suggesting the underlying literature the review synthesizes already contains multiple competing classification schemes for corporate response types, none confirmed to map 1:1 onto our own substantive/symbolic/mixed categories.
- UNVERIFIED — Any claim about specific named indicator variables, exact coding categories, inter-coder reliability statistics, or quantitative results (e.g., prevalence rates of each manifestation across the 88 studies) could not be found at snippet level and must not be cited until full text is obtained.
