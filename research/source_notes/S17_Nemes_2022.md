## Reference
Nemes, N., Scanlan, S. J., Smith, P., Smith, T., Aronczyk, M., Hill, S., Lewis, S. L., Montgomery, A. W., Tubiello, F. N., & Stabinsky, D. (2022). An integrated framework to assess greenwashing. *Sustainability*, 14(8), 4431. https://doi.org/10.3390/su14084431

## Research aim
DIRECT (recurring near-identically across independent WebSearch-rendered summaries/metadata of the abstract): the paper "examines definitions of 'greenwashing' and its different forms, developing a tool for assessing diverse 'green' claims made by various actors." It notes that "significant deception and misleading claims exist both in the regulated commercial sphere and unregulated non-commercial sphere (such as governments, NGO partnerships, international pledges)," with particular concern raised "over rampant greenwashing, particularly regarding rapidly emerging net zero commitments." The stated aim is to produce what is described as "the first actionable tool for analysing the quality and truthfulness of such claims," with the framework said to have "widespread and unique potential for highlighting efforts that seek to delay or distract real solutions ... to tackle multiple climate and environmental crises." This last phrase recurred verbatim/near-verbatim across two independently issued search queries, which increases confidence it reflects genuine abstract wording rather than search-summarizer paraphrase, though it was never read directly from the publisher page (blocked, see Evidence gaps).

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (MDPI metadata, recurring across an independent search not seeded with these terms): the paper's listed keywords are "green claims, greenwashing, greenwashing framework, misleading/deceptive environmental communication, net zero, public relations, selective disclosure, sustainability, transparency." Note that **"selective disclosure" is an author-assigned keyword of this article**, directly supporting its use for the codebook's selective-disclosure indicator.
- PARTIAL/UNVERIFIED: one search response, made in reply to a query that itself asked about "institutional theory" and "legitimacy theory," stated the paper "is based on Legitimacy, Institutional, and Agency theories" and that it aims to "synthesize contemporary research on greenwashing, examining its conceptual foundations, practices, impacts, and the efficacy of regulatory frameworks." Because the query itself named these theories, this claim carries meaningful risk of being a query-echo rather than an independently verified fact about the article's actual theoretical framing, and it is flagged UNVERIFIED pending full-text confirmation — it must **not** be cited in the thesis as evidence that Nemes et al. explicitly deploy institutional/legitimacy theory until checked against the source.
- PARTIAL (search-summarized, not independently verbatim-confirmed as page text): the framework is reported to define greenwashing along a set of named themes/indicators, including **vagueness** ("a claim that is poorly defined/broad, so its real meaning is misunderstood"), **selective disclosure** ("partial, incomplete, and insufficiently contextualized statements that can mislead the public," with emphasis on positive environmental/social performance information over negative), and **unsubstantiated/no-proof claims** ("a claim cannot be substantiated by easily accessible supporting information"). These three map directly onto this thesis's stated codebook indicators (selective disclosure, vague claims, lack of substantiation), but the exact wording, and whether these are the article's own definitions versus a secondary paraphrase, cannot be confirmed without full text.
- PARTIAL: separate search summaries additionally mention **exaggerated claims**, **false narratives/falsehoods**, misleading **imagery**, and **jargon** as further themes in the framework's taxonomy, alongside vagueness/selective disclosure/unsubstantiated claims — consistent with (though not confirmed identical to) the well-known "seven sins of greenwashing" (TerraChoice) lineage that greenwashing indicator literature commonly builds on.
- UNVERIFIED: one search summary (sourced from a query about the framework's scale, drawing on a secondary trade-press article, trellis.net, that could not itself be fetched directly) reported the framework comprises "13 themes" operationalised via "28 questions" covering "lifecycle assessments and carbon credits to marketing budgets and the use of jargon." This specific figure (13 themes / 28 questions) is plausible and internally consistent with the themes described above but was not independently corroborated by a second, differently-sourced query, and should be treated as UNVERIFIED until checked against the primary article or its CSSN working-paper predecessor (both blocked from direct fetch — see Evidence gaps).

## Method and data of the article
PARTIAL/UNVERIFIED: no search result specified the exact methodological procedure (e.g., systematic literature review protocol, number of definitions/frameworks reviewed, expert panel/co-author consensus process) used to build the integrated framework. What is confirmed at DIRECT level is only that the article "reviews definitions" of greenwashing across "regulated commercial" and "unregulated non-commercial" domains and "different forms" of the phenomenon, and synthesizes these into a single assessment tool/framework — i.e., this reads as a conceptual/synthesis paper producing an analytic instrument, not an empirical single-case or multi-case study with its own primary dataset. This is consistent with a large multi-author (10 co-authors, many affiliated with the Climate Social Science Network, CSSN) collaborative synthesis piece. No sample of documents, companies, or claims analysed within this specific 2022 article was identified in search snippets (a follow-up 2025 article by an overlapping author team, "Testing the greenwashing assessment framework," Ecology & Society 30(2):31, appears to be the first paper to empirically apply/test the framework — this is a separate, later publication and is not part of source S17 itself).

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Greenwashing/misleading environmental claims are found across both the regulated commercial sector and the largely unregulated non-commercial sphere (governments, NGO partnerships, international pledges), indicating the problem is not confined to corporate marketing alone.
2. DIRECT: Concerns about greenwashing are described as particularly acute around "rapidly emerging net zero commitments," situating the framework within a contemporary climate-pledge/net-zero accountability debate.
3. DIRECT: The paper's central contribution is presented as an integrated, actionable assessment framework/tool for evaluating the "quality and truthfulness" of green claims, positioned as the first such actionable tool (per the recurring abstract-level phrasing).
4. PARTIAL: The framework operationalises greenwashing through multiple named thematic indicators, at minimum vagueness, selective disclosure, and lack of substantiation/proof, and reportedly also exaggeration, false narratives, misleading imagery, and jargon — though the complete, authoritative list and its exact definitions could not be verified verbatim.
5. UNVERIFIED: The framework is structured around a specific number of themes and diagnostic questions (one secondary source cites "13 themes" and "28 questions"), but this granularity is not independently corroborated at DIRECT level.
6. DIRECT: The framework is explicitly framed as applicable beyond commercial products/advertising, to actors such as governments and NGO partnerships, and to instruments such as international pledges — a scope broader than corporate CSR reporting alone.

## Connection to RQ
- RQ1 (institutional pressures and corporate responses, substantive/symbolic/mixed): Indirect support — the framework's distinction between claims that are substantiated/verifiable versus vague or unsubstantiated offers a conceptual bridge to classifying corporate responses as substantive (verifiable, well-documented action) versus symbolic (vague, unsubstantiated, image-management rhetoric), which is directly useful for operationalising the substantive/symbolic/mixed coding distinction in RQ1's codebook, though this bridge is an inference by this thesis rather than a claim made explicitly by the source itself.
- RQ2 (competitive pressure and selective-disclosure patterns): Direct support — selective disclosure is a named, keyword-level construct of this article and is the most directly relevant indicator for coding selective-disclosure patterns in H&M Group's and Inditex's communications.
- Methodology: Direct support for justifying and citing the codebook's indicator set (selective disclosure, vagueness, lack of substantiation) as grounded in an established, peer-reviewed integrated framework rather than invented ad hoc for this thesis.

## Limitations of the source
- This is a conceptual/synthesis article producing an assessment tool, not an empirical case study of fast fashion, H&M, or Inditex specifically; no evidence was found that the article itself analyses fashion-industry or apparel-sector claims — it appears to address greenwashing generically across sectors and actor types (commercial and non-commercial).
- Several of the most codebook-relevant details (the precise number and definitions of themes/indicators, and whether the "13 themes/28 questions" structure is accurate) rest on search-engine-generated summaries of secondary/trade-press sources rather than verbatim-confirmed article text, and must be checked before being cited with specificity (e.g., exact indicator counts) in the thesis.
- The claim that the article is explicitly grounded in "Legitimacy, Institutional, and Agency theories" is UNVERIFIED and carries a specific, identified risk of being a query-echo artifact of the search process (the query that produced it named those same theories); this should be independently re-verified against full text before being used to justify the B4 (Legitimacy & decoupling) classification in prose.
- All direct WebFetch attempts on the publisher page (MDPI), institutional repository mirrors (Aberdeen, White Rose, Ohio, IDEAS/RePEc), ResearchGate, and the CSSN working-paper PDF predecessor returned HTTP 403; no page-anchored, directly-read evidence exists for this card — everything rests on WebSearch-tool-synthesized snippets.
