## Use in thesis
- Chapter/Section 2.3 (Institutional Theory foundations): cite as an empirical precedent demonstrating that coercive/normative/mimetic pressures can be operationalized as distinct, measurable constructs with independent explanatory power — supports the theoretical legitimacy of treating them as separate coding categories in our directed content analysis.
- Methodology (codebook development for RQ1): cite as justification for using a multi-indicator approach per pressure type, by analogy to Latif et al.'s multi-item survey design (twelve items/three factors) — noting explicitly that our codebook translates this into qualitative indicators for coding corporate disclosures rather than replicating their survey instrument.
- Limitations/reflexivity discussion: usable as a contrast case — Latif et al. is quantitative/survey-based in a non-fashion, developing-country manufacturing context, so its findings support the general institutional-theory taxonomy but cannot be used as direct empirical evidence about fast-fashion firms' disclosure behavior.

## Connection to RQ
- RQ1 (institutional pressures and corporate responses): Primary connection. Supports the theoretical validity and empirical precedent for decomposing "institutional pressure" into coercive/normative/mimetic sub-types as separately codeable categories, which is foundational to the RQ1 coding scheme. Does not directly evidence the "substantive/symbolic/mixed response" typology side of RQ1 — that requires other sources (e.g., Meyer & Rowan-type decoupling literature).
- RQ2 (competitive pressure and selective disclosure): Weak/indirect connection only. Mimetic pressure (competitor-referenced) is confirmed here as a significant driver of EMA adoption, which is a substantive management practice, not disclosure per se — so this source should be cited cautiously for RQ2, primarily as background on how "competitive/mimetic pressure" is defined in the institutional-theory literature, not as evidence about selective disclosure patterns.

## Limitations of the source
- Quantitative, cross-sectional questionnaire study of manufacturing firms in Pakistan — very different institutional, sectoral, and geographic context from the thesis's cases (H&M Group and Inditex, European-headquartered fast-fashion multinationals, 2022-2025).
- Studies EMA *adoption* (an internal management-accounting practice), not corporate environmental *communication/disclosure* — a construct gap relative to our RQ1/RQ2 focus on communication and reporting.
- As a quantitative variance-based study, it cannot directly inform qualitative document-analysis coding procedures; it can only serve as theoretical/construct-validation background, not as a methodological template for content analysis coding.
- Full text unseen; all evidence in this card derives from search-engine snippets, aggregated summaries, and secondary citations, several of which come from papers citing/adapting Latif et al. rather than from the primary source itself.
