## Research aim
DIRECT (snippet-level, from the article's own abstract as rendered by search-engine indexing of the Springer/IDEAS-RePEc landing pages): The paper "aims to define a theoretical background for investigating greenwashing from a business economic perspective, considering possible research questions in the relevant field of study." Its stated steps are to (1) "propose a path to orient scholars to the multifaceted perspectives of greenwashing," (2) "analyze[s] the main theories that can support researchers and might motivate possible greenwashing strategies," (3) "highlight[s] the potential link between greenwashing, reputational and relational capital, and a broad concept of value that includes the social dimension," and (4) "propose[s] a conceptual framework that highlights emerging research issues and anticipates the effects of greenwashing." This confirms the article is a conceptual/theoretical framework-building piece (with a review component), not a primary empirical study of specific firms.

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (snippet-level, abstract-derived): The article explicitly works across multiple theories used in greenwashing research — described in secondary citing sources as spanning "institutional theory to stakeholder, legitimacy, and signaling theories" — and aims to move toward "a more cohesive and integrated framework of these perspectives." This directly supports its CORE-T role for B3 (institutional pressures) and B4 (legitimacy/decoupling).
- PARTIAL: Legitimacy theory is used in the paper's framework to explain greenwashing as a strategy through which firms "seek legitimation... grounded on misleading disclosure" and seek to "align with societal norms and values surrounding environmental responsibility to secure a social license to operate." This phrasing recurs across independent search snippets summarizing/citing the article, but no verbatim quoted sentence with page location could be retrieved.
- PARTIAL: Decoupling is treated in institutional-theory terms consistent with the standard neo-institutional definition — "an organization seeks to gain legitimacy by adopting a façade of acquiescence to widely held societal norms while making — whether intentionally or not — minimal commensurate changes to internal practices." This appears in search results discussing the article's theoretical grounding, but it reads as a general neo-institutional-theory definition (possibly Bromley & Powell-style) rather than a confirmed verbatim sentence unique to Bernini & La Rosa; treat as PARTIAL pending full text.
- PARTIAL: Greenwashing is framed at two levels — action-level and communication-level — with the communication-level split into "selective-disclosure" (a passive-deception strategy: omitting or downplaying negative information) and "misleading-disclosure" (an active-deception strategy: exaggerating or fabricating positive information). This distinction, including the "passive" vs. "active" deception framing, recurs in search-engine renderings that appear to draw on or paraphrase the article's terminology closely, including the quoted terms "selective-disclosure" and "misleading-disclosure" — but it could not be confirmed whether this exact typology is native to Bernini & La Rosa (2024) or attributed by them to prior literature (e.g., Marquis et al., Delmas & Burbano). Flag for full-text verification.
- PARTIAL: Environmental disclosure is framed as a signal (drawing on signaling theory) that can strengthen reputational and relational capital under normal conditions, but whose "signaling power" is exploited in greenwashing because it "derives precisely from information asymmetries, allowing even poor environmental performers to convey misleading messages." This is consistent with a genuine-signal-vs-cheap-talk framing common in the signaling/greenwashing literature and is attributed to this article across multiple independent search summaries.
- UNVERIFIED at the level of a firm-answer: Whether the article takes an explicit position that intention is a *necessary defining condition* of greenwashing, or instead treats greenwashing as potentially occurring "whether intentionally or not" (as the decoupling language above suggests for the general neo-institutional definition it cites). The two pieces of snippet evidence found are in tension (one framing implies deliberate deception — "corporation's deliberate attempt to mislead stakeholders"; another, in the decoupling discussion, allows for unintentional decoupling). This is a key focus question for this card and could NOT be resolved definitively at snippet level — it requires full-text access to the article's own conceptual-definition section.

## Method and data of the article
DIRECT (snippet-level, abstract-derived): The article is structured as a theory-building/conceptual contribution rather than a hypothesis-testing empirical study: it "analyzes the main theories" relevant to greenwashing, links them to a value framework (reputational/relational capital, economic and social value), and "proposes a conceptual framework." A companion/related paper by the same authors (Bernini, Giuliani & La Rosa, 2024, *Business Ethics, the Environment & Responsibility*, "Measuring greenwashing: A systematic methodological literature review") is a distinct, separately published systematic review of empirical greenwashing-measurement studies (1990-2022) — it should NOT be conflated with S05; S05 itself appears to be more conceptual/synthesis-oriented, built on a literature base, but the exact review protocol (database, search string, inclusion criteria, number of articles reviewed) used specifically in S05 could not be confirmed at snippet level. UNVERIFIED whether S05 reports a formal systematic-review sample size or is a narrative/conceptual synthesis without a disclosed PRISMA-type protocol.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: The paper explicitly links greenwashing research to multiple theoretical lenses (institutional, legitimacy, stakeholder, signaling) and proposes an integrative conceptual framework rather than testing a single theory.
2. PARTIAL: Institutional theory is used to explain greenwashing as a strategic response to coercive, mimetic, and normative pressures from regulators, competitors, and societal expectations around environmental responsibility. This is stated consistently across independent search summaries discussing the article's institutional-theory content, but no verbatim sentence from the article itself (with page number) could be retrieved to confirm the precise wording or whether the three pressure types are explicitly named as a triad inside this specific article versus being a general institutional-theory framing applied to it by citing sources.
3. PARTIAL: Legitimacy theory explains greenwashing as an attempt to secure/repair a "social license to operate" by aligning corporate communication with societal environmental norms, independent of underlying substantive performance.
4. PARTIAL: Decoupling is presented as the mechanism connecting institutional pressure to symbolic (rather than substantive) organizational response — adopting the appearance of conformity while making minimal internal change.
5. PARTIAL: The paper links greenwashing to erosion of reputational and relational capital when stakeholders detect deceptive ESG communication, and — per one search summary — notes a practical implication that "self-regulation is not effective in reducing the gap between substantive and symbolic behaviors," pointing toward a need for stronger external regulation/enforcement. This is a notable, thesis-relevant claim but is PARTIAL because it comes from a secondary aggregated summary rather than a confirmed verbatim passage.
6. UNVERIFIED: Any specific typology, number of theories reviewed, number of articles included if a systematic method was used, or explicit page-located definitions of substantive vs. symbolic conformity as coined/adopted specifically in this article (as opposed to cited from Meyer & Rowan / Oliver-type institutional literature) — full text needed.
