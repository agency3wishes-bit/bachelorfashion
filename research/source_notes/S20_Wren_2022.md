## Main findings
1. DIRECT: in 2018 the fashion sector was responsible for approximately 2.1 billion metric tons of GHG emissions, roughly half of which were attributed to fast fashion specifically.
2. DIRECT: fast fashion brands produce high volumes of synthetic, petroleum-based garments largely manufactured in developing countries, generating high levels of emissions and textile waste.
3. DIRECT (recurring across independent summaries): the comparative analysis of H&M's and Everlane's sustainability reports found that fast fashion (as represented by these cases) has failed to sufficiently engage upstream and downstream stakeholders (suppliers and consumers) in its SSCM strategies.
4. DIRECT: H&M's engagement with SSCM/sustainability initiatives is presented as triggered by public backlash and greenwashing accusations; in 2013 H&M introduced its "H&M Conscious" line and eco-friendly product claims, which the Norwegian Consumer Authority reportedly found so misleading as to be considered "illegal."
5. PARTIAL: Everlane is presented as differently positioned, having engaged in SSCM-type practices since its founding, which the article uses as a contrast case to fast-fashion incumbents' reactive sustainability posturing — the specific comparative conclusions drawn from this contrast are only available as paraphrase, not verbatim text.
6. DIRECT (recurring): the article's overall recommendation is that fast-fashion/apparel brands must move beyond short-term fixes (e.g., "conscious collections") and instead invest in long-term decarbonization and energy/processing infrastructure, and engage more substantively with suppliers and consumers, and re-evaluate design standards for products, in order to meaningfully address the climate crisis at the scale required.
7. UNVERIFIED: no quantitative results, effect sizes, or a formal set of "best practices" as a discrete numbered list could be confirmed at snippet level; the "best practices" framing in the title is not yet matched to specific verifiable content beyond the general recommendations in point 6.
