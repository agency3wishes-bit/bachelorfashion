## Research aim
DIRECT (snippet-level, from search-engine-indexed renderings of the article's own abstract via WebSearch): The article "introduces a theoretically driven framework to assess the alignment (or lack thereof) between firms' ESG policies and their actual implementation," with the stated purpose of ensuring "that externally oriented ESG claims are supported by genuine internal actions that benefit both the firm and society at large." The framework was then "empirically validated using data from ASSET4," examining "the extent and persistence of this phenomenon using a sample of S&P 1500 firms from 2016 to 2022." This confirms the paper is a measurement/methods-development article (framework construction + empirical validation), not a case study of fashion firms specifically.

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (snippet-level): The paper is framed around "ESG policy–practice decoupling," i.e., the gap between what firms formally claim/commit to (policy) and what they actually do internally (practice).
- PARTIAL (recurring across independent search summaries, consistent with standard neo-institutional theory but attributed to this article's framing): Decoupling is presented in institutional-theory terms as occurring "when firms adopt external, socially sanctioned practices such as sustainability reporting primarily to gain legitimacy from stakeholders," with environmental and social ESG dimensions experiencing "policy-practice gaps when firms respond to institutional pressures primarily for legitimacy rather than substantive operational change." One search-engine synthesis explicitly linked the paper to "coercive and mimetic pressures, such as tightening disclosure regulations and investor demands, that often lead to symbolic adoption, particularly in contexts with weak enforcement or fragmented oversight" — this is PARTIAL because it is a secondary aggregated summary rather than a confirmed verbatim sentence from the article.
- DIRECT (snippet-level): The framework distinguishes "internal" ESG actions (i.e., substantive/operational practices) from "external" ESG actions (i.e., externally oriented endorsement/communication practices) as the two poles whose (mis)alignment constitutes decoupling — this internal-vs-external action distinction is the paper's core operationalization of the policy/practice (i.e., symbolic/substantive) divide.
- UNVERIFIED: Whether the article explicitly names or cites "Legitimacy Theory" as a named theoretical framework (as opposed to only using decoupling/institutional-theory language) could not be confirmed from search snippets; only the general institutional-theory/legitimacy-seeking framing recurs across summaries.

## Method and data of the article
DIRECT (snippet-level): The paper is a quantitative measurement-framework/methods-validation study, not qualitative document analysis. Key elements confirmed via search snippets:
- Data source: ASSET4 (Refinitiv/LSEG ESG database). One search result specifically references a "Breakdown of ASSET4 data points by categories and ESG dimensions" table in the article.
- Framework construction: DIRECT — the framework "classified 595 data points from ASSET4's reference guide, focusing on 98 internal ESG actions and 59 external ESG actions," retaining "only data items representing internal and external ESG actions applicable across industries," and proposing "a measure to assess decoupling between practices and policies for each of the ESG dimensions" (i.e., separate decoupling indices per E, S, and — per one figure caption referencing a "decoupling index-social dimension" — at least a social-dimension index is explicitly constructed, with an implied parallel environmental-dimension index).
- Sample: DIRECT — S&P 1500 firms, panel period 2016-2022.
- This is explicitly a large-N, database-driven, cross-industry quantitative validation study — methodologically very different from this thesis's qualitative comparative multiple-case document analysis of two named firms, but directly useful as a conceptual/measurement template (see below).

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: "Misalignment between internal actions and external endorsements in managing environmental and social issues is both significant and persistent across the years analyzed, with over 80% of the sample firms exhibiting this misalignment."
2. DIRECT: "In more recent years, firms have shown a clear tendency to prioritize internal actions over initiatives aimed at externally endorsing their efforts" — i.e., a shift in the direction of decoupling over the panel period (toward more substantive/internal action relative to external endorsement), rather than a static gap.
3. PARTIAL: The persistence and prevalence findings are presented as evidence that ESG policy-practice decoupling is a widespread, structural phenomenon among large-cap firms generally (S&P 1500), not an isolated or industry-specific pathology — this generalization is inferred from the reported >80% prevalence figure and multi-year persistence claim, and should be treated as PARTIAL until the article's own discussion/conclusion section is read in full for how the authors themselves characterize the generality of the finding.
