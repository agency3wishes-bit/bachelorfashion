## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT: **Greenwashing** is framed broadly as encompassing "a range of misleading communications that promote unduly positive beliefs regarding an organization's environmental performance" (recurring search-synthesis paraphrase/definition attributed to the article).
- DIRECT: The review organizes the evolution of greenwash research/practice into **three phases**: (a) **Greenwash 1.0 — Static Communication**; (b) **Greenwash 2.0 — Dynamic Management**; (c) **Greenwash 3.0 — Narratives about the Future**. This three-phase labeling recurred identically across independent search results, indicating high confidence it is a genuine, central organizing framework of the article.
- DIRECT: The authors propose a **"Corporate Miscommunication" model**, integrating multiple streams of greenwash research, that specifies how firms miscommunicate with different stakeholders and which stakeholders are most at risk of being deceived.
- DIRECT: A list of **greenwash forms/tactics** recurs across search syntheses attributed to this article (building on and extending the authors' 2015 typology): **selective disclosure**, empty green claims and policies, dubious certifications and labels, co-opted NGO endorsements/partnerships, ineffective public voluntary programs, misleading narrative and discourse, and misleading visual imagery.
- PARTIAL: **Decoupling** is described in search synthesis as referring to situations where entities commit to "symbolic environmental protection behaviours" without following through on them — i.e., a gap between espoused commitment and actual practice. This is consistent with standard institutional-theory usage of decoupling but its exact wording/placement inside this specific article is not independently confirmed at snippet level.
- PARTIAL/UNVERIFIED: A search-engine synthesis (not a verbatim quote) associated "cognitive, pragmatic, and moral legitimacy" (a Suchman-style tripartite legitimacy typology) and an "ExxonMobil CSR/symbolic management" example with Montgomery/Lyon's work; because Montgomery and Lyon have multiple co-authored papers on greenwashing and legitimacy (including work predating this 2024 review), it is UNVERIFIED whether this specific typology/example appears in the 2024 review itself versus being conflated from an earlier or different Lyon/Montgomery publication. ## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Greenwashing has become "more virulent than ever," with proliferating ESG and net-zero commitments increasingly "fraught with questionable and misleading claims," even as underlying environmental/social problems remain unresolved.
2. DIRECT: Greenwash research and practice can be organized into three evolving phases — 1.0 Static Communication, 2.0 Dynamic Management, and 3.0 Narratives about the Future — reflecting a shift from studying discrete misleading communications, to studying how firms dynamically manage green claims/behavior over time, to studying forward-looking narratives (e.g., net-zero pledges, future promises) as a distinct and emerging locus of greenwash.
3. DIRECT: The authors propose an integrative "Corporate Miscommunication" model spanning multiple stakeholder types, intended to clarify which stakeholders are most vulnerable to being misled and how.
4. PARTIAL: The review extends the list of recognized greenwash forms/tactics (selective disclosure, empty claims/policies, dubious certifications/labels, co-opted NGO endorsements, ineffective public voluntary programs, misleading narrative/discourse, misleading visual imagery), situating **selective disclosure** and **decoupling** (a gap between symbolic commitments and actual behavior) as recurring, central mechanisms across the literature.
5. PARTIAL: The "Greenwash 3.0 — Narratives about the Future" phase specifically foregrounds **future-oriented greenwashing**, i.e., forward-looking claims such as net-zero and other long-horizon environmental commitments, as a newer and growing form of potentially misleading corporate communication that the pre-2024 literature had not fully addressed — this is a plausible and recurring inference from the phase-naming and framing found in search snippets, but the article's own detailed argument/evidence for this phase (e.g., specific case examples, criteria distinguishing genuine long-term commitment from future-oriented greenwash) could not be confirmed at snippet level.
6. UNVERIFIED: Specific empirical examples, industries, or companies (if any) used to illustrate each of the three phases, the full content/structure of the "Corporate Miscommunication" model, and the review's concrete research-agenda items (specific gaps/questions proposed for future scholars) could not be located at snippet level and require full-text access.
