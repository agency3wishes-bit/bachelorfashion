## Research aim
DIRECT/PARTIAL — The article proposes an action-oriented sustainability disclosure tool for the fast-fashion sector, shifting analytical focus away from measurement/indicator-style reporting and toward the *actions* companies report taking on sustainability. It does this by content-analyzing the sustainability reporting of "the world's two largest fast-fashion companies" (H&M and Inditex — our exact two cases) in three phases: (1) inductively identifying the sustainability challenges/actions firms report facing and addressing; (2) introducing the UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as an external, non-self-selected framework to check what is/is not being disclosed relative to it (thereby surfacing "orphan issues" — material topics that receive little or no action-level disclosure); and (3) proposing a new disclosure tool, the "Fast-Fashion Sustainability Scorecard." (Synthesized consistently across multiple independent search snippets; abstract not read verbatim, hence PARTIAL on exact wording.)

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — Core problem framing: voluntary sustainability reporting has an inherent "choosing what to disclose" drawback — i.e., firms self-select which topics to report on, which the authors address by introducing an external benchmark (the SDGs) rather than relying on companies' own reported priorities.
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — "Orphan issues" / "orphan material issue(s)": a key analytic concept in the paper, denoting sustainability topics that appear as materially important (e.g., in a "Fast-Fashion Common Materiality Matrix" the authors construct) but that receive disproportionately little disclosed action. Concrete example found via search: Innovation was identified as the most material issue in the Fast-Fashion Common Materiality Matrix, yet only 3.54% of analyzed Reported Action Statements (RASs) addressed Innovation/"Innovation and Technology" as an instrument for action — illustrating a large materiality-versus-disclosed-action gap.
- PARTIAL — The paper analyzes reported sustainability actions by decomposing them into topics, instruments, and actors (an analytic coding scheme, name/structure inferred from a search snippet, not confirmed against the actual coding table).
- UNVERIFIED — No search evidence found that the article explicitly names or applies Institutional Theory, Legitimacy Theory, decoupling, or "selective disclosure" as a labelled theoretical construct. Its own vocabulary appears to be reporting-practice/CSR-disclosure-literature based (voluntary disclosure bias, materiality matrices, SDG mapping) rather than organizational-theory based. The paper is therefore best used in this thesis as an empirical/methodological precedent illustrating the *phenomenon* of selective disclosure (via "orphan issues"), not as a source of the institutional-theory vocabulary itself — that must come from dedicated theory sources. ## Method and data of the article
DIRECT/PARTIAL — Qualitative and quantitative content analysis applied to the sustainability reporting of H&M and Inditex, conducted in three phases as described above. Sample, per search snippets: Inditex data drawn from its 2015 fiscal-year reporting (February 2015–January 2016; i.e., its Annual Report 2015), and H&M data from its 2016 fiscal-year Sustainability Report (December 2015–November 2016). The authors identified 106 sustainability-related "Reported Actions and Statements" (RASs) from Inditex and 138 from H&M, then condensed/harmonized these into 198 primary RASs for cross-case comparison. This is directly relevant as a method precedent for a directed qualitative content analysis of the same two companies' disclosures, though the exact coding protocol, intercoder reliability procedure, and unit-of-analysis definitions have not been confirmed from the full text — treat the RAS counts as PARTIAL (consistent across an independent search summary but not verified against the primary tables).

## Main findings
- DIRECT — Fast-fashion sustainability reporting (H&M and Inditex) "has a broadly developed analysis base, but lacks action orientation" — i.e., reports are rich in describing issues/impacts but comparatively thin on describing concrete actions taken.
- DIRECT/PARTIAL — Applying the SDG framework as an external benchmark reveals "orphan issues" — material sustainability topics receiving minimal disclosed action — broadening the analytical scope "from internal corporate challenges to issues impacting the ecosystems in which companies operate."
- PARTIAL — Concrete illustrative statistic (found via search, not confirmed against the primary source table): only 3.54% of the analyzed RASs addressed "Innovation" as the instrument for action, despite Innovation being identified as the most material issue in the authors' constructed "Fast-Fashion Common Materiality Matrix" — presented as a paradigm case of an "orphan" (materially important but under-disclosed) issue.
- DIRECT — Based on these findings, the authors propose the "Fast-Fashion Sustainability Scorecard," a disclosure framework intended to shift reporting practice (i) from reporting toward action, (ii) from financial performance toward sustainable value creation, and (iii) from corporate boundaries toward value creation for the broader supply-chain ecosystem.
- UNVERIFIED — Any additional company-specific comparative findings (e.g., which specific topics H&M vs. Inditex individually under-report, or any explicit ranking/comparison between the two firms on selective-disclosure patterns) were not surfaced in available search snippets and cannot be reported without full-text access.
