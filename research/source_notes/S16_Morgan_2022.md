## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (recurring across independent snippets): The article treats documents for qualitative analysis against four selection/quality criteria — the documents should be **authentic** (or from a primary source), **credible**, **representative** of the period of investigation, and **meaningful** to represent the context of the agency involved. This four-criteria structure (authenticity, credibility, representativeness, meaning) closely resembles the classic Scott (1990) framework for assessing documentary sources, though the search snippets attribute the four-part list directly to Morgan's own discussion rather than confirming Morgan's explicit citation of Scott; this attribution should be checked against full text.
- DIRECT (recurring across independent snippets): On **authenticity**, the article warns that "researchers need to be sure that the documents they select are not forged," and lists indicators that a document "may be inauthentic," including "inconsistent content such as changes in typography, handwriting, and style of language," the existence of "different versions of the same document," or a document that "might make little sense, contain obvious errors, or come from an unreliable secondary source."
- PARTIAL (search-engine-rendered summary, not verbatim-confirmed as an exact quotation string): On **credibility/bias in organizational documents**, the article is reported to state that documents produced by commercial organizations "are usually intended to create a positive impression," and that "since employees are typically interested in protecting themselves, they often hide their mistakes, leading to differences between what a document contains and what actually happens at their organizations." This is the single most directly relevant passage found for this thesis's corporate self-reporting bias concern, but it was retrieved as an AI-generated search summary rather than as a copy-pasted verbatim excerpt, so it is tagged PARTIAL pending full-text verification of exact wording.
- PARTIAL: On **representativeness**, one summary states the article explains that "representativeness refers to how typical a document is, and if a document contains idiosyncratic content rather than material reflecting other documents on the same topic, it lacks representativeness." Same provenance caveat as above (search-summary paraphrase, not confirmed verbatim).
- DIRECT: The article explicitly discusses and recommends **reflexive thematic analysis** (associated in the qualitative-methods literature generally with Braun & Clarke) as the analytic approach to pair with document analysis, and illustrates its use through a "hypothetical example of how a researcher might conduct a document analysis." UNVERIFIED: the specific step sequence used inside Morgan's own hypothetical example (e.g., whether it follows the standard six-phase Braun & Clarke sequence — familiarization, initial coding, generating themes, reviewing themes, defining/naming themes, producing the report) could not be confirmed from Morgan's article itself at snippet level; the six-phase description found in search results traces to general reflexive-thematic-analysis literature, not to a passage identified as being from Morgan (2022).

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Document analysis is described as an underused qualitative method whose main practical advantages are that it allows researchers to study pre-existing texts without the resources or time required for field research, and that it can reduce some ethical concerns associated with other qualitative methods (e.g., no direct human-subject data collection).
2. DIRECT: Selecting documents for qualitative analysis should be guided by four criteria: authenticity, credibility, representativeness, and meaning.
3. DIRECT: Authenticity should be actively checked for signs of forgery or inconsistency (e.g., changes in typography/style, conflicting versions of the same document, internal errors, or unreliable secondary provenance) rather than assumed.
4. PARTIAL: Credibility assessment for organizational/commercial documents specifically should account for a self-interested reporting bias — such documents tend to be produced to create a positive impression of the organization, and internal actors (employees) have incentive to conceal mistakes, producing a gap between what a document states and what actually occurred in the organization. (Tagged PARTIAL because this specific finding was retrieved via a search-engine-generated summary rather than a verbatim-confirmed excerpt.)
5. PARTIAL: Representativeness requires that a selected document reflect typical/recurring content on its topic rather than idiosyncratic, one-off material.
6. DIRECT: The article recommends reflexive thematic analysis as a fitting analytic strategy for coding document data and provides a hypothetical worked example to illustrate the overall document-analysis process.
7. UNVERIFIED: A precise, numbered "steps of document analysis" list (e.g., a formal 1-2-3-4 procedure as might appear in a methods figure/table within the article) was not found or confirmed at snippet level; only the criteria (point 2) and the general advantages/limitations/reflexive-TA framing (points 1 and 6) could be verified.
