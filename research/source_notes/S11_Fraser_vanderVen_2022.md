## Research aim
DIRECT (recurring verbatim/near-verbatim across independent WebSearch renderings of the abstract): The fast fashion industry is subject to growing calls for transparency from civil society groups and consumers; despite this pressure, "uptake of transparency policies has been heterogeneous amongst large fast fashion companies." The article sets out to "explain variation in transparency practices through a comparison of the four largest fast fashion retailers: H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing." The research question is essentially: given that all four firms face similar external/civil-society pressure to disclose supply-chain information, why do their transparency practices differ so much — i.e., what conditions explain heterogeneous institutional responses to a common pressure.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: "Sustainability scandals are a necessary but insufficient condition for motivating firms to increase transparency in their supply chains. Scandals can be an important driver of increased transparency, but only when accompanied by support from senior management and alignment with domestic norms about appropriate corporate conduct." (recurring verbatim across independent WebSearch renderings, treated as DIRECT at snippet level).
2. DIRECT: "H&M is currently the most transparent of the four cases, despite ranking second in 2020 revenues." More specifically, "a combination of reputational risk due to scandals, domestic norms and institutions, and CEOs' values have led to higher levels of transparency in H&M."
3. DIRECT: "Where only one of these variables is present, it is insufficient to shift corporate transparency practices, as in the cases of Gap and Inditex" — i.e., Gap and Inditex each possess only a subset of the three conditions (scandal exposure, favorable domestic norms, committed leadership) and consequently show lower transparency uptake than H&M.
4. DIRECT: Cost of monitoring the supply chain is "a frequently cited obstacle to increased transparency," and "more profitable firms have been found to be more transparent, partly because they are more likely to have funds available to implement mechanisms for reporting and monitoring" — a resource/capacity-based finding alongside the institutional/legitimacy account.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 3.2 (case selection)** and **RQ1**: This is the single most directly usable source for justifying the H&M–Inditex comparative case selection design, since it is itself a four-firm comparative case study of the same industry (including both H&M and Inditex) explaining *why transparency uptake varies* despite a common external pressure environment — precedent for treating H&M and Inditex as a matched-but-contrasting pair (H&M = higher transparency/more conditions present; Inditex = fewer conditions present, per this source), supporting our rationale for selecting these two firms for deeper comparison.
2. DIRECT — maps to **RQ1 (institutional pressures and corporate responses)**: The three explanatory conditions identified (reputational-risk/scandal exposure, domestic institutional norms, senior-management/leadership commitment) are directly usable as an institutional-pressures-and-response account: scandal exposure functions similarly to coercive/reputational pressure, domestic norms function similarly to normative pressure, and the finding that "only one … is insufficient" supports RQ1's core expectation that institutional pressures alone do not determine outcome — internal organizational agency (leadership) mediates whether pressure produces substantive (vs. symbolic/mixed) change.
3. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 2.3 (institutional pressures)** and **section 2.6**: The finding that scandals are "necessary but insufficient" is directly usable to argue, in the literature review, that institutional-pressure exposure alone is an incomplete explanation for corporate sustainability communication outcomes — motivating this thesis's attention to firm-level mediating factors (leadership, decoupling risk) rather than treating pressure as deterministic.
4. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2 (fast-fashion model)** and **section 3.2**: The direct comparison of H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing (all major global fast-fashion players) is useful background for characterizing the competitive set/industry structure our thesis's two-case comparison sits within, and for noting that H&M's higher revenue-rank-adjusted transparency is explained by non-financial (institutional/leadership) rather than purely financial capacity factors — though the profitability/monitoring-cost finding is also relevant.

PARTIAL — maps to **RQ2 (competitive pressure and selective-disclosure patterns)**: The paper's focus on *transparency levels* (how much information is disclosed) is conceptually adjacent to, but not identical with, this thesis's *selective-disclosure* concept (which favorable metrics vs. unfavorable ones); it is useful as background evidence that transparency/disclosure practices vary systematically by firm-level institutional and leadership factors, but does not itself analyze selective/asymmetric disclosure content — a gap our RQ2 can position itself against.
