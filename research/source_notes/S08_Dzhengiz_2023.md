## Research aim
DIRECT (snippet-level, from abstract text rendered consistently across multiple independent search results — CoLab, WebSearch synthesis of the publisher/aggregator pages): "Due to pressing sustainability challenges, the fashion industry is undergoing tremendous change... this context has yet to receive the attention of transition scholars. Our article explores fashion transitions and develops a conceptual framework demonstrating this transition's multi-level and multi-dimensional interactions." The article states it draws "on three literature areas: multi-level perspective (MLP) of sustainable transitions, institutional logics and framing contests," and it "introduce[s] a conceptual framework and illustrative examples from the industry demonstrating the tensions between positive and negative environmental and social sustainability developments at the niche, regime and landscape levels." This is a theory/framework-building piece, not a primary empirical study with original interview/survey data.

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (abstract-derived): Three literature strands are explicitly combined: (1) multi-level perspective (MLP) of sustainable transitions (niche/regime/landscape levels), (2) institutional logics, and (3) framing contests.
- PARTIAL (recurring across independent WebSearch snippets, not a single confirmed verbatim block): The framework distinguishes "positive impact niches" that use pro-sustainability framings (described with terms including consistency, degrowth-sufficiency, ethical, slow, circular and sharing) aiming to shift the dominant market logic at the landscape level, versus "negative impact niches" that use framings such as "ultra-fastness" to reinforce/legitimise the existing market logic rather than challenge it.
- PARTIAL: Most "regime" players (i.e., incumbent fast-fashion firms) are described as engaging with sustainability primarily through a "business case" / instrumental lens, operating under exposure to both a market logic and a sustainability logic simultaneously (institutional logics multiplicity).
- PARTIAL: Regime-niche relationships are characterized as "coopetitive" (both cooperative and competitive framing observed), and coalitions between regime players are described as differentiable into "protagonist" vs. "antagonist" coalitions depending on their sustainability orientation. This vocabulary is specific enough (coopetitive, protagonist/antagonist coalitions) that it plausibly reflects the article's own terminology, but it was retrieved only via WebSearch synthesis, not a directly viewed verbatim passage — treat as PARTIAL pending full-text confirmation.
- PARTIAL: The article links "eco-efficiency framing" used by negative-impact (ultra-fast) niches/regime actors to legitimise practices, and explicitly connects this to a risk of greenwashing "if and when the corporate action and language are decoupled" — i.e., the paper appears to invoke decoupling explicitly as a mechanism linking framing/communication to greenwashing risk. This directly supports the thesis's decoupling/selective-disclosure supporting concepts, but was found via WebSearch synthesis rather than a confirmed verbatim sentence — PARTIAL.
- UNVERIFIED: Whether Institutional Theory (in the DiMaggio & Powell coercive/mimetic/normative sense used as this thesis's primary lens) is explicitly operationalized in this article, versus the article using the related but distinct "institutional logics" (Friedland & Alford / Thornton et al. tradition) framework. The abstract explicitly says "institutional logics," not "institutional pressures" in the coercive/mimetic/normative sense — this is an important distinction for RQ1 alignment and needs full-text confirmation before being cited as direct evidence for the coercive/mimetic/normative typology.

## Method and data of the article
DIRECT (abstract-derived, corroborated by an independent WebSearch summary): The paper is a conceptual paper that develops a theoretical framework and illustrates it with archival/secondary examples from the fashion industry, including named ultra-fast-fashion firms (Shein and Boohoo mentioned in search summaries as illustrative cases). It is not a primary empirical study built on original interviews, surveys, or a defined coded document sample; it functions as a conceptual/synthesis contribution combining MLP, institutional logics, and framing-contest literatures, then applying the resulting framework to illustrative industry examples (e.g., a Boohoo "sustainability collaboration" example discussed in relation to greenwashing accusations). PARTIAL: the exact nature and boundaries of the "archival data" (systematic corpus vs. illustrative anecdote selection) could not be confirmed at snippet level.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Clothing production "almost doubled over the last 15 years" alongside a tremendous increase in speed-to-market over the last two decades under the dominance of fast fashion — a quoted sentence recurring verbatim, in quotation marks, across independent WebSearch renderings: "With the dominance of fast fashion, speed-to-market has increased tremendously over the last two decades (Bhardwaj & Fairhurst, 2010), and clothing production almost doubled over the last 15 years." Directly relevant to the "why does improving one indicator not necessarily reduce total impact" focus question — this is the volume/growth trend the paper situates its sustainability-tension argument against.
2. PARTIAL: The framework identifies a structural tension whereby "positive impact niches" (sufficiency/slow/circular/sharing-oriented actors) push for reduced consumption and pro-sustainability framings, while "negative impact niches" (ultra-fast fashion) use "ultra-fastness" itself as a framing that reinforces, rather than disrupts, the dominant growth-oriented market logic — i.e., the ultra-fast segment is characterized as a business model built on maximizing speed and volume, which is presented as being in direct tension with absolute/total environmental impact reduction regardless of any per-unit eco-efficiency gains.
3. PARTIAL: Negative-impact niches/regime actors are described as using "eco-efficiency framings to legitimise their practices and operations," which the paper links to greenwashing risk "if and when the corporate action and language are decoupled" — supporting the "improving one indicator does not reduce total impact" logic: eco-efficiency communication can mask continued or growing absolute volume/impact.
4. PARTIAL: A Boohoo example is used to illustrate the gap between announced sustainability collaborations/marketing positioning and underlying practices, with the paper noting accusations of greenwashing in that context — offered as an illustrative case of the framing/practice decoupling described above.
5. PARTIAL: Regime-niche relationships are "coopetitive," and coalitions among regime players can be "protagonist" or "antagonist" toward sustainability transition, suggesting the industry-level response to sustainability pressure is heterogeneous and contested rather than uniform.
6. UNVERIFIED: Any specific quantitative findings (e.g., precise emissions/output figures beyond the "doubled over 15 years" claim), explicit page-located definitions of "ultra-fast fashion" versus "fast fashion" as distinct business-model categories, and the full list/typology of framings discussed — full text needed.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 2.2 (Fast-fashion business model)**: The "clothing production almost doubled over the last 15 years" finding, combined with "speed-to-market has increased tremendously," is directly usable evidence that the fast/ultra-fast fashion business model is defined by volume growth and cycle-time compression, not merely by low price — this is core B2 material answering "what makes fast/ultra-fast fashion a distinct business model."
2. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2** and **RQ1 (Methodology/Discussion framing)**: The distinction between fast fashion (regime incumbents operating a "business case"/instrumental sustainability logic) and ultra-fast fashion (negative-impact niche actors like Shein/Boohoo using "ultra-fastness" as a framing that reinforces growth-oriented market logic) is a candidate conceptual basis for treating fast fashion and ultra-fast fashion as related but distinct points on a speed/volume spectrum — useful for framing the B2 literature block, though the precise defining criteria (e.g., number of new SKUs/day, design-to-shelf time) are not confirmed at snippet level and would need a more empirical B2 source to quantify.
3. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2** and directly answers the "why does improving one indicator not necessarily reduce total impact" focus question and supports RQ2 (selective-disclosure patterns): The paper's argument that "eco-efficiency framing" can legitimise ultra-fast practices while total/absolute impact continues to rise (i.e., relative/per-unit improvements coexisting with, or masking, absolute volume growth) is directly usable to explain the rebound-effect logic: a business model whose core competitive lever is speed and volume growth can improve an efficiency indicator (e.g., emissions per garment, % recycled fiber) while total impact still rises because unit volume growth outpaces the per-unit improvement. This maps to RQ2's interest in selective-disclosure (highlighting favorable relative metrics while omitting absolute/total trends).
4. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2 / RQ1 institutional-pressures framing**, with a caveat: The paper's "institutional logics" and "framing contests" apparatus is related to, but not identical with, this thesis's primary DiMaggio & Powell institutional-pressures (coercive/mimetic/normative) lens. It should be cited as a business-model/industry-dynamics source (B2), not conflated with the thesis's primary institutional-theory citations, unless full-text reading confirms explicit coercive/mimetic/normative language.
5. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.2 and Discussion**: The Boohoo illustrative example (marketing a sustainability collaboration while facing greenwashing accusations) is a secondary, useful real-world illustration of decoupling in the ultra-fast-fashion segment specifically — a useful contrast case to the thesis's own H&M/Inditex comparison, since Boohoo/Shein represent a more extreme "ultra-fast" point on the spectrum than either case-study firm.

## Use in thesis
- **Section 2.2 (Fast-fashion business model)**: Use as the CORE-T anchor for defining what distinguishes fast/ultra-fast fashion as a business model — centered on speed-to-market compression and volume growth (production "almost doubled over the last 15 years"), and for introducing the niche/regime/landscape (MLP) framing to characterize ultra-fast entrants (Shein, Boohoo) as a distinct, more extreme segment relative to fast-fashion incumbents (relevant to positioning H&M and Inditex as regime incumbents in the thesis's own case selection).
- **Section 2.2 / Discussion**: Use to support the rebound-effect / "relative vs. absolute impact" argument — that eco-efficiency improvements at the per-unit level do not necessarily reduce total environmental impact when volume growth is a core competitive strategy, directly answering this card's second focus question.
- **RQ2 framing (Methodology or Discussion)**: Use the eco-efficiency-framing/decoupling/greenwashing-risk linkage as background support for why selective disclosure of favorable relative metrics (e.g., % sustainable materials, per-garment emissions) without corresponding absolute/volume data is a plausible corporate communication strategy under competitive pressure.
