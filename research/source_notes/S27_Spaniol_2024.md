## Research aim
DIRECT (recurring near-identically across independent WebSearch-rendered summaries of the abstract/landing page): the article addresses the problem that "the lack of a shared, operant definition for greenwashing has led to fragmented scholarly research, unclear guidelines for practice, inconsistent enforcement, and reactive policy frameworks," which the authors argue results in "ineffective efforts to combat its growth." The stated aim is to use concept analysis to establish a **composite definition of greenwashing**, built by identifying constitutive attributes sourced across **~79 scholarly definitions** of the term. PARTIAL: the article is also described as providing "a diagnostic tool for separating similar but often conflated concepts from greenwashing," intended "to organize scholarly research, provide guidelines for practitioners, and support regulators' case analysis" — this framing recurred across two independent search syntheses in close-to-identical language, suggesting it tracks actual abstract/conclusion wording, though exact sentence-level phrasing is not confirmed verbatim from a directly fetched page.

## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT: The article's core method/output is a **concept analysis** (a definitional/lexical-synthesis method, common in nursing and social-science literature for clarifying ambiguous concepts) applied to greenwashing, drawing on a corpus of ~79 existing scholarly definitions rather than a single new empirical dataset.
- DIRECT: The resulting composite definition is built from **six constitutive attributes**, recurring consistently across multiple independent search syntheses: greenwashing requires (1) an **environmental claim** (i.e., "environmental purview"), (2) made by a **private-sector organization/firm**, (3) about a **product or service** marketed **to consumers/stakeholders**, (4) that **cannot be substantiated** (unsubstantiated), (5) made with **deceptive intent**, and (6) undertaken to secure a **competitive advantage** for the firm (and, per one synthesis, that ultimately "misleads stakeholders about the organization's environmental performance").

## Main findings
1. DIRECT (recurring across multiple independent WebSearch syntheses in near-identical language): The analysis identifies **six attributes necessary for identifying greenwashing**: an environmental claim, made by a private-sector organization/firm, about a product or service marketed to consumers, that is unsubstantiated, made with deceptive intent, and made to establish competitive advantage.
2. DIRECT: The absence of a shared definition is described as having led to "fragmented scholarly research, unclear guidelines for practice, inconsistent enforcement, and reactive policy frameworks."
3. DIRECT: The European Commission has amended the **Unfair Commercial Practices Directive (UCPD)** and the **Consumer Rights Directive** in relation to greenwashing/sustainability claims. One search synthesis reproduced language attributed to the paper stating this amendment aims "to avoid ultimately diluting genuine sustainability claims and causing overall harm to consumer trust within the field of sustainability" (see Useful quotes below for provenance caveat).
4. PARTIAL: In the absence of consistent regulatory enforcement, the paper is reported (via search synthesis) to note that NGOs, popular media, and opinion influencers play an important watchdog role identifying greenwashing — this detail recurred in one synthesis but was not independently cross-confirmed in a second, so it is tagged PARTIAL rather than DIRECT.
5. PARTIAL: The paper positions its composite definition/diagnostic tool as useful "to organize scholarly research, provide guidelines for practitioners, and support regulators' case analysis" — recurring phrasing across syntheses, interpreted as close to actual text but not confirmed verbatim from a directly fetched page.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT — maps to **thesis section 2.1 (definitional grounding) and Methodology**: The composite, attribute-based definition (environmental claim + firm-level actor + product/service marketed to consumers + unsubstantiated + deceptive intent + competitive-advantage motive) is directly usable as the thesis's operational definition of greenwashing for the directed qualitative content analysis, since it gives concrete, checkable criteria (substantiation, intent-inference via context, competitive framing) that can guide a coding scheme applied to H&M Group's and Inditex's disclosures.
2. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.3 (institutional pressures) and RQ1**: The reported link between the UCPD/Consumer Rights Directive amendments and the goal of preventing unsubstantiated/misleading sustainability claims can be used as illustrative evidence of an EU-level **coercive regulatory pressure** aimed specifically at the "unsubstantiated claim" attribute of greenwashing (attribute 4 above). However, whether the source itself explicitly frames this regulatory amendment in institutional-theory terms (i.e., as "coercive pressure" in DiMaggio & Powell's sense) is UNVERIFIED — this connection is presently the thesis's own theoretical application to the source's regulatory-policy finding, not a confirmed claim made by the authors themselves.
3. PARTIAL — maps to **thesis section 2.1/2.3 and RQ2 (competitive pressure)**: Attribute 6 (competitive-advantage motive) is directly relevant to RQ2's focus on competitive pressure and selective disclosure, since it establishes, at the definitional level, that mainstream scholarly consensus treats seeking competitive advantage as a constitutive (not merely incidental) element of greenwashing — supporting the thesis's framing that comparative rival dynamics (H&M vs. Inditex) are conceptually, not just empirically, tied to greenwashing behavior.
