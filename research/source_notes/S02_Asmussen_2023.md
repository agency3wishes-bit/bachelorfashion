## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (snippet-level): Coalitional game theory is the core analytical framework. The authors "develop a model where an MNE collaborates, monitors, and negotiates prices with a supplier whose CSR breaches may be revealed by the MNE, external agents, or remain hidden."
- DIRECT (snippet-level): Central economic concept is the "hold-up problem" — MNEs may face a hold-up problem when suppliers' irresponsible actions become public and suppliers gain bargaining power to engage in "opportunistic renegotiation."
- DIRECT (snippet-level): "Social brand" concept — large, highly visible MNEs are said to be incentivized to protect their "social brands" from reputational damage, since these brands can be undermined by social/environmental disasters at supplier sites.
- PARTIAL: This is a bargaining-power / reputation-risk framework applied to MNE-supplier relations, distinct from (but adjacent to and citable alongside) institutional theory and legitimacy theory used as this thesis's primary lenses. It is not itself framed in the search evidence as institutional-theory or legitimacy-theory research; it is closer to organizational economics / game theory applied to IB and CSR.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: MNEs may face a "hold-up problem" when irresponsible supplier actions are made public — suppliers then hold bargaining power to engage in opportunistic renegotiation with the MNE.
2. DIRECT: Greater monitoring by MNEs, "if not combined with specific strategies, can have negative consequences by weakening the MNE's bargaining position and, in some cases, even prompting more irresponsible actions by the suppliers" — i.e., a monitoring paradox where visibility/detection capacity alone can backfire.
3. DIRECT: Large, highly visible MNEs are incentivized to protect their "social brands" from reputational damage because these brands can be undermined by social and environmental disasters occurring at supplier sites; this incentive drives lead firms to "strive to ensure CSR compliance throughout the GVC."
4. PARTIAL: The model implies that responsibility for CSR outcomes in GVCs is not simply assignable to the MNE alone but emerges from the bargaining dynamic between MNE and supplier (who reveals breaches, who has leverage, who bears reputational vs. financial cost) — this is an inference from the combination of findings 1-3 rather than a directly quoted single-sentence finding.

## What it says for OUR topic
1. PARTIAL — maps to RQ1 / thesis section 2.6: The "social brand" reputational-protection mechanism found here supports a *legitimacy-theory-compatible* reading of why highly visible fast-fashion MNEs (H&M Group, Inditex) would respond to institutional/stakeholder pressure with visible CSR/sustainability commitments — protecting the brand from reputational damage caused by supplier-level failures is a plausible micro-mechanism underlying symbolic or substantive corporate responses to normative and coercive pressures identified in institutional theory. Usable as a bargaining-theory complement to legitimacy theory when discussing *why* brand-level disclosure and supplier-code programs exist.
2. PARTIAL — maps to RQ2 / thesis section 2.6 (selective disclosure): The finding that supplier CSR breaches "may be revealed by the MNE, external agents, or remain hidden" is directly relevant to the thesis's decoupling/selective-disclosure argument: it models information asymmetry and *deliberate or structural non-disclosure* of supplier-level CSR breaches as an equilibrium outcome of bargaining, not just an oversight. This gives a formal-economics grounding for why GVC-embedded MNEs like H&M/Inditex might exhibit selective disclosure (disclosing favorable practices, remaining silent on supplier-level breaches) as a rational response to bargaining incentives rather than only as an institutional/legitimacy phenomenon.
3. PARTIAL — maps to RQ1/RQ2 and Methodology: The "monitoring paradox" (finding 2) is relevant to any argument in the thesis that increased supplier auditing/monitoring commitments (a common symbolic-response indicator in institutional-theory coding) do not straightforwardly translate into improved substantive outcomes — it offers a bargaining-theoretic explanation (weakened MNE leverage) that is complementary to a decoupling explanation (symbolic compliance without operational change), useful for triangulating why monitoring/audit disclosures in H&M/Inditex sustainability reports may be a "mixed" or "symbolic" response category rather than "substantive."
4. UNVERIFIED at present (needs full text): Whether the article discusses fast fashion, textile/apparel GVCs specifically, or any brand comparable to H&M/Inditex. No evidence found tying this paper to the apparel sector specifically — it should be cited as a general IB/GVC-CSR theory source (per its B5 classification), not as sector-specific evidence.
5. Distribution-of-responsibility question (explicit focus question for this card): PARTIAL. The paper does not appear (per abstract-level evidence) to argue for a fixed normative allocation of CSR responsibility between MNE and supplier; instead it models responsibility/outcomes as *emergent from bargaining power*, which is analytically useful for the thesis's B5 discussion of how "distributed responsibility" claims in H&M/Inditex CSR reporting can be read skeptically — i.e., stated commitments to shared/supplier responsibility may reflect real bargaining-power asymmetries (MNE retains leverage via reputational stakes; supplier retains leverage via hold-up potential) rather than a neutral governance choice.
6. Information asymmetry question (explicit focus question for this card): DIRECT at the concept level — the model explicitly incorporates information asymmetry as a structural feature of GVCs (breaches "may... remain hidden"), directly supporting the thesis claim that fragmented GVCs structurally enable selective disclosure by brand-owners who may lack, or choose not to surface, full visibility into supplier-level (sub)practices.

## Use in thesis
- Section 2.6 (literature synthesis, B5 IB & global value chains subsection): Cite as the core theoretical bargaining-perspective account of how CSR responsibility and information about CSR breaches are distributed/asymmetric between MNE brand-owners and suppliers in GVCs — sets up the structural/economic rationale (information asymmetry, bargaining power, hold-up risk) that complements the thesis's institutional-theory and legitimacy-theory explanations for symbolic/substantive corporate responses.
- RQ1 discussion: Use to explain a plausible economic mechanism (reputational/"social brand" protection, hold-up risk) underlying why MNEs respond to coercive/normative pressure with particular response types, and why monitoring commitments alone (a common "symbolic" indicator) may not guarantee substantive change (monitoring paradox).
- RQ2 discussion: Use as theoretical support for treating selective disclosure not purely as an institutional-legitimacy artifact but also as a structurally rational outcome of GVC information asymmetry and bargaining incentives — strengthens the thesis's argument that fragmented, multi-tier supply chains create structural opportunities for selective disclosure independent of, or in addition to, institutional pressures.
- Methodology chapter: Can be cited briefly when justifying why document analysis of MNE-level sustainability reporting alone cannot verify supplier-level practice (methodological limitation flagged in the thesis), since this paper models exactly that visibility gap.
