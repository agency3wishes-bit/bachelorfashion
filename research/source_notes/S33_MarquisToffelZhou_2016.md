## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (recurring in near-identical wording across at least four independent search syntheses): **selective disclosure** is defined as a firm behavior whereby, facing pressure to report on its environmental impacts, it chooses to disclose only relatively benign or favorable impacts while withholding disclosure of its more damaging impacts — thereby creating "an impression of transparency" without actually revealing its true (worse) environmental performance. This is explicitly a disclosure-level, reporting-behavior construct, distinct from a general definition of greenwashing as claim-performance mismatch (cf. S31 Delmas & Burbano, S07 de Freitas Netto et al.).
- DIRECT: the paper's theoretical framing centers on organizational and institutional/country-level factors that intensify **scrutiny** (of the focal firm) and diffuse **global norms** (to the firm's home country), arguing that both scrutiny and norm-diffusion function as *limiting* conditions on selective disclosure — i.e., the paper's theoretical contribution is specifically about what constrains or reduces selective-disclosure behavior, not only about what causes it.
- PARTIAL (recurring across search summaries but without independently confirmed page-level detail in this round): the "company-level" factors are described in general terms as organizational characteristics that increase a firm's visibility/scrutiny (e.g., firm size, media/public profile), while "country-level" factors are described as national exposure to global environmental norms and civil-society mobilization/scrutiny (e.g., NGO presence, press freedom, or ratification of international environmental treaties) — the precise named variables and their operational measurement were not confirmed at snippet level beyond this general characterization in this round.

## Method and data of the article
DIRECT (converging across at least three independent search queries, all reporting the identical sample description): the study is a quantitative panel-data analysis testing its hypotheses on **a novel panel data set of 4,750 public companies across many industries, headquartered in 45 countries, covering the period 2004–2007**. DIRECT: the dependent construct operationalizes selective disclosure of environmental impacts (the specific measurement instrument's exact variable definitions — e.g., which environmental-impact categories or disclosure indices were used — were not confirmed at full detail in this round beyond the general "selectively disclose relatively benign impacts" framing). ## Main findings
- DIRECT (converging across at least four independent search queries in near-identical wording): firms that are **more environmentally damaging** — particularly those headquartered in **countries where they are more exposed to scrutiny and global (environmental) norms** — are **less likely to engage in selective disclosure**. In other words, scrutiny and norm-exposure function empirically as constraints that reduce selective disclosure, rather than conditions that increase it.
- DIRECT: this finding runs counter to a naive expectation that more environmentally damaging firms would have the greatest incentive to selectively disclose (to hide poor performance); instead, the paper's central empirical result is that exactly these firms are *less* likely to do so once scrutiny and norm-diffusion are sufficiently intense, implying that external scrutiny and norm pressure operate as an effective (if partial) check on this specific greenwashing behavior at a global, cross-country scale.
- PARTIAL: the relative magnitude of the scrutiny effect versus the norm-diffusion effect, and whether the two operate independently or interact, was not confirmed at snippet level in this round; both are reported together in aggregated summaries without disaggregated effect sizes.

## What it says for OUR topic
- CLAIM 1 (DIRECT): This paper provides the corpus's only large-sample (4,750 firms, 45 countries), multi-year, quantitative precedent for the **selective disclosure** construct specifically — as distinct from Nemes et al. (2022), which names selective disclosure as a keyword/indicator within a broader assessment framework without a comparable large-N empirical test, and Bernini & La Rosa (2024), which discusses selective disclosure conceptually within a communication-level greenwashing typology. → Maps to thesis §2.4/§2.5, where it should sit alongside Nemes et al. and Bernini & La Rosa as a complementary, quantitative large-N precedent for the same construct these qualitative/conceptual sources define.
- CLAIM 2 (DIRECT, central to RQ2): the paper's key finding — that scrutiny and global-norm exposure *limit* (reduce) selective disclosure, rather than merely accompanying or enabling it — offers a valuable large-N precedent that complements this thesis's own qualitative, two-firm approach. Where this thesis's directed content analysis traces selective-disclosure patterns within two firms' reports over a defined recent period, Marquis, Toffel & Zhou's finding establishes, at global scale, that the *general* mechanism this thesis investigates (selective disclosure as a competitive/reputational response) is itself moderated by scrutiny and normative pressure — directly relevant to RQ2's framing of selective disclosure "in light of competitive pressure," since scrutiny and competitive pressure are related but distinguishable external forces.
- CLAIM 3 (DIRECT, maps to Discussion §5.2): the thesis's own qualitative finding that Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area discloses only a "people-reached" count across all four coded years, with no wage-level or wage-gap figure, while H&M discloses wage tables, an independent gender-pay-gap study, and concrete outcome figures (Section 4.4), can be read as a firm-level, in-depth instance of the general pattern Marquis, Toffel & Zhou document at scale: withholding of a specific, competitively/reputationally sensitive category of environmental (or, by extension in this thesis's case, social/labor) information persists except where scrutiny and norm pressure are sufficiently intense. This is an analogy across construct (their study is specifically environmental-impact disclosure; the thesis's finding concerns labor/wage disclosure) that should be flagged explicitly as an extension, not a direct replication, of their construct.
- CLAIM 4 (PARTIAL, maps to §2.3 and RQ1 only as background): the paper's "global norms" mechanism is conceptually adjacent to, but not identical with, DiMaggio & Powell's (1983) *normative* isomorphism (professionalization-driven) — Marquis, Toffel & Zhou's "norms" operate at the country level (diffusion of global environmental expectations into a firm's home country) rather than through professional associations or formal education specifically, so this connection should be drawn cautiously as a parallel rather than an equivalence.

## Limitations of the source
- A quantitative, cross-country, panel-data study of general corporate environmental-impact disclosure (2004–2007), not a study of the fast-fashion sector, H&M Group, or Inditex specifically, and predating the thesis's 2022–2025 study window and the EU's CSRD/CSDDD/Taxonomy regulatory developments by roughly fifteen to twenty years; any application to the fast-fashion, EU-regulatory context of this thesis is an extension this thesis must justify itself, not a claim the source makes.
- Studies environmental-impact disclosure broadly, not labor/wage disclosure specifically; the analogy drawn in Claim 3 above (Discussion §5.2) between the source's construct and the thesis's own wage-disclosure finding is a cross-domain extension that should be explicitly flagged as such wherever cited, not presented as if the source itself studied wage disclosure.
- As a large-sample, variance-based quantitative study, it cannot directly inform qualitative document-analysis coding procedures; its relevance to this thesis is as complementary macro-level empirical precedent, not as a methodological template for content-analysis coding.
