## Main findings
- DIRECT — "Although SDGs are frequently mentioned in the corporate reports, detailed disclosures relating to specific targets and specific initiatives are lacking." (Recurring wording across independent WebSearch queries.)
- DIRECT — "A misalignment was found between the SDG focus in corporate reports and in media reports, with media reports tending to provide a more balanced perspective, addressing both positive and negative impacts of the industry's practices on the SDGs." (Recurring wording across independent WebSearch queries.)
- DIRECT — "Coverage of specific sustainability transgressions by media reports makes them a relevant source of information that is not discussed in corporate reports." (Recurring wording across independent WebSearch queries.)

## What it says for OUR topic
1. DIRECT/PARTIAL — Provides directly on-point, near-contemporaneous empirical evidence (2020–2022, immediately preceding our 2022–2025 window) of a systematic pattern in the fast-fashion industry whereby corporate sustainability disclosures name SDGs/commitments frequently but omit target-level, verifiable specifics — a textbook instance of symbolic (rather than substantive) disclosure response as this thesis's RQ1 typology defines it. Maps to: 2.2 (fast-fashion model/sustainability challenges), 2.5 (selective disclosure), RQ1.
2. DIRECT/PARTIAL — The finding that independent media reports surface negative/transgression-related information "not discussed in corporate reports" is direct comparative evidence of selective disclosure (omission of unfavorable information from self-reported channels) — precisely the phenomenon RQ2 investigates for H&M and Inditex, even though this source's own sample composition vis-à-vis those two specific firms is unconfirmed. Maps to: 2.5 (competitive pressure/selective disclosure), RQ2.
3. DIRECT/PARTIAL — The corporate-vs.-media triangulation design itself (comparing self-reported disclosure against an independent, third-party information source to test corroboration/contradiction) is a directly transferable methodological precedent for this thesis's document analysis method, which likewise needs to test whether case-firm disclosures are substantiated or contradicted by independent evidence. Maps to: Methodology (3.4).
4. PARTIAL — The industry-level framing of greenwashing/"SDG washing" as an "ever-present phenomenon" in fast fashion, "particularly exposed by media reports," supports this thesis's B2 fast-fashion-model literature block by evidencing that the sustainability-communication gap is a structural, industry-wide condition rather than an idiosyncrasy of any single firm — useful context for situating H&M and Inditex within a broader industry pattern. Maps to: 2.2, RQ1.
5. UNVERIFIED — Whether H&M and/or Inditex are among the "eight largest global fast fashion companies" sampled is not confirmed; if the full text later confirms their inclusion, the source would become a direct, company-specific comparator rather than only an industry-level analogy, and this card should be revised accordingly upon full-text access.
