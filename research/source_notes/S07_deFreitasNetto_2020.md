## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT: **Greenwashing**, framed via the widely cited Delmas & Burbano (2011)-style definition that recurs across search syntheses of this article: the intersection/co-occurrence of *poor environmental performance* and *positive (misleading) environmental communication* by a firm.
- DIRECT: A fourfold **typology/classification of greenwashing forms**, crossing two dimensions — (1) level of analysis: **firm-level** vs. **product-level**, and (2) communicative form: **claim** vs. **executional** — yielding four categories: firm-level executional, firm-level claim, product-level executional, product-level claim. This classification recurred identically and consistently across nearly every independent search result and synthesis, indicating high confidence it is a core, verbatim-adjacent finding of the article.
- PARTIAL: **Claim greenwashing** — described in search synthesis as the use of textual arguments (wording in firm reporting, product description, or advertising copy) that explicitly or implicitly assert or imply environmental/ecological benefits of a product, brand, or firm, in a misleading way.
- PARTIAL: **Executional greenwashing** — described in search synthesis as the use of nature-evoking, non-textual elements (e.g., imagery, colors — green palettes, natural imagery — visual cues) in advertising or packaging to suggest environmental friendliness without substantiated basis.
- PARTIAL/UNVERIFIED: **Selective disclosure** — multiple independent secondary sources describe de Freitas Netto et al. (2020) as characterizing/reviewing greenwashing as (or as encompassing) a form of selective disclosure: firms are "rather silent on negative information" regarding their environmental or CSR performance while "highlighting positive information" about it — i.e., asymmetric emphasis rather than outright fabrication. This framing recurred across secondary-citation search results, but could not be confirmed as the article's own verbatim terminology (vs. a citation the article makes to Delmas & Burbano or Lyon & Maxwell, whose typologies this review likely synthesizes) without full-text access. Tag as PARTIAL for the underlying concept, UNVERIFIED for whether "selective disclosure" is the article's own term versus a term it reports from reviewed sources.
- PARTIAL/UNVERIFIED: Search synthesis also links the article (via secondary citations discussing it) to **decoupling** — the gap/disconnection between a firm's espoused values/communication and its actual practices — and to greenwashing as "a questionable way to get and maintain **legitimacy**." These connections appear in how *other* papers characterize/cite de Freitas Netto et al. (2020), so they are evidence that the article sits within (and is used by later literature to link) the institutional-theory/legitimacy-theory space this thesis relies on, but the exact framing/wording used inside the 2020 article itself is UNVERIFIED pending full text.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Greenwashing is identifiable as the co-occurrence of poor environmental performance and positive/misleading environmental communication by a firm (consistent with, and citing, the Delmas & Burbano-style definition).
2. DIRECT: The review proposes/confirms a **major fourfold classification** of greenwashing forms: firm-level executional, firm-level claim, product-level executional, and product-level claim greenwashing — distinguishing *where* the greenwashing occurs (about the whole firm vs. about a specific product) and *how* it is communicated (through explicit/textual claims vs. through suggestive, non-textual/executional cues such as imagery or color).
3. PARTIAL: Claim greenwashing operates through textual, explicit or implicit assertions of environmental benefit (in reports, advertising, or product descriptions); executional greenwashing operates through non-textual, suggestive/associative cues (e.g., nature imagery, green color schemes) that imply environmental friendliness without an explicit substantiated claim.
4. PARTIAL/UNVERIFIED: The review is reported (in secondary citations of it) as linking greenwashing to selective disclosure of information (omitting negative environmental/CSR information while emphasizing positive information), to decoupling (gap between communicated values and actual practice), and to legitimacy-seeking behavior — but the specific wording, structure, and emphasis given to each of these connections inside the original article are not confirmed at snippet level.
5. UNVERIFIED: Any additional typologies, sub-forms, or definitional debates the review may report (e.g., alternative typologies from other authors it might review and compare, such as Lyon & Maxwell's or TerraChoice's "sins of greenwashing"), specific example companies/industries discussed, and the review's stated implications/recommendations for practice or future research cannot be confirmed without full-text access.

## Limitations of the source
- This is a **general, cross-industry conceptual/systematic review**, not an empirical study of fast fashion, apparel, H&M, or Inditex — it supplies definitional/taxonomic scaffolding only, not case-specific evidence.
- As a 2020 publication reviewing "the past 10 years" of literature (i.e., roughly 2010–2020), it predates the thesis's 2022–2025 study window and cannot speak to recent regulatory developments (e.g., EU Green Claims Directive discussions, CSRD) or to H&M/Inditex's most recent disclosure practices.
- Full text was not accessible: direct WebFetch attempts on the Springer/enveurope publisher page, Semantic Scholar, ResearchGate, AGRIS, Scilit, OUCI, and the DRZE secondary-analysis page all returned HTTP 403. All findings above rest on WebSearch-engine-synthesized snippets and secondary citations/paraphrases of the article by other papers, not on a verified reading of the original PDF/HTML.

(2020) rather than from the article's own abstract/snippet text directly, there is a risk that these secondary characterizations blend the 2020 article's own framing with the framing of sources it reviews (e.g., Delmas & Burbano 2011; Lyon & Maxwell 2011) — this attribution ambiguity must be resolved with full-text access.
