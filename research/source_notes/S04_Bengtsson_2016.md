## Theory / key concepts
- DIRECT (snippet-level): The paper distinguishes **manifest content analysis** ("what has been said," a broad surface structure, concrete/visible and descriptive) from **latent content analysis** ("what was intended to be said," a deep structure requiring interpretation of underlying meaning). Both manifest and latent content require interpretation, differing in depth/level of abstraction rather than being a strict either/or.
- DIRECT (snippet-level): Bengtsson identifies four main stages of the qualitative content-analysis research process: **decontextualisation, recontextualisation, categorisation, and compilation**.
  - Decontextualisation: breaking the data (whole) into smaller parts — identifying meaning units and creating a code list.
  - Recontextualisation: content selection — deciding what belongs to "content" and what should be excluded as "dross," and comparing selections back against the original data.
  - Categorisation: identifying homogeneous groups of codes/meaning units (forming categories), with credibility strengthened via triangulation among investigators.
  - Compilation: drawing conclusions from the categorised material, with credibility strengthened via member checks and inquiry audits.
- PARTIAL: The core conceptual vocabulary of the method — unit of analysis, meaning unit, condensation, abstraction, content area, code, category, theme — is listed by multiple sources as covered in the article, consistent with it drawing on the Graneheim & Lundman qualitative-content-analysis tradition, though the exact definitional wording for each term (beyond manifest/latent and the four stages above) could not be independently confirmed at snippet level.
- PARTIAL: Trustworthiness criteria (credibility, dependability, transferability) are explicitly framed as something to be built into every stage of the process, not assessed only after analysis.

## Main findings
1. DIRECT: Content analysis can be applied with different emphases along a manifest-latent continuum: manifest analysis stays close to the visible/surface content of the text, latent analysis interprets underlying meaning; both still involve interpretation, differing in depth.
2. DIRECT: The research process consists of four analytic stages — decontextualisation, recontextualisation, categorisation, compilation — each of which is also an opportunity to build in trustworthiness-enhancing procedures (e.g., triangulation at categorisation, member checks and inquiry audits at compilation).
3. DIRECT: Decontextualisation specifically involves identifying "meaning units" in the text and generating an initial code list from them — i.e., coding operates on meaning units extracted from decontextualised data, not on the raw whole text.
4. PARTIAL: Categorisation is described as isolating and grouping key phrases/codes based on similarities and differences into homogeneous categories — this is attributed by secondary sources to the Bengtsson/Graneheim tradition and is consistent with, but not itself a verbatim-confirmed sentence from, the original article.
