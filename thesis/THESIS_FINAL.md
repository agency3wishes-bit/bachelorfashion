# Abstract

This bachelor's thesis examines the drivers of greenwashing in the fast-fashion industry — specifically how competitive and institutional pressures shape environmental communication within international business — through a comparative case study of H&M Group and Inditex across their 2022–2025 reporting years, a period spanning the European Union's shift from voluntary to mandatory sustainability disclosure. Using Institutional Theory as its primary lens, it asks how coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures produce substantive, symbolic, or mixed corporate conformity, and how selective disclosure functions as a legitimacy-seeking mechanism under market competition and information asymmetry. A directed qualitative content analysis of both firms' company-published annual and sustainability reports produced 237 page-cited coded units across ten disclosure themes. The analysis finds institutional pressure was absorbed through two distinct sequences — rhetorical anticipation preceding structural change at one firm, structural change with comparatively little preceding rhetoric at the other — and that substantive and symbolic conformity resolve more clearly by disclosure theme than by firm. Selective-disclosure patterns appear in both firms' reporting, alongside one directional asymmetry in the withholding of supply-chain wage data. The thesis contributes a theme-level refinement to institutional-conformity classification and a case-based account of selective disclosure under an evolving EU regulatory regime.

# 1 Introduction

### 1.1 Background of the Study

Fast fashion is a leading test case for the credibility of corporate sustainability communication. The model rests on compressed design-to-shelf cycles, high-volume output, and low unit prices, sustained by geographically dispersed supply chains. Bick et al. (2018) trace how inexpensive, rapidly produced clothing has driven a rise in garment consumption, while the environmental and social costs of production — water use, chemical pollution, hazardous working conditions — remain concentrated in low- and middle-income manufacturing countries far from consumer markets. The scale keeps growing: Dzhengiz et al. (2023) document intensifying speed-to-market, with global clothing production almost doubling in fifteen years and an ultra-fast segment accelerating the cycle. The environmental burden is therefore not incidental but a product of the same volume-and-turnover logic that constitutes the model's competitive advantage — a structural tension any sustainability claim by such a firm must confront.

Against this backdrop, the industry's sustainability communication has expanded, and with it the concern that it may outrun practice. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) describe a fashion industry under sustained public scrutiny, where promoting environmental credentials has become commercially important while the gap between promoted image and production reality remains hard for outsiders to assess — the condition under which greenwashing concerns arise. This scrutiny has not produced uniform behavior: Fraser and van der Ven (2022), comparing the transparency practices of the largest fast-fashion retailers, find that disclosure uptake varies markedly even among firms facing similar external pressure, suggesting that how a firm communicates about sustainability is a corporate response worth studying in its own right, not a mechanical reflection of its environment. At product level, H&M's "Conscious" collection label and Inditex's group-wide "Join Life" label (applied across its brands, including Zara) both promise a higher share of preferred or lower-impact materials, yet the two took different paths over the study period — one retired under external pressure, the other still reporting a compliance figure that beats its own target.

The years 2022 to 2025 make this question well defined, spanning a rapid hardening of the European regulatory environment within which both firms are headquartered. The EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (European Commission, 2022, COM(2022) 141 final) announced a sector-specific policy agenda in March 2022; the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, Directive (EU) 2022/2464), in force from January 2023, began converting voluntary reporting into a mandated regime through the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), applying to large EU companies from fiscal year 2024; and a proposed Green Claims Directive (COM(2023) 166 final), tabled in March 2023, signaled forthcoming substantiation requirements for environmental marketing claims — though its status grew contested toward the end of the window, with the European Commission indicating in June 2025 that it might withdraw the proposal amid opposition to its verification requirements, an outcome unresolved at the time of writing and noted only as regulatory backdrop. Enforcement reached the sector directly too: in September 2022 the Dutch Authority for Consumers and Markets concluded a commitment decision concerning H&M's sustainability claims (ACM, 2022). The study period thus straddles the transition from a largely voluntary to an increasingly mandatory disclosure regime, letting the communication of H&M Group and Inditex be observed before, during, and after the change.

### 1.2 Research Problem and Questions

The core problem is that the line between legitimate and misleading communication is genuinely difficult to draw. Gatti et al. (2019) characterize voluntary, largely unaudited CSR disclosure as a "grey zone" between promotion and deception, treating this ambiguity as a structural feature of a voluntary regime rather than a failure of individual firms — which is why the voluntary-to-mandatory transition matters analytically. The greenwashing literature, meanwhile, is large and theoretically plural yet unresolved on how such communication should be detected: Montgomery et al. (2024) find selective disclosure and symbolic commitment among its most persistent concerns and see "no end in sight" to the phenomenon, while Bernini and La Rosa (2024) show that the field draws on institutional, legitimacy, and related lenses linking corporate communication to the external pressures firms face.

What this literature has not yet supplied — the gap developed in Section 2.7 — is a theory-driven comparison of institutional and competitive pressures operating jointly within the environmental communication of two closely matched fast-fashion multinationals over a defined recent period. Evidence on institutional pressures is largely survey-based, large-sample, or drawn from other sectors; evidence on competition and disclosure is similarly aggregate; and the small body of work analyzing H&M and Inditex together predates the 2022–2025 regulatory transition. No focused account exists of how the same institutional environment and competitive rivalry are differently absorbed in these two firms' disclosure during exactly the period in which European disclosure rules changed around them.

This thesis addresses that gap through two research questions:

**RQ1:** How do cross-border coercive, normative, and mimetic institutional pressures shape the environmental communication of H&M Group and Inditex (2022–2025), and how do their corporate responses compare in terms of substantive, symbolic, or mixed conformity?

**RQ2:** How is selective disclosure (e.g., omission, disproportional emphasis, inconsistent metrics) used as a legitimacy-seeking mechanism in the environmental communication of H&M Group and Inditex (2022–2025), and how do market competition and information asymmetry shape these patterns across the two firms?

Institutional Theory is the primary theoretical lens: the triad of coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures structures RQ1 and anchors the framework throughout. Legitimacy Theory, decoupling between policy and practice, and selective disclosure serve as supporting concepts, specifying how institutional pressure becomes substantive change, symbolic conformity, or selectively framed communication, without displacing it as the organizing framework.

### 1.3 Objectives, Scope, and Contribution

The objective is to analyze and compare the environmental communication of H&M Group and Inditex across their 2022–2025 reporting years, answering RQ1 and RQ2. The study is a qualitative comparative multiple-case study based on document analysis. Its material is the two firms' company-published annual and sustainability reporting for all four fiscal years — six H&M Group documents and the sustainability statements embedded in four Inditex Group Annual Reports — analyzed through directed qualitative content analysis using a deductive codebook derived from the theoretical framework. The codebook operationalizes both the institutional-pressure and response categories required by RQ1 and a set of greenwashing indicators (among them omission, disproportional emphasis, unclear boundary, and inconsistent metric) required by RQ2; every finding is anchored to a specific document, year, and page. Chapter 3 details this design, the case-selection logic, and the document corpus, including comparability caveats from the two firms' non-identical fiscal calendars.

The scope is deliberately bounded. The analysis concerns communication patterns and disclosure quality in corporate documents: what the two firms state, emphasize, omit, quantify, and leave unquantified. It does not assert legal wrongdoing, fraud, or deceptive intent, and no passage is treated as greenwashing merely because it sounds promotional — a coding is applied only where a specific, evidence-based indicator is met, and all claims remain within what the coded documentary material supports. External assessments by regulators or NGOs, where referenced, are attributed to their authors rather than adopted as findings. The study examines disclosure, not underlying environmental or social performance: a finding that a topic is thinly documented is a finding about reporting practice, not about outcomes.

Three results give the comparison its analytical payoff. First, the substantive-versus-symbolic distinction at the center of RQ1 discriminates at the level of disclosure themes rather than whole firms — each company is substantive in some domains and symbolic in others, in near mirror-image patterns — refining how institutional conformity is typically classified. Second, the two firms absorbed the same coercive regulatory pressure through different sequences, one rhetorical before structural and the other structural with little preceding rhetoric. Third, on RQ2, the analysis identifies page-cited selective-disclosure patterns in both firms' reporting, together with one directional asymmetry in the withholding of supply-chain wage data that is consistent with competitive-pressure explanations in the literature. These findings are developed with full documentary evidence in Chapter 4 and interpreted in Chapter 5.

The remainder proceeds in five chapters: Chapter 2 reviews the literature and specifies the research gap and conceptual framework; Chapter 3 presents the methodology; Chapter 4 reports the empirical results, without interpretation; Chapter 5 discusses them through the institutional-theoretical framework, answering RQ1 and RQ2; and Chapter 6 concludes.

# 2 Literature Review

### 2.1 Conceptualizing Greenwashing

Definitions of greenwashing converge on a core mismatch between a firm's environmental communication and its actual performance while diverging on scope and mechanism. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) characterize it as falsely promoting environmental efforts or spending disproportionately more on green promotion than on genuine practice, whereas de Freitas Netto et al.'s (2020) systematic review establishes it as the co-occurrence of poor performance and positive, misleading communication and proposes a fourfold typology crossing level of analysis (firm- versus product-level) with communicative form—claim greenwashing operating through textual assertions, executional greenwashing through non-textual cues such as green palettes or nature imagery. Where these establish what greenwashing is, Delmas and Burbano (2011) address why it occurs, decomposing its drivers into external (institutional pressure from regulators and NGOs; market pressure from consumers, investors, and competitors), organizational, and individual levels, the last including optimistic bias; Sections 2.3 and 2.5 return to the external categories.

Whether deceptive intent is definitionally necessary remains contested. Spaniol et al. (2024), synthesizing roughly seventy-nine definitions, derive six constitutive attributes—an unsubstantiated environmental claim, made by a private firm about a consumer product, with deceptive intent, for competitive advantage—treating intent as required, whereas Bernini and La Rosa (2024) allow that symbolic conformity can occur "whether intentionally or not," leaving unsettled whether greenwashing is a discrete deceptive act or a broader, potentially unintentional decoupling between communicated values and practice. A narrower related construct is selective disclosure: Nemes et al. (2022) treat it as partial or insufficiently contextualized statements emphasizing positive performance while omitting negative information, and Bernini and La Rosa (2024) distinguish passive omission from active exaggeration, together positioning it as asymmetric emphasis distinct from outright fabrication.

Separating misleading from ordinary positive communication is the field's unresolved crux. Gatti et al. (2019) frame a "grey zone" in which voluntary, unaudited CSR disclosure sits ambiguously between legitimate promotion and misleading claims, and Montgomery et al. (2024) locate an intensifying instance in a "Greenwash 3.0" stage of net-zero pledges not yet verifiable against outcomes; combined with Spaniol et al.'s (2024) substantiation criterion, this suggests the workable dividing line is evidentiary rather than tonal—positivity alone does not make a claim greenwashing, but its unsubstantiated, selectively emphasized, or not-yet-verifiable character does.

### 2.2 The Fast-Fashion Business Model and Sustainability Tensions

Fast fashion is best defined not as a product category but as a business logic of compressed cycle time, high-volume output, low unit price, and geographically dispersed sourcing. Bick et al. (2018) characterize it through inexpensive, rapidly produced clothing that has driven a sharp rise in garment consumption; Dzhengiz et al. (2023) show speed-to-market has intensified as global clothing production nearly doubled in fifteen years; and Wren (2022) attributes roughly half of the sector's 2018 emissions of about 2.1 billion metric tons to fast fashion specifically. The advantage to consumers—speed and affordability—is generated by the same volume and turnover that create its environmental burden, not a separable defect.

This places the model in persistent tension with sustainability claims. Bick et al. (2018) note the costs are unevenly distributed, with water-intensive cultivation, untreated-dye pollution, and hazardous labor concentrated in low- and middle-income manufacturing countries while consumption concentrates in the Global North—separating where impact is generated from where it is narrated—while Manshoven and Van Opstal (2022) describe a prisoner's-dilemma logic in which no single firm can unilaterally absorb the cost of deeper circularity without competitive penalty, so even willing actors gravitate toward regulation as a shared solution; Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) likewise describe an industry under sustained scrutiny in need of more circular practice. A further tension is whether improvement on a single indicator reduces total impact: Dzhengiz et al. (2023) argue firms adopt "eco-efficiency" framings—per-unit gains in recycled-fiber share or emissions per garment—while absolute production, and impact, keeps rising, risking decoupling between language and action, and Inês et al. (2023) frame environmental responsibility as a potential supply-chain competitive advantage while cautioning that greenwashing risk here is structural rather than a mere communication failure.

Two studies content-analyzed the sector's disclosures. Garcia-Torres et al. (2017) found the two largest fast-fashion companies' reporting analytically rich but thin on reported action, producing "orphan issues" in which materially important topics receive disproportionately little follow-through, while Feng and Ngai (2020), studying twelve global fashion companies including both firms here, found CSR reporting comprehensiveness rose industry-wide between 2013 and 2016, led by labor and environmental themes. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) add that transparency uptake nonetheless remained markedly uneven across major retailers despite shared external pressure—a common environment does not produce uniform disclosure, motivating the turn to institutional pressures.

### 2.3 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Environmental Communication

Institutional theory, in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) foundational formulation, explains organizational conformity to sustainability expectations through three pressures: coercive, arising from dependence on regulators and resource-holders; normative, from professionalization and the diffusion of shared standards; and mimetic, whereby firms imitate peers perceived as legitimate under uncertainty rather than as general competitive benchmarking. Delmas and Burbano's (2011) external-driver taxonomy maps onto the coercive and normative categories, and Latif et al. (2020) provide the clearest validated operationalization: modeling the three as separately measurable constructs, they find each independently predicts adoption of environmental management accounting among manufacturing firms. Although their survey-based Pakistani setting is far from document analysis of European fast fashion, this shows the triad is empirically tractable and separately codeable; Bernini and La Rosa (2024) situate it within the greenwashing literature, arguing firms respond to coercive, mimetic, and normative pressures by adopting communication intended to secure legitimacy.

Coercive pressure interacts temporally with mimetic dynamics rather than operating in isolation. Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023) trace non-financial disclosure among Spanish firms across the transposition of EU Directive 2014/95/EU, finding it rose in the anticipatory period before enactment—especially among environmentally sensitive sectors and lagging reporters—then reversed afterward for all but already-leading firms, which they read as mimetic and normative isomorphism operating ahead of coercive regulation. Manshoven and Van Opstal (2022) find comparable support, including from industry, for mandatory circularity regulation justified through a prisoner's-dilemma preference for a regulatory "level playing field" over voluntary first-moving; mimetic and coercive pressures are thus frequently entangled rather than cleanly separable.

Within fast fashion, Fraser and van der Ven (2022) offer the most direct account of uneven translation: comparing H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing, they find scandals necessary but insufficient for increased transparency, effective only alongside senior-management commitment and alignment with domestic norms—where only one condition held, as in Gap and Inditex, transparency did not shift, whereas H&M's combination of exposure, favorable institutions, and leadership commitment produced higher transparency. Identical exposure does not guarantee identical response, because internal agency mediates whether pressure becomes genuine change or symbolic conformity. Gatti et al. (2019) argue a wholly voluntary CSR regime is a "grey zone" facilitating greenwashing and propose combined voluntary-mandatory governance; consistent with this, Papa et al. (2024) find mandatory EU reporting coincided with improved narrative disclosure without matching most environmental KPIs, and Forliano et al. (2025) describe decoupling as symbolic communication with minimal substantive change. Notably, no corpus source documents institutional-pressure indicators observable inside report content itself, as distinct from surveys, disclosure indices, or talk-versus-KPI proxies—an acknowledged gap this thesis's directed content analysis must address.

### 2.4 Legitimacy, Symbolic Responses, and Environmental Decoupling

Legitimacy Theory supplies this thesis's principal supporting concept, treating environmental communication as a resource for aligning with societal expectations rather than as a neutral report of performance. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) frame legitimacy management as securing a "social license to operate" through disclosure rather than demonstrable performance change, and Forliano et al. (2025) find legitimacy theory ranks among the dominant macro-level frameworks across the ninety-seven studies they reviewed, alongside institutional and stakeholder theories. This thesis uses the term legitimacy gap as a working label for the space between the environmental image a firm's communication projects and what its underlying practice can substantiate—the gap that widens whenever that social license comes under strain. The label is a convenience, not attributed to any corpus source; it names a divergence Bernini and La Rosa (2024) and Forliano et al. (2025) describe without the phrase.

The mechanism most consistently linked to legitimacy-driven communication is decoupling—divergence between formally adopted environmental policy and its operational implementation. Papa et al. (2024), examining Polish listed firms under EU non-financial reporting, find narrative improvements unmatched by measurable environmental KPIs outside carbon emissions, indicating coercive pressure can produce a "talk" response without commensurate "action," while Loko and Schiehll (2025) extend this into a measurement framework distinguishing internally oriented ESG actions from externally oriented endorsement, finding policy-practice misalignment affects over four-fifths of a large cross-industry sample and persists across the panel. Neither examines fast fashion, yet together they anchor policy-practice decoupling as both theoretically expected and empirically demonstrable at scale.

This converges with a broader concern over the gap between communication and performance. Montgomery et al. (2024) chart greenwash scholarship across three phases culminating in forward-looking narratives, identifying selective disclosure and symbolic commitment without follow-through as persistent mechanisms; Nemes et al. (2022) operationalize related concerns through named criteria—selective disclosure, vagueness, and lack of substantiation—which this thesis adopts directly as anchors for its own codebook; and Janik and Ryszko (2026) reinforce decoupling between symbol and substance as a recurring manifestation among content-analysis approaches for detecting divergence between disclosure and organizational reality.

### 2.5 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure

Where Section 2.4 situated legitimacy-seeking as the dominant motive, the literature also identifies a countervailing dynamic: firms in competitive markets may disclose selectively rather than more as legitimacy pressure intensifies. Montgomery et al. (2024) treat selective disclosure—omitting or downplaying unfavorable information while emphasizing favorable claims—as among the most persistent forms of greenwashing, and Bernini and La Rosa (2024) sharpen this by separating passive-deception omission ("selective disclosure") from active-deception exaggeration ("misleading disclosure"); Forliano et al. (2025), mapping ninety-seven peer-reviewed studies, corroborate that legitimacy and institutional theory remain the field's dominant macro-level lenses and that decoupling recurs as the mechanism linking institutional pressure to disclosure behavior.

The clearest evidence that competition itself shapes disclosure comes from Lee and Lee (2022), whose study of South Korean listed firms found a negative relationship between industry competition and voluntary greenhouse-gas disclosure: as rivalry intensifies, managers withhold competitively sensitive information—a proprietary-cost mechanism governance quality moderated but did not eliminate—complicating any assumption of a "race to the top." Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou (2016) add scale: across 4,750 firms in 45 countries, more environmentally damaging firms are, if anything, less likely to selectively disclose once national scrutiny and global normative exposure are sufficiently intense, so scrutiny and norms function as constraints rather than enablers—selective disclosure appearing a default tendency external scrutiny can suppress rather than a fixed firm-level trait. Competition also operates through imitation: Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023) document mimetic convergence toward sector leaders before disclosure regulation took effect, then reversal for laggards once compliance turned coercive—firms may imitate rivals' framing and headline commitments while withholding the granular detail that carries competitive cost, consistent with Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) finding that fast-fashion firms frequently mention Sustainable Development Goals yet provide little target-level substantiation, with independent media surfacing negative practices absent from those reports.

Within fast fashion, Garcia-Torres et al. (2017) content-analyzed H&M's and Inditex's own reporting and found it richly descriptive but thin on reported action, producing "orphan issues"—material topics a company's own assessment identifies as important yet that receive little disclosed follow-through (in one example, innovation was the most material issue while accounting for a reported 3.54% of coded action statements). This action-level thinness is a different axis from Feng and Ngai's (2020) contemporaneous finding that reporting comprehensiveness was rising across the sector's largest firms, including both cases, over the same pre-2022 period: a report can cover more topics while following through on any one only thinly—the gap this thesis's directed content analysis is designed to trace.

### 2.6 International Business and Global Value Chains

The fragmentation of production across borders is not merely logistical; it is a structural condition shaping what a brand-owner can know, and choose to reveal, about its own supply chain. Asmussen et al. (2023) modeled this formally, showing that in globally dispersed value chains a supplier's CSR breaches "may be revealed by the MNE, external agents, or remain hidden," building information asymmetry directly into the lead-firm–supplier relationship, while Bick et al. (2018) grounded it geographically: consumption concentrates in the Global North while chemically intensive manufacturing and waste-disposal are outsourced to the Global South, where environmental regulation is comparatively weaker and less consistently enforced. The multinational-supplier relationship is thus not only economically but jurisdictionally uneven, supplying the structural opportunity for the selective-disclosure patterns this thesis investigates in RQ2.

Within that relationship, responsibility is bargained rather than fixed. Asmussen et al.'s (2023) coalitional model indicates large multinationals are incentivized to protect their "social brand" from reputational contagion originating at supplier sites—a plausible economic mechanism beneath the legitimacy-seeking discussed earlier—and points to a monitoring paradox whereby increased oversight without a coherent strategy can weaken the MNE's bargaining position rather than strengthen compliance, suggesting why audit and certification commitments may function symbolically. Inês et al.'s (2023) systematic review situates greenwashing as a supply-chain-management problem spanning supplier relationships, certification, and leadership commitment—general framing of where such risk arises rather than confirming evidence. Regulatory variation across borders compounds the asymmetry: Fraser and van der Ven (2022) explain heterogeneous transparency among the largest retailers, including H&M and Inditex, by the joint presence of scandal-driven reputational risk, home-country institutional norms, and senior-management commitment, with any single condition's absence sufficient to explain lower uptake, as in Inditex—domestic environments do not travel evenly across a firm's global footprint, reinforcing Bick et al.'s (2018) point that oversight is comparatively lax at the production end even where stringent at home. This literature locates the chapter's preceding threads within a globally fragmented, jurisdictionally uneven value chain.

### 2.7 Research Gap and Conceptual Framework

The preceding sections establish that greenwashing is best understood not as a single act but as a family of related mismatches—between claim and performance, policy and practice, emphasis and omission—whose boundary with ordinary promotional communication remains genuinely contested (Gatti et al., 2019; Spaniol et al., 2024; de Freitas Netto et al., 2020). Institutional Theory supplies the explanatory architecture for why firms produce such communication: coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures push organizations toward conformity, a triad originating in DiMaggio and Powell (1983), validated by Latif et al. (2020), and traced temporally by Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023), whose evidence of mimetic anticipation preceding coercive enactment shows these pressures interacting rather than operating in isolation. Legitimacy Theory and decoupling explain how conformity is delivered: firms may satisfy institutional pressure symbolically without matching substantive change, as Papa et al. (2024) and Loko and Schiehll (2025) confirm directly, if outside fast fashion.

Within fast fashion, this architecture meets a structurally distinct industry. The volume-and-speed logic documented by Bick et al. (2018), Dzhengiz et al. (2023), and Wren (2022) generates a burden isolated efficiency gains cannot resolve; Fraser and van der Ven (2022) show identical institutional exposure across H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing produced markedly different transparency depending on leadership commitment; Garcia-Torres et al. (2017), analyzing both firms' reporting, add that disclosure can be descriptively rich yet thin on action; and Lee and Lee (2022) show competitive rivalry independently pushes firms toward withholding rather than volunteering sensitive information, complicating any assumption that legitimacy and competitive pressure pull in the same direction. The international-business literature locates this asymmetry structurally: Asmussen et al. (2023) and Bick et al. (2018) show fragmented, jurisdictionally uneven value chains give lead firms both the opportunity and the incentive to communicate selectively about conditions they only partially observe.

What the literature has not yet done is bring institutional and competitive pressures together within a focused comparison of two closely matched multinationals over a defined recent period. Existing institutional-pressure evidence is single-firm, large-sample, or drawn from non-fashion sectors (Latif et al., 2020; Loko & Schiehll, 2025), and no source documents institutional-pressure indicators observable inside report content itself; competitive-disclosure evidence is similarly aggregate or single-country (Lee & Lee, 2022); and the only source analyzing H&M and Inditex together, Garcia-Torres et al. (2017), predates the 2022–2025 period of intensifying EU sustainability-disclosure regulation. A comparative, qualitative case study tracing both pressures jointly across H&M Group and Inditex during exactly this period remains absent.

Conceptually, this thesis draws the pressures and mechanisms traced above into a four-step framework—Drivers, IB Filter, Strategy, Outcome—organized as this thesis's own synthesis of the preceding review rather than a model borrowed from a single source. The Drivers are the coercive, normative, and mimetic institutional pressures reviewed in Section 2.3, joined by the competitive, market-based pressures that Lee and Lee (2022) and Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou (2016) document. Before becoming communication, these pass through an IB Filter specific to fast fashion's globally fragmented value chains—the jurisdictionally uneven, information-asymmetric buyer-supplier relationships that Asmussen et al. (2023) and Bick et al. (2018) describe, which limit what a lead firm can know, let alone choose to reveal, about conditions in its own supply chain. What emerges is a Strategy—substantive or symbolic response paired with a selective-disclosure orientation toward omission, disproportional emphasis, or inconsistent metrics—through which a firm manages the legitimacy gap introduced in Section 2.4. The Outcome is the environmental communication actually observed in each firm's reporting, sitting between substantive conformity and residual legitimacy gap. RQ1 traces the Drivers-to-Strategy sequence for institutional pressure specifically; RQ2 traces the same sequence for competitive pressure and the IB Filter's disclosure consequences.

In sum, this chapter has traced greenwashing's conceptual boundaries, fast fashion's structural tensions, and the institutional, legitimacy, and competitive mechanisms shaping disclosure, identifying a gap this thesis addresses through paired case comparison. Chapter 3 sets out the methodology developed to operationalize this framework.

# 3 Research Methodology

### 3.1 Research Design

This thesis adopts a qualitative research design to examine how H&M Group and Inditex construct environmental communication under differing configurations of institutional and competitive pressure. A qualitative approach is warranted because the framing, emphasis, and omission patterns within corporate sustainability disclosure are interpretive by nature and cannot be reduced to predetermined quantifiable indicators without losing the contextual meaning institutional theory requires. Bengtsson (2016) frames this demand through the distinction between manifest content, the visible surface of what a text states, and latent content, what a text intends or implies — both involving interpretation, differing in depth rather than in kind. This supports treating corporate disclosures not merely as a record of stated commitments but as material whose underlying framing must be read alongside its explicit claims.

The research uses a multiple-case variant of case study methodology, following from this interpretive orientation and the bounded, comparative nature of the problem. Baxter and Jack (2008) situate the design in the case-study tradition associated with Stake and Yin and justify examining H&M Group and Inditex together to explore similarities and differences between two comparable, bounded organizational cases rather than as an aggregated single-case account — matching the thesis's aim of contrasting institutional responses and disclosure patterns across two matched fast-fashion multinationals over a shared reporting period.

Documentary research is the primary data-collection strategy, treating corporate sustainability reports, press communications, and related disclosure documents as the empirical record from which meaning units are drawn — positioning the documents themselves, rather than interview or survey data, as the site where institutional pressures and disclosure choices become observable.

The analytic approach is directed qualitative content analysis, a deductive method in which coding categories are derived in advance from an established theoretical framework rather than emerging inductively. Kibiswa (2019) supports the principle that deriving codes from existing theory before engaging new empirical material serves to test, corroborate, or extend that theory's applicability to a context in which it was not originally developed — here, applying institutional theory's coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures, together with substantive and symbolic response categories, to fast-fashion corporate communication.

### 3.2 Case Selection and Study Period

H&M Group and Inditex were selected because they are the phenomenon under investigation rather than convenient examples of it: both are leading, globally visible fast-fashion multinationals whose environmental communication is subject to sustained public, regulatory, and academic attention. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) treat both as central members of the industry's competitive set in their comparative analysis of transparency practices among the four largest fast-fashion retailers, lending precedent to isolating them as analytically significant units. The two firms also offer strong comparability: both operate at similar global scale, rely on comparable multi-tier global sourcing models, are domiciled in the European Union, and compete in overlapping consumer markets. The shared EU domicile places both under a common coercive regulatory environment — most notably the EU's evolving sustainability-disclosure requirements — so that observed differences in communication can be attributed more plausibly to firm-level variation than to divergent external pressure.

The multinational and global-value-chain (GVC) character of both firms justifies analysis at the MNE level. Asmussen et al. (2023) argue that IB research on CSR has been dominated by an MNE-centric perspective and propose instead a bargaining view in which the visibility of CSR breaches at supplier sites depends on the negotiated relationship between lead firm and supplier; brand-owning MNEs such as these are incentivized to protect their "social brand" from reputational damage originating deeper in the value chain. Studying brand-level communication of such firms is therefore appropriate precisely because information asymmetry and selective visibility are structural features of these chains.

On variation in external scrutiny, Fraser and van der Ven (2022) report that H&M ranked as the most transparent of the four retailers examined in their pre-2022 comparison, while Gap and Inditex showed lower transparency because only one of three conditions — reputational risk, domestic norms, and leadership commitment — was present in each case. This finding, restricted to their pre-2022 window, supports treating the two firms as a meaningfully contrastable pair without asserting any claim about current behavior. Both publish detailed annual sustainability and non-financial disclosures, ensuring adequate data for the 2022–2025 period — a window expected to capture intensifying EU sustainability-disclosure regulation relevant to institutional-pressure analysis.

### 3.3 Data Collection and Document Selection

The document corpus comprises the primary, company-published annual and sustainability disclosure documents of H&M Group and Inditex covering the four fiscal years overlapping the 2022–2025 study window, supplemented by two categories of external material used strictly as empirical context. This section summarizes the collection logic and screening criteria; the individual documents, with their page ranges, are identified where they are first cited.

For H&M Group, six documents were collected: a dedicated "Sustainability Disclosure" for 2022 and 2023, and, from fiscal year 2024, a renamed "Sustainability Progress Report" issued alongside the combined Annual and Sustainability Report. This naming change is a documented fact rather than an inconsistency: both the 2023 and 2024 documents state the reporting-structure rationale, including anticipatory language about aligning with the forthcoming EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD). For Inditex, the sustainability content ("Statement on Non-Financial Information" in 2022–2023, retitled "Consolidated Statement of Non-Financial Information and Sustainability Information" from 2024) is embedded within the combined Inditex Group Annual Report, which runs to roughly 475–525 pages per year. Each year's report was split into multiple PDF parts purely for file-handling; each part is a direct, unedited excerpt of the official filing, and the report's own printed page numbers were used throughout coding to preserve continuous, citable pagination.

A further structural difference is the 2024 transition at which Inditex's disclosure adopts the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) architecture (General disclosures, E1–E5, S1–S4, G1), replacing the informal subsection headings used in 2022 and 2023, whereas H&M's reports do not adopt an equivalent ESRS-numbered structure within the window even while anticipating CSRD compliance from as early as 2022. This asymmetry — one firm restructuring around the mandatory EU standard a year before the other, while both discuss the same forthcoming regulation in narrative text — is treated as RQ1-relevant evidence about the timing and form of institutional pressure, not as a data-quality problem.

**Fiscal-year comparability caveat.** H&M Group's fiscal year runs 1 December–30 November, labeled by the year it mostly falls within ("FY2024" spans December 2023–November 2024); Inditex's runs 1 February–31 January, labeled by the year it begins ("FY2024" spans February 2024–January 2025). A same-numbered fiscal year therefore denotes two different, only partially overlapping windows, roughly two months apart. Each report's actual covered period, not merely its label, is what the analysis relies on when comparing across the two firms.

**Inclusion and exclusion criteria.** Included in the core coding corpus are primary, company-published annual or sustainability disclosure documents in English covering fiscal years overlapping the 2022–2025 window, for both firms. Excluded are press releases, marketing microsites, and social media content, which are not equivalent in evidentiary weight to a formal disclosure; such material may be referenced qualitatively in the Discussion where directly relevant but is not part of the coded corpus.

**Document screening.** Before admission to coding, each document was checked against Morgan's (2022) criteria of authenticity, credibility, representativeness, and meaning. Authenticity was assessed by cross-validating Board composition, named executives, and the Ernst & Young audit-signature pattern for internal consistency across each firm's successive years (for example, Inditex's Marta Ortega Pérez as Chair and Óscar García Maceiras as CEO recur across all four years, each audited by the same firm). Representativeness and meaning were addressed by relying exclusively on each firm's own primary, full-length filing rather than a third-party excerpt, translation, or summary.

**External/context documents.** Two categories of material outside the firms' own disclosures are used as empirical context, never as academic literature, consistent with the study's scope boundary (Section 1.3): (1) regulatory and policy documents — the European Commission's Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (COM(2022) 141 final) and the Dutch Authority for Consumers and Markets' 2022 commitment decision regarding H&M's sustainability claims — used to situate coercive-pressure coding (RQ1) and, for the ACM decision, as an external-challenge reference for RQ2; and (2) two NGO/industry assessments used as triangulation sources for RQ2. The primary triangulation source is Fashion Revolution's Fashion Transparency Index, which scores brands on the transparency of their disclosure rather than on underlying performance — a direct fit for RQ2 — and which H&M's own reports cite as a benchmark in several coded years. The secondary source is the Changing Markets Foundation's "Synthetics Anonymous" series, used narrowly as EXTERNAL CHALLENGE context; because it is advocacy-toned, any use in Chapter 4/5 attributes claims explicitly to the NGO rather than adopting them as this thesis's finding.

Both NGO sources carry a verification limitation: only index scores that could be confirmed directly against the primary reports are used, and figures that could not be so confirmed are excluded rather than reported. A further limitation is that the full-scope Index edition covers 2022 and 2023, after which the comparable instrument narrows to the climate-focused "What Fuels Fashion?" edition. The 2024 edition scores both firms directly (H&M 61%, Zara/Inditex 41%) and supplies the clearest cross-company RQ2 triangulation point, while the earlier full-scope editions confirm only H&M's score, so the triangulation is one-sided before 2024.

### 3.4 Data Analysis

The corpus is analyzed through directed qualitative content analysis, in which coding categories are established from theory before the data are read. Following Kibiswa (2019), the researcher draws categories from existing theory to guide the research — here, the coercive, normative, and mimetic pressure categories and the substantive, symbolic, and mixed response categories derived from Institutional Theory in Chapter 2, together with the eight greenwashing-indicator categories drawn from the greenwashing literature (Nemes et al., 2022; Spaniol et al., 2024). Applying these categories to fast-fashion communication is precisely the kind of theory extension directed content analysis supports.

The coding process follows the four stages Bengtsson (2016) identifies: decontextualisation, recontextualisation, categorisation, and compilation. In decontextualisation, each document was read in full and broken into meaning units — discrete passages making a claim, commitment, target, or disclosure-relevant statement — rather than coded at the level of isolated words, consistent with the unit-of-analysis definition in the codebook. In recontextualisation, each unit was checked against the codebook's a priori categories; a unit corresponding to none was logged as a residual observation rather than forced into an ill-fitting code — the process through which pilot coding surfaced the refinements incorporated into the second version of the codebook. Categorisation groups units along three simultaneous dimensions: institutional-pressure type and corporate-response type (RQ1), and greenwashing-indicator and competitive-pressure-signal codes (RQ2), following the codebook's rule that a single unit may carry codes on more than one dimension. Compilation draws the coded material into within-case findings and a cross-case comparison, addressing both research questions through the same matrix.

This process is grounded in Elo et al.'s (2014) description of the organization phase of content analysis as the development and application of a categorization matrix against which all data are reviewed for correspondence — the codebook's matrix operationalizes exactly this logic, each row recording the coded passage, its codes, the supporting evidence, and any counter-evidence. Bengtsson's (2016) manifest/latent distinction justifies why coding for response type and greenwashing indicators cannot rest on manifest wording alone: judging whether a response is substantive or symbolic, or a pattern disproportional emphasis or omission, requires interpreting the relationship between what is stated and what is left unstated — a latent-level judgment the codebook's evidence and counter-evidence fields make transparent and auditable.

### 3.5 Trustworthiness and Limitations

Because this thesis uses a qualitative, comparative document analysis, trustworthiness was evaluated using the criteria Elo et al. (2014) establish for qualitative research: credibility, dependability, confirmability, and transferability, each pursued through a specific procedural choice. Credibility was supported by triangulating across multiple document types per company — annual and sustainability reports and, where available, press communications and third-party coverage — following Wood et al.'s (2020) argument that using multiple sources for triangulation enhances credibility. Dependability and confirmability were pursued jointly through the coding matrix's audit-trail structure: every coded unit records the code applied, the textual evidence supporting it, and any counter-evidence, allowing coding decisions to be traced rather than taken on the analyst's authority — consistent with Wood et al.'s (2020) argument that rigour is achieved substantially through explicitness about assumptions and procedures. Transferability is limited by the two-case design: findings describe how pressures manifest in H&M Group's and Inditex's communication specifically and are not generalizable to fast fashion as an industry, though the paired-case logic of Section 3.2 supports analytic rather than statistical generalization.

A central limitation of document analysis is the self-reporting bias inherent in corporate-authored disclosures. Morgan (2022) argues that documents produced by commercial organizations are typically intended to create a positive impression, and that internal actors have an incentive to conceal mistakes, producing a gap between what a document states and what actually occurs. Because RQ1 and RQ2 both investigate whether communication diverges from underlying practice, the corpus must be read as a strategically constructed artifact, which is why the codebook (Section 3.4) requires counter-evidence logging alongside every code and restricts greenwashing-indicator codes to units with an identifiable textual or cross-referential basis, never a tone judgment alone.

Further limitations follow from the codebook's construction and application. The codebook was piloted on a small, matched sample of both companies' FY2024 disclosures before full coding and refined into a second version that resolved the pilot's open questions on the entanglement of coercive and mimetic pressure (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023; Manshoven & Van Opstal, 2022) and on the codeability of the OMISSION indicator by splitting it into two narrower sub-tests; the cross-case PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING judgment could only be tested once the full four-year, two-company matrices were complete. Finally, coding was conducted by a single analyst; without a second coder, inter-coder reliability could not be formally assessed, and the audit-trail approach is offered as a partial, transparency-based substitute rather than an equivalent.

In sum, this chapter has set out a qualitative, comparative case-study design grounded in directed content analysis of H&M Group's and Inditex's own disclosures, justified the case selection and 2022–2025 study period, described the document corpus and coding procedure, and identified the trustworthiness measures and limitations that bound what the analysis can claim. Chapter 4 now reports the results this design produced.

# 4 Results

### 4.1 Overview of the Document Corpus

The findings draw on primary, company-published sustainability and annual reports for both firms across 2022–2025. For H&M Group: the Sustainability Disclosure 2022 and 2023, the Annual & Sustainability Report 2024 with its Sustainability Progress Report 2024, and the Annual & Sustainability Report 2025 (H&M Group, 2022; H&M Group, 2023; H&M Group, 2024; H&M Group, 2025). For Inditex: the sustainability sections of the Group Annual Report — the "Statement on Non-Financial Information" in 2022–2023, restructured as the "Consolidated Statement of Non-Financial Information and Sustainability Information" from 2024 (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023; Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025). Coding produced 237 units across ten themes common to both firms — climate and emissions, water, chemicals and pollution, biodiversity, materials and circular economy, waste, own workforce, workers in the value chain, communities, and consumers — 122 from H&M and 115 from Inditex.

Two structural features bear on the sections below. First, the firms report on non-identical fiscal calendars: H&M's runs 1 December to 30 November, Inditex's 1 February to 31 January, so a given "FY2024" denotes two windows roughly two months apart; same-year comparisons refer to each report's own covered period. Second, reporting architectures diverge from 2024: Inditex's 2024 and 2025 statements adopt the EU's European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS 2, E1–E5, S1–S4, G1), replacing the informal headings of 2022–2023, while H&M's reports anticipate future CSRD compliance without an ESRS-numbered structure. Per Methodology Section 3.3, external NGO/industry transparency-assessment data (the Fashion Transparency Index and Changing Markets Foundation reporting) was reserved for triangulation in Chapter 5 rather than coded here.

### 4.2 H&M Group

Across H&M's four disclosures, climate framing shifts alongside a recurring transparency pattern. The 2022 report frames H&M as anticipatorily "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded COERCIVE/NORMATIVE and SYMBOLIC (no date or metric attaches) (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16). By the FY2024 Progress Report the comparable unit states the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 2). A MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern appears in the earliest and FY2024 report: 2022 discloses a 9,270-tonne (+18%) year-on-year scope 1+2 increase alongside an 8% reduction against the 2019 baseline, attributed to the Russian wind-down (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses a 201,000-tonne (+3%) scope 3 increase alongside a 23.7% baseline reduction (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9). By 2025 scope 3 reverses to a 13.6% decrease (34.6% against the 2019 baseline), coded SUBSTANTIVE (H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). A scope 3 baseline restatement on the same progress-versus-baseline KPI in 2022 and 2023 — from -9% to -6% and -7% to -15% — coded INCONSISTENT METRIC in 2022 but uncoded in 2023, where the report explains the change in the same passage (H&M Group, 2022, p. 28; H&M Group, 2023, pp. 20–21).

Materials and circular economy shows a recurring bundling pattern in the headline "recycled or sustainably sourced materials" percentage. In 2022 the 84% headline (23% recycled, remainder "other more sustainably sourced") is broken out only via a chart, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY (H&M Group, 2022, p. 43). The 2023 report breaks the same aggregate (85%) into 25% recycled and 60% sustainably-sourced within one table and is uncoded (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 38–39). By FY2024 the aggregate rises to 89% (29.5% recycled), again without an in-unit split and not formally coded (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 18–20); the 2025 aggregate (91%, 32% recycled) is likewise uncoded (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 90–91). A related denominator pattern appears in resale: 2022 gives no group-wide scale denominator for resale, rental, or repair, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses resale at 0.6% of turnover, flagged only as an unconfirmed DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS candidate (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 21–22); and 2025 states 0.8% of turnover in the same sentence as the growth claim, recorded as not meeting that trigger (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90).

Biodiversity is the thinnest theme in quantification. The 2022 and 2023 reports present regenerative-agriculture pilot data — farmer counts and tonnage targets for named projects — without a denominator connecting them to total sourcing volume, both coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33; H&M Group, 2023, p. 28). The FY2024 Progress Report states Biodiversity is one of only two sections (with Communities) reporting no quantified progress figure, its ambition statement coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17). The 2023 report separately discloses a CDP Forest score decline across all three tracked commodities (Cattle Products B- to C, Timber B to C, Palm Oil B to D), admitting "the decreased scores have not been fully analysed," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 29). By 2025 the report states no biodiversity-related targets exist for that year, adoption deferred to 2026, recorded as a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING candidate (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87).

Workers findings show a recurring gap between reported wage data and living-wage or equity outcomes claimed. The 2022 report states H&M's Wage Strategy, via its ACT (Action, Collaboration, Transformation) membership, "aims to deliver living wages," while the wage tables measure only average wages against the statutory minimum, coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION with a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING flag (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71). An independent gender-pay-gap study led by Professor Raymond Robertson, covering more than 1,300 production units, finds a "statistically significant pay gap between men and women in most... production countries" in 2022, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend), and in 2023 finds gaps "highest in Bangladesh and India" without disclosing magnitude, coded SUBSTANTIVE despite being unquantified (H&M Group, 2022, p. 71; H&M Group, 2023, p. 61). Tier-1 trade-union representation falls from 42% to 37% (2022–2023) and 39% to 36% (2024–2025), both self-disclosed alongside prior-year figures and coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 62; H&M Group, 2025, p. 110). The FY2024 wage-grid disclosure (290 factories) lacks the percentage-of-tier-1-suppliers denominator given for the adjacent enrolment figure in the same paragraph, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 31); by 2025 wage-management-system enrolment reaches 80% against a 100%-by-2028 target, and the report states the target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact," coded MEANS-ENDS DECOUPLING (self-disclosed) (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110).

Within water and chemicals, effluent-treatment-plant functionality at green grade declines across the three tracked years — 90% (2022), 89% (2023), 84.4% (FY2024) — each disclosed in the water performance table, the 2022 figure also stated in the narrative alongside an acknowledgement of "missing our goal of 100%," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) without meeting the text-anchored DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS trigger (H&M Group, 2022, p. 36; H&M Group, 2023, p. 25; H&M Group, 2024b, p. 15). The 2025 report discloses a re-scoped chemicals metric — 92.6% ZDHC Manufacturing Restricted Substances List assurance against a 95%-by-2030 target — while stating "these targets have not been validated by an external party," coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 82–83).

In a rare 2022 instance a company self-identifies an external challenge to a prior claim: the decade-long "Conscious" product-label program is retired, the decision "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation," coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE and MIXED (scope-narrowed), as no successor metric or date is given (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14). The same report discloses a 21-percentage-point decline in stakeholder-rated transparency trust, from 71% to 50%, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2022, p. 14). By 2024 and 2025, the report states, under each topic's targets-and-actions subheading, that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets are set for Consumers and end-users and Affected communities; the 2025 report extends this to Business conduct, whereas 2024 reports targets there on preventing corruption and bribery — recorded as POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING alongside the dated, quantified targets given for every environmental topic in the same reports (H&M Group, 2024a, pp. 102–103, 105; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118).

### 4.3 Inditex

Inditex's Statement on Non-Financial Information changes structure partway through the period: the 2022 and 2023 reports (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023) use company-defined "Committed to" headings with no ESRS cross-references, while the 2024 and 2025 reports (Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025) follow the CSRD-mandated ESRS structure — affecting which findings trace continuously and which appear only once the relevant ESRS chapter exists.

On climate, the 2022 Environment section opens with Inditex having "achieved our goal of 100% renewable electricity in our facilities" (Inditex, 2022, p. 197); the emissions chart two pages later shows Scope 1 and 2 combined at well under one percent of total emissions, Scope 3 the remainder (Inditex, 2022, p. 199), coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS: the favorable, small-denominator achievement is narrated without the large-denominator context, though tabulated nearby. The 2024 report repeats the pattern for the Scope 1+2 versus Scope 3 trajectory (down 87.7% versus down 1.3% from 2018), again coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS, the "Towards net-zero emissions" graphic foregrounding the Scope 1+2 trend while the full breakdown sits in a separate table (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163). By contrast, 2023 and 2025 place the unfavorable figure in the same sentence as the favorable: in 2023 the "scope 1 and 2 emissions by 97%" reduction and the target-relevant figure that "increased by 4%" appear together (Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194); in 2025 an 88% Scope 1+2 reduction and a 7% Scope 3 (SBT-relevant) reduction are reported together, "in both cases compared to 2018" (Inditex, 2025, p. 134) — both coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend). Self-consumption follows a related pattern: on-site generation is narrated with a rising figure in every report (7,756 MWh in 2022 to 11,506 MWh in 2025), while total energy consumption — roughly 1.6–1.7 million MWh throughout — appears elsewhere without the resulting share (under one percent every year) in the same passage (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2023, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2024, pp. 157, 165; Inditex, 2025, pp. 129, 132), coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022–2024 and DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS in 2025.

Inditex's headline materials metric changes label across the period. The 2022 "preferred" fibers and materials reach 60% of total use, with a footnote stating the company had "aligned the terminology used in our objectives with that currently being used by benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" while asserting "the meaning and ambition of our goals remains unchanged" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) — coded INCONSISTENT METRIC (self-disclosed realignment, not substantiated with a like-for-like conversion). The same page reports a separate metric: 61% of products met Inditex's group-wide Join Life standard, against the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187), not flagged and distinct from the INCONSISTENT METRIC coding above. The 2023 report retains the "preferred raw materials" label at 68% (Inditex, 2023, pp. 236–237), while 2024 and 2025 relabel the metric "lower-impact fibres," at 73% (Inditex, 2024, p. 200) and 88% (Inditex, 2025, p. 150); the 2025 table gives only current- and target-year columns, and definitional continuity with 2024 was not verified, so no cross-year comparison is drawn. Within "preferred cotton," the largest component in 2022 and 2023 is Better Cotton, footnoted both years as a mass-balance calculation "not physically traceable to the final product" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 188–189; Inditex, 2023, pp. 238–239) — coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022, not coded in 2023. The Clothing Collection Programme's recovered tonnage recurs every year — 17,015 (2022), 20,259 (2023), 19,484 (2024), 20,495 tonnes (2025) — each with a reuse/recycling split but no denominator expressing tonnage as a share of garments placed on the market (Inditex, 2022, pp. 192–193; Inditex, 2023, p. 243; Inditex, 2024, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2025, pp. 150–152). Coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test in 2022, 2023, and 2025; in 2024 it was treated as a cross-case comparability gap against H&M's percentage-of-turnover resale disclosure.

Biodiversity shows the clearest quantified trajectory. The 2022 report frames biodiversity action under the Science Based Targets Network's "Avoid, Reduce, Restore, Regenerate and Transform" (AR3T) framework and names certifications and restoration projects, but discloses no Group-wide hectare or aggregate metric, unlike the numeric targets given for climate, water, and materials in the same report; coded SYMBOLIC and WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211). The only quantified area that year is a regenerative-agriculture partnership in India spanning 300,000 hectares, without a share-of-sourcing statement (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–210). The 2023 report introduces a dated, quantified Group-wide target — protect, restore, regenerate, or otherwise improve biodiversity across five million hectares by 2030 — but discloses no cumulative progress figure, the same India partnership remaining the only area figure (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), coded OMISSION. From 2024 the target is tracked with a current-year figure: 758,534 hectares (622,878 restored plus 135,656 regenerated), a sum the coding matrix verified reconciles (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192); by 2025 it reaches 1.5 million hectares (1,107,098 restored plus 398,217 regenerated), again reconciling (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Both units are coded SUBSTANTIVE with no greenwashing indicator.

Within the "Workers at the Centre" strategy, the Living Wages Priority Impact Area shows a consistent pattern across all four years. In 2022 the sub-section names country-specific projects — the ACT collaboration in Bangladesh, Cambodia, and Türkiye; LEAN; SCORE; HERfinance — but discloses no wage-level figure, living-wage-benchmark gap, or even a people-reached figure (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228), coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE decoupling. From 2023 a people-reached figure appears but is consistently the smallest of the strategy's five Priority Impact Areas: 31,318 in 2023, against Health's 1,125,665 (Inditex, 2023, pp. 289–290); 86,268 in 2024, against Health's 1,313,217 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262); 120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028 (Inditex, 2025, p. 178). No wage-level, benchmark, or wage-gap metric appears in any year. The 2023 and 2024 units are coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE (candidate); the 2025 coding records the same reach-only pattern for a second consecutive year as a more confirmed cross-year pattern of disclosure practice.

On water, the 2022 report discloses both an absolute water-consumption figure and two intensity denominators (240 litres/m² and 55 ml/EUR) for its own facilities (Inditex, 2022, pp. 206–207), a level of denomination that year's Waste sub-section does not match. A related asymmetry appears in 2025 between the Water and Energy chapters: Energy discloses an absolute total plus intensity metrics, while Water reports only a relative intensity metric (litres per kilogram of garment), stating "the amount of water consumed is very low" and treating withdrawal as more representative without an absolute volume (Inditex, 2025, pp. 142–143) — coded a provisional OMISSION candidate. On communities, the 2025 materiality assessment states "the identified potential impacts and risks linked to ESRS 'S3 - Affected communities' are not material for Inditex" (Inditex, 2025, p. 121); community content appears only as narrative color across the biodiversity, materials, and workers-in-the-value-chain chapters, without a dedicated metric framework (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148, 151, 175, 179). This exclusion is coded OMISSION under the cross-case topic-omission sub-test.

### 4.4 Cross-Case Comparison

The clearest recurring pattern in both firms' climate disclosure is a structural separation between scope 1+2 emissions, framed with SUBSTANTIVE, SBTi-validated targets, and scope 3, the large majority of each footprint, on a more uneven trajectory. H&M shows a stable MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) scope 3 pattern every coded year: +18% (Russia exit) in 2022, +3% (material weight, airfreight) in the 2024 Progress Report, and -13.6% in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). Inditex shows a narrower, intermittent version: DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS in 2022, the headline scope 1+2/renewable-electricity figure stated narratively while the scope 3 figure sits in a chart (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199), and again in 2024 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 162–163); the test is not met in 2023 or 2025, where favorable and unfavorable figures share a sentence (Inditex, 2023, pp. 190–191, 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). In both, scope 3 figures are self-disclosed rather than absent.

A second contrast concerns Living Wages, which both firms name after a wage outcome but document with markedly different metrics. H&M's 2022 disclosure pairs an ACT/living-wage aim with a minimum-versus-statutory-wage gap table that is not itself a living-wage benchmark (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71), while its 2024 Progress Report reports a seven-factory Mainland China gender-pay-gap reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% alongside an admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 29, 31). By 2025 H&M introduces a coverage-based Wage Management System target (80% enrolment in 2025, 100% by 2028) described by the company as focused on "programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110). Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area reports zero wage-level, living-wage-benchmark, or wage-gap metrics in any of the four coded years, disclosing only a "people reached" count rising to 86,268 in 2024 and 120,050 in 2025 — consistently the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas, well below the 2,159,028 reported for Health in the same year (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). This most consistently meets the cross-case PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING test: a topic named after a wage outcome, reported with a reach figure but never a wage-level figure, across three consecutive Inditex years, in a document quantifying most other themes to a comparable or greater level of detail. The finding is specific to supply-chain wage data; both firms separately report comparable gender-pay-gap percentages for their own direct workforce, so the asymmetry is not a general wage-transparency gap.

A third contrast concerns Biodiversity, where the firms reach markedly different quantification. H&M's disclosure remains SYMBOLIC throughout: an "aim to make time-bound commitments in 2023" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34), a 2023 statement that a target is "still in the process of being developed" (H&M Group, 2023, p. 27), a 2024 Progress Report in which Biodiversity is one of only two sections carrying no quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17), and a 2025 statement that the company has "not yet set biodiversity-related targets" and intends to adopt them "in 2026" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex's follows a different trajectory: a quantified 2030 target of 5 million hectares present from 2022 (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211), without a Group-level current-year progress figure through 2023 (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), before becoming fully quantified and internally arithmetic-consistent — 758,534 hectares in 2024 and 1.5 million in 2025, anchored to the Science Based Targets Network AR3T framework (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147) — a disclosure-practice asymmetry, not evidence about ecological outcomes.

A fourth divergence appears in the Communities theme in 2025. H&M's 2024 Progress Report lists Communities as one of only two sections without a quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 32), and its 2025 report states the company has "not set any time-bound and outcome-oriented targets related to affected communities" (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112), retaining the topic but attaching no target — a SYMBOLIC target status. Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment concludes instead that "S3 - Affected communities... are not material for Inditex," removing Communities from its material-topic list (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both reduce commitment-level disclosure relative to earlier years in structurally different forms — a retained, target-less topic at H&M, a materiality-based exclusion at Inditex — coded as distinct.

Finally, the firms differ in how they anchor competitive or peer-benchmarking language. Rows tagged COMPETITIVE FRAMING or PEER BENCHMARKING appear nine times across H&M's four reports against seven across Inditex's, H&M's count rising from one in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in the 2024 Progress Report before falling to one in the ESRS-structured 2025 report; Inditex's stays flat (two, two, one, two). H&M's instances are more often anchored to verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP climate score of A in the 2024 Progress Report (A− in 2023), a Fashion Revolution Transparency Index rank, a Textile Exchange Material Change Index "Leading" designation (one of six overall leaders of 130 companies in 2023, and one of just 11 to reach "Leading" in 2024), and one named-competitor comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 5, 20). Inditex's rows are more often self-referential leadership statements without a named comparator — on-site self-consumption capacity as an achievement, and Care for Water framed as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2025, p. 144) — with a single exception, an IPE ranking citing the company as "third... globally and in the textile sector" (Inditex, 2023, p. 229). Competitive-framing language remains secondary for both firms.

The cross-case coding also produced one explicit negative result relevant to RQ2: no category of granular data disclosed by Inditex was found conspicuously and consistently absent from H&M's coverage of the same topic. Candidates considered and rejected include hectare-level biodiversity project reporting and named community-investment figures; in each case H&M's own reporting discloses the same underlying information, if in a differently presented or less consolidated form (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148; H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33, 81; Inditex, 2023, pp. 234–235; Inditex, 2025, p. 176). The withholding asymmetries therefore run in one direction only, from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds, most clearly on supply-chain wage data; no comparably supported finding runs the other way.

### 4.5 Summary of Findings

Across the ten themes, Sections 4.2 through 4.4 present structural divergence and uneven emphasis rather than a single shared pattern: substantive, SBTi-validated scope 1+2 targets against more uneven scope 3 in both firms; rising recycled- or lower-impact-material headlines paired with missing production- or sales-volume denominators; a sharp biodiversity divergence, with H&M symbolic and untargeted throughout while Inditex quantifies from a 2022 hectare target; living-wage ambitions unaccompanied by any wage-level or benchmark figure at either firm; and a 2025 Communities split — retained but target-less at H&M, excluded as non-material at Inditex.

Taken together, the coding identifies patterns traceable to specific documents, years, and pages rather than a single label for either firm. For RQ1, both firms' material mixes substantive and symbolic responses rather than one uniform type. For RQ2, the cross-case matrices identify page-cited selective-disclosure patterns — omission, disproportional emphasis, unclear boundary, and inconsistent metric — in both firms, alongside one withholding asymmetry running from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds on supply-chain wage data, with no comparably supported instance the other way. Chapter 5 now turns to interpreting these findings against the institutional-theoretical framework developed in Chapter 2, answering RQ1 and RQ2 directly and drawing out their theoretical and practical implications.

# 5 Discussion

### 5.1 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Responses (RQ1)

The clearest institutional-pressure signal is coercive, and its striking feature is timing. H&M Group's climate disclosure carries anticipatory regulatory language from the first study year — framing the company in 2022 as "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded symbolic because no date or metric attaches (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16) — and sustains it through the FY2024 statement that the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 2). Inditex's 2022–2023 material contains little equivalent rhetoric, yet its architecture converts to the full ESRS-numbered structure already in 2024, a cycle before H&M's disclosure takes a comparably compliance-scoped form in 2025 (Sections 4.1, 4.4). This maps onto, and complicates, Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) finding that non-financial disclosure expands before a regulation binds: H&M exemplifies their "talk-first" pattern, absorbing coercive pressure as language for roughly three cycles before absorbing it as structure, while Inditex shows what that account does not predict — early structural conformity with little preceding narrative. Coercive pressure thus surfaced through two absorption sequences: rhetoric before architecture in one case, architecture without much rhetoric in the other. This within-pair variation supports Latif et al.'s (2020) premise that institutional pressures are tractable as distinct constructs, one pressure type leaving different observable traces.

The substantive–symbolic comparison RQ1 calls for resolves most informatively at theme level, not firm level. Both firms anchor scope 1 and 2 emissions to SBTi-validated targets coded substantive — a normative anchoring, since science-based target-setting is voluntary, not legally mandated — while scope 3, the majority of each footprint, follows a more uneven trajectory (Section 4.4). For H&M this recurs as the codebook's MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) sub-pattern in nearly every year: an unfavorable year-on-year scope 1+2 or scope 3 rise printed beside a favorable baseline reduction (a +18% rise beside an 8% reduction in 2022), reversing to a substantive −13.6% scope 3 figure only in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). This is not decoupling in the concealment sense: the unfavorable figures are self-disclosed, merely placed beside a flattering comparator, a transparent-setback pattern the coding kept distinct from scope manipulation. Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) observation that symbolic conformity can operate "whether intentionally or not" is apposite here. The persistence of the split in both firms is consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) finding that decoupling under EU regulation is uneven across sub-dimensions: where measurement infrastructure is mature and externally validated, responses are substantive; where it is not, they thin out.

Biodiversity is where that thinning is most visible and the firms diverge most sharply. H&M's biodiversity disclosure stays symbolic across all four years — an aim to set commitments in 2023, a target "still in the process of being developed," one of only two FY2024 sections without any quantified progress figure, and an explicit 2025 statement that no biodiversity targets exist, adoption deferred to 2026 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34; H&M Group, 2023, p. 27; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex moves the opposite way, from a symbolic 2022 certification-listing, through a dated five-million-hectare 2030 target without progress data in 2023, to arithmetically reconciled hectare figures in 2024 and 2025 anchored to the Science Based Targets Network framework (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211; Inditex, 2023, p. 230; Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Read through Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) argument that institutional pressure is mediated by internal organizational commitment, the divergence extends their firm-level insight inward: mediation operates theme by theme within each firm, not between firms (Section 4.4). Normative pressure leaves a further trace: Inditex's realignment of its materials terminology to match "benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) reads as convergence on field-level standard vocabulary — a mimetic-normative move under definitional uncertainty — though it earned an INCONSISTENT METRIC coding because the continuity claim was asserted, not demonstrated. Genuinely mimetic conformity in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) strict sense — imitation of organizations perceived as legitimate under uncertainty, not benchmarking against a named rival — is thus better evidenced by convergence on shared voluntary frameworks (SBTi, Textile Exchange, ZDHC) than by the peer-ranking language examined next, which belongs to competitive signaling rather than institutional isomorphism (Section 5.3).

### 5.2 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure (RQ2)

The most consistent selective-disclosure finding is the supply-chain wage asymmetry, aligning closely with the proprietary-cost mechanism. Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area — a program named after a wage outcome — reports no wage-level, benchmark, or wage-gap figure in any of the four years, disclosing only a people-reached count that is consistently the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas (120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028), in reports that quantify most other themes in comparable detail (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). H&M, by contrast, publishes wage tables against statutory minima, an independent supplier gender-pay-gap study, and a concrete outcome figure — a seven-factory reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside its admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 29, 31). Lee and Lee's (2022) finding that intensified competition depresses voluntary disclosure of competitively sensitive information gives the most parsimonious reading: country- and factory-level wage data reveal sourcing-cost structure, a plausibly proprietary category for an apparel buyer. Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) scrutiny-and-norms finding adds a complement: the gap may persist where wage-level scrutiny and normative pressure remain weaker than for the environmental metrics this corpus shows Inditex quantifying rigorously under an otherwise-comparable regime (Section 5.1). Three qualifications keep this within what the coding supports. It is domain-specific — both firms disclose own-workforce gender-pay-gap figures, so it is not a general wage-transparency gap. It is directional: the cross-case coding found no category that Inditex discloses and H&M comparably withholds (Section 4.4), which strengthens the pattern because disconfirming candidates were sought and rejected. And it is a disclosure finding, not a wage finding: nothing in the coded material speaks to what Inditex's suppliers actually pay, only to what the reports state.

A second family of findings concerns emphasis rather than absence. Inditex's climate narrative in 2022 and 2024 foregrounds favorable small-denominator achievements — the 100% renewable-electricity statement, the −87.7% scope 1+2 trajectory — while the corresponding scope 3 figures, though tabulated nearby, do not share the same or an adjacent sentence, meeting the codebook's DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS test; in 2023 and 2025 the figures share a sentence and the test is not met (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199; Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163; Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). The self-consumption energy pattern behaves identically (Section 4.3). This is selective disclosure in Nemes et al.'s (2022) sense — partial, insufficiently contextualized emphasis on positive performance, distinct from fabrication — and Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) passive, omission-based mode rather than their active, misleading one: the unfavorable numbers exist in the same documents, so only narrative prominence varies, the "grey zone" of unaudited voluntary framing that Gatti et al. (2019) argue resists any bright-line classification. The denominator findings carry the same signature on the H&M side: resale reported with no group-wide scale figure in 2022, then 0.6% of turnover in FY2024 (an emphasis candidate only), then 0.8% stated in the same sentence as the growth claim in 2025, no longer triggering (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 21–22; H&M Group, 2025, p. 90) — a gap that closes over time, unlike Inditex's collection-tonnage series, which lacks a garments-placed-on-market denominator in nearly every year (Section 4.3).

Selectivity can also be procedural. H&M retains Communities as a topic while stating that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets exist for it (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112); Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment concludes that ESRS "S3 – Affected communities" is "not material for Inditex," removing the theme from its material-topic list (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both reduce commitment-level disclosure, but the second operates upstream: under a double-materiality regime the materiality assessment determines what must be reported at all, so exclusion at that stage is a structurally stronger form of omission than an unmet target. This extends Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) "orphan issues" logic in an unexpected direction — where their concern was material topics receiving thin follow-through, the ESRS architecture creates the option of resolving that tension by re-drawing materiality rather than deepening disclosure.

External triangulation, within its limits, points the same way as the coded asymmetry. Fashion Revolution's Fashion Transparency Index scored H&M at 66% in 2022, tied with The North Face and Timberland in the report's top-10 table (Fashion Revolution, 2022, p. 13); Zara/Inditex does not appear there, placing its 2022 score below the lowest tied group shown (54%). In 2023 the Index scored H&M at 71%, confirmed directly against the report's "Highest Scoring Brands" chart (Fashion Revolution, 2023, p. 19), where H&M sits between United Colors of Benetton (73%) and C&A (68%). The narrower climate-only successor edition scores both firms comparably in 2024: H&M at 61%, confirmed against the "What Fuels Fashion?" 2024 results (Fashion Revolution, 2024, p. 21), and Zara/Inditex at 41% in the full distribution (Fashion Revolution, 2024, p. 20) — a directly-confirmed twenty-point gap, though climate-and-energy-only and thus narrower than the full-scope Index. On every directly confirmed figure H&M scores higher than Zara/Inditex, consistent with the withholding pattern the coding identified independently. Two nuances complicate a simple "H&M is more transparent" gloss. First, H&M's reports cite its Fashion Revolution ranking as a competitive credential (Section 4.4), so the index is at once evidence and marketing material. Second, high disclosure and external challenge coexist: the Changing Markets Foundation's Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 report notes that H&M Group "has been reprimanded for its vague claims," "has been forced to remove 'sustainability' labelling," and "faces multiple legal cases," while identifying Inditex as reporting the second-highest disclosed volume of synthetic fibres in its sample — 178,030 tonnes, behind only Nike (Changing Markets Foundation, 2023). These advocacy-toned NGO observations, not adopted here as findings, illustrate Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) point that disclosure volume and contested claim quality are separate dimensions. Transparency indices reward disclosure; they do not adjudicate it.

The same logic appears at brand level. H&M's decade-long "Conscious" label was retired in 2022; the company said the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), disclosing no successor metric at retirement. This is coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE: sustained outside scrutiny — the market and normative pressure Delmas and Burbano (2011) count among greenwashing's principal external drivers — visibly altered a communication choice mid-program. Inditex's group-wide "Join Life" label, used across its brands including Zara, took a different path in the same year: the 2022 report states that 61% of products complied with the Join Life standard, ahead of the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187), and was not flagged under any greenwashing indicator. The contrast fits Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) finding that external scrutiny — present for Conscious, less evident for Join Life — disciplines selective disclosure, not disclosure volume or labeling activity alone: one firm pulled its label under pressure without naming a replacement; the other reported a target-beating figure without apparent challenge in the coded material.

### 5.3 Interaction Between Institutional and Competitive Pressures

Competitive signaling appears to layer onto institutional-pressure-driven disclosure during discretionary periods and recede when coercive structure binds — but only for one firm. H&M's competitive-framing and peer-benchmarking count rises from one coded row in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in FY2024, then falls back to one in the ESRS-structured 2025 report (Section 4.4). The peak coincides exactly with the CSRD-anticipation window of Section 5.1, when H&M's conformity was still rhetorical rather than architectural. This fits Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) evidence that anticipatory periods are the most discretionary and peer-oriented: the space accommodating "preparing to comply" language also accommodates differentiation claims, which the standardized format later crowds out. For Inditex, competitive-signal rows stay low and flat across all four years (two, two, one, two), with no spike around its 2024 ESRS transition (Section 4.4) — a stable background register, not a format-driven one.

The wage finding adds a second interaction mode: domain-specific insulation. Inditex's environmental disclosure was substantially restructured by the coercive ESRS transition, yet its Living Wages sub-section reports the identical reach-only metric structure on both sides of that transition (Sections 4.3, 4.4). Institutional pressure reorganized the domains it directly addressed while leaving the competitively sensitive wage domain untouched; the proprietary-cost logic (Lee & Lee, 2022) and the legitimacy-seeking logic operated side by side, as Chapter 2 anticipated: firms can converge on headline frameworks while guarding granular detail. Finally, normative infrastructure and competitive instruments are often the same artifacts used in different directions. H&M's competitive instances mostly anchor to independently verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP A score, a Textile Exchange "Leading" designation, a named comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 5, 20) — citing norm-carriers as rankings in which it performs well. Inditex's instances are more often self-referential benchmark-setting ambitions, such as framing Care for Water as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2025, p. 144): aspiring to author the norm rather than score against it. Neither register is mimetic in the institutional-theory sense (DiMaggio & Powell, 1983), and competitive language remains a secondary feature of both firms' communication (Section 4.4).

### 5.4 Theoretical Implications

For Institutional Theory, the case pair's principal contribution is a resolution-level correction: the substantive–symbolic distinction discriminates poorly at firm level but well at theme level, since neither company is uniformly one or the other and each shows a mirror-image cluster (Sections 4.4–4.5). This extends Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) firm-level mediation argument into intra-firm territory: whatever mediates institutional pressure appears to operate per theme, plausibly tracking each theme's measurement-infrastructure maturity — the reading most consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) sub-dimension-uneven decoupling. Studies that assign whole firms to conformity types risk averaging away this variation. The timing evidence adds a second refinement: anticipatory isomorphism (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023) describes H&M's rhetoric-then-structure sequence but not Inditex's structure-without-rhetoric one, suggesting the anticipatory-talk phase is contingent, not necessary.

For the decoupling and legitimacy literature, the recurrent MIXED pattern complicates the implicit association between decoupling and concealment. H&M's 2025 report itself states that its wage-management target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110), and both firms print unfavorable scope 3 and effluent-plant trends beside their favorable baselines (Sections 4.2, 4.4). Where Loko and Schiehll (2025) measure policy–practice misalignment as something researchers detect, this corpus shows firms increasingly *announcing* it — legitimacy management through disclosure incorporating calibrated candor, not only positive asymmetry, with no inference about motive. Where the codings fired they concerned emphasis, boundaries, denominators, and metric consistency, never fabricated evidence, supporting the position Chapter 2 distilled from Gatti et al. (2019) and Spaniol et al. (2024): the workable line between misleading and merely positive communication is evidentiary — substantiation, scope, comparability — not tonal. The selective-disclosure construct (Nemes et al., 2022; Bernini & La Rosa, 2024) also needs a procedural extension for the CSRD era, since omission produced upstream — through a materiality assessment that removes a topic from the reportable set — migrates selectivity from the text to the process that generates it. Finally, the wage finding corroborates Lee and Lee's (2022) proprietary-cost and Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) scrutiny-and-norms mechanisms in a paired-case setting, adding that the withholding was directional and category-specific, not general.

### 5.5 Practical Implications

For report preparers, the findings identify an inexpensive drafting discipline with outsized effect: comparator co-location. Inditex's climate units were coded disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024 but not in 2023 and 2025 — not because the data differed (the scope 3 figures were disclosed in all four years) but because of whether the unfavorable figure shared a sentence with the favorable one (Section 4.3); likewise, H&M's resale metric exited the emphasis-candidate category the year its turnover share appeared in the same sentence as the growth claim (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90). Placing the unfavorable or large-denominator figure adjacent to the headline claim, and attaching a scale denominator to every absolute figure, would eliminate most of the codings observed here without any change in performance.

For regulators and assurance providers, the ESRS transition shows a double edge. On one side, the standardized format coincided with new candor: H&M's 2025 report states explicitly which topics carry no time-bound targets (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118), admissions absent from the pre-ESRS reports. On the other, the materiality assessment becomes the critical control point: Inditex's exclusion of Affected Communities as non-material (Inditex, 2025, p. 121) is formally compliant behavior that nonetheless removes a previously reported theme from commitment-level disclosure. Scrutiny of materiality determinations — not only reported content — is therefore where review effort buys the most. The retirement of H&M's "Conscious" label, described by the company as "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), further indicates that consumer-facing green-claims enforcement registers in communication decisions. For investors, NGO raters, and other report users, the lessons are to read tables against narratives; to check which comparator a headline uses (year-on-year versus baseline, relative versus absolute); to treat outcome-named programs without outcome metrics — a "Living Wages" area reporting only reach counts — as a specific follow-up question; and to remember that transparency rankings measure disclosure, not performance, as the coexistence of H&M's high index scores with its own self-disclosed unfavorable trends illustrates.

### 5.6 Limitations and Future Research

The design-level limitations of Section 3.5 carry forward and can now be stated more precisely. Single-analyst coding weighs most on the codes this chapter leans on — the disproportional-emphasis test, the two MIXED sub-patterns, and the omission sub-tests all turn on judgment about sentence adjacency, comparator flattery, and internal consistency, which a second coder would most plausibly contest; the audit trail in the matrices mitigates but does not replace that check. Second, the NGO triangulation draws only on figures confirmed directly against the primary reports — H&M's Index scores for 2022 (66%) and 2023 (71%), both firms' What Fuels Fashion? 2024 scores (H&M 61%, Zara/Inditex 41%), and the Changing Markets Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 observations — a deliberately conservative evidence base that nonetheless points consistently in one direction. Third, several codings are explicitly provisional — the FY2024 resale emphasis instance, the Inditex 2025 water-denominator omission, and the H&M 2025 biodiversity decoupling flag. Fourth, comparability ceilings the analysis could not lift: offset fiscal years, and headline materials metrics definitionally non-comparable across firms (and, for Inditex's 2025 figure, not verifiably continuous), so no cross-firm headline-percentage comparison was drawn. Finally, document analysis cannot observe motive: the proprietary-cost reading of the wage gap is the most consistent with the pattern and the literature, but alternatives such as data-availability differences cannot be excluded, and no claim about intent is made.

These limitations translate into a research agenda. A second coder on the existing matrices, with formal inter-coder reliability on the emphasis and MIXED codes, would be the single most valuable robustness step. Verifying the remaining NGO index figures against primary documents would harden or retire the triangulation. Extending the window into the fully post-CSRD years would test two predictions this analysis generates: that H&M's competitive-framing count remains suppressed under the standardized format, and that Inditex's wage-disclosure gap either persists (proprietary-cost reading) or closes under widening value-chain obligations (regulatory-lag reading). Adding a third case outside the EU's coercive reach — or an ultra-fast online-only competitor — would separate what is CSRD-driven from what is sector- or firm-specific. And converting the disproportional-emphasis candidates into measured findings via a page- or word-share method, following Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) action-statement counting, would put them on firmer footing than the sentence-adjacency test alone.

In sum, coercive pressure was absorbed through two different sequences, responses proved substantive or symbolic theme by theme rather than firm by firm, and the selective-disclosure patterns proved consistent with proprietary-cost logic operating alongside, not instead of, legitimacy-seeking conformity. # 6 Conclusion

This thesis examined the credibility of fast fashion's environmental communication at its most consequential site: the published reporting of H&M Group and Inditex across 2022–2025, as European disclosure regulation shifted from a largely voluntary to an increasingly mandatory regime. Approaching that problem through Institutional Theory and a directed qualitative content analysis of the firms' own documents, the study posed two research questions, now answerable in the terms the coded evidence supports.

On RQ1, coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures are all identifiable, but the most instructive finding concerns how differently the same pressures were absorbed. Coercive pressure left the clearest trace, and its signature is timing: H&M Group absorbed the approaching CSRD/ESRS regime first as language — anticipatory regulatory rhetoric from 2022, a hedged "aligned with" framing by FY2024 — and only in 2025 as reporting architecture, whereas Inditex converted to the full ESRS structure a cycle earlier, in 2024, with little preceding rhetoric. Normative and mimetic pressures surface chiefly as convergence on shared voluntary frameworks such as SBTi-validated targets and field-standard terminology, rather than in the peer-ranking language the analysis assigned to competitive signaling. Neither firm is uniformly substantive or symbolic: the distinction discriminates at the level of disclosure themes, in near mirror-image patterns — H&M consistently quantified on climate, water, and materials yet without biodiversity targets through 2025 (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87), Inditex progressively quantified on biodiversity to 1.5 million hectares (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147) yet reach-only on living wages. Both responses are therefore best characterized as mixed conformity, resolved theme by theme rather than firm by firm.

On RQ2, the coding identified page-cited selective-disclosure patterns in both firms — disproportional emphasis where favorable small-denominator achievements were narrated apart from the larger unfavorable figure, omissions of scale denominators for circularity metrics, unclear claim boundaries, and inconsistent metrics where headline definitions changed without demonstrated continuity. Two qualifications apply: most emphasis findings concern placement rather than concealment, since the unfavorable figures were self-disclosed in the same documents, and the patterns were distributed across both firms. The exception is directional: across all four years, Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area reported only a people-reached count, never a wage-level or benchmark figure (Inditex, 2025, p. 178), while H&M published wage tables, an independent supplier pay-gap study, and a self-described coverage-rather-than-impact target; no comparable withholding ran in the other direction. This asymmetry, specific to competitively sensitive supply-chain wage data in reports that quantify most other themes in detail, is the finding most consistent with the proprietary-cost account of competitive pressure (Lee & Lee, 2022). It remains a finding about disclosure practice, not about wages actually paid, and no claim about either company's intent is made or implied.

The contribution follows directly. To Institutional Theory and greenwashing scholarship, it offers a resolution-level refinement — the substantive–symbolic classification performs poorly on whole firms and well on themes — plus evidence that anticipatory regulatory rhetoric (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023) is a contingent rather than necessary stage in how coercive pressure becomes reporting practice, and paired-case corroboration that selective disclosure can operate procedurally as well as textually, migrating into the materiality assessment that determines what is reported at all. To practice, it identifies comparator co-location and denominator attachment as inexpensive drafting disciplines that would have removed most observed codings, and directs regulatory and assurance attention toward materiality determinations rather than reported content alone.

These conclusions carry the limits appropriate to their design. A two-case qualitative study of self-authored documents, coded by a single analyst, supports analytic rather than statistical generalization; Section 5.6 details these constraints and the agenda they generate, including second-coder reliability checks and extension into the fully post-CSRD years. Within those bounds, the closing observation is modest but usable: over four years in which the rules changed around them, these firms' environmental communication proved analysable not by its tone but by its evidence — substantiation, scope, comparability — and it is on that evidentiary ground, not on impressions of sincerity or spin, that corporate sustainability claims can and should be judged.
