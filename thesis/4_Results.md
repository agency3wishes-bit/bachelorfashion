### 4.1 Overview of the Document Corpus

The findings draw on primary, company-published sustainability and annual reports for both firms across 2022–2025. For H&M Group: the Sustainability Disclosure 2022 and 2023, the Annual & Sustainability Report 2024 with its Sustainability Progress Report 2024, and the Annual & Sustainability Report 2025 (H&M Group, 2022; H&M Group, 2023; H&M Group, 2024; H&M Group, 2025). For Inditex: the sustainability sections of the Group Annual Report — the "Statement on Non-Financial Information" in 2022–2023, restructured as the "Consolidated Statement of Non-Financial Information and Sustainability Information" from 2024 (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023; Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025). Coding produced 237 units across ten themes common to both firms — climate and emissions, water, chemicals and pollution, biodiversity, materials and circular economy, waste, own workforce, workers in the value chain, communities, and consumers — 122 from H&M and 115 from Inditex.

Two structural features bear on the sections below. First, the firms report on non-identical fiscal calendars: H&M's runs 1 December to 30 November, Inditex's 1 February to 31 January, so a given "FY2024" denotes two windows roughly two months apart; same-year comparisons refer to each report's own covered period. Second, reporting architectures diverge from 2024: Inditex's 2024 and 2025 statements adopt the EU's European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS 2, E1–E5, S1–S4, G1), replacing the informal headings of 2022–2023, while H&M's reports anticipate future CSRD compliance without an ESRS-numbered structure. Per Methodology Section 3.3, external NGO/industry transparency-assessment data (the Fashion Transparency Index and Changing Markets Foundation reporting) was reserved for triangulation in Chapter 5 rather than coded here.

### 4.2 H&M Group

Across H&M's four disclosures, climate framing shifts alongside a recurring transparency pattern. The 2022 report frames H&M as anticipatorily "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded COERCIVE/NORMATIVE and SYMBOLIC (no date or metric attaches) (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16). By the FY2024 Progress Report the comparable unit states the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 2). On the figures, a MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern appears in the earliest and the FY2024 report: 2022 discloses a 9,270-tonne (+18%) year-on-year scope 1+2 increase alongside an 8% reduction against the 2019 baseline, attributed to the Russian wind-down (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses a 201,000-tonne (+3%) scope 3 increase alongside a 23.7% baseline reduction (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9). By 2025 year-on-year scope 3 reverses to a 13.6% decrease (34.6% against the 2019 baseline), coded SUBSTANTIVE (H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). The matrix also records a scope 3 baseline restatement on the same progress-versus-baseline KPI in 2022 and 2023 — from -9% to -6% and -7% to -15% — coded INCONSISTENT METRIC in 2022 but uncoded in 2023, where the report explains the change in the same passage (H&M Group, 2022, p. 28; H&M Group, 2023, pp. 20–21).

Materials and circular economy shows a recurring bundling pattern in the headline "recycled or sustainably sourced materials" percentage. In 2022 the 84% headline (23% recycled, remainder "other more sustainably sourced") is broken out only via a chart, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY (H&M Group, 2022, p. 43). The 2023 report breaks the same aggregate (85%) into 25% recycled and 60% sustainably-sourced within one table and is uncoded (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 38–39). By FY2024 the aggregate rises to 89% (29.5% recycled), again without an in-unit split and not formally coded (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 18–20); the 2025 aggregate (91%, 32% recycled) is likewise uncoded (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 90–91). A related denominator pattern appears in resale: 2022 gives no group-wide scale denominator for resale, rental, or repair, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses resale at 0.6% of turnover, flagged only as an unconfirmed DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS candidate (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 21–22); and 2025 states 0.8% of turnover in the same sentence as the growth claim, recorded as not meeting that trigger (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90).

Biodiversity is the thinnest theme in quantification. The 2022 and 2023 reports present regenerative-agriculture pilot data — farmer counts and tonnage targets for named projects — without a denominator connecting them to total sourcing volume, both coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33; H&M Group, 2023, p. 28). The FY2024 Progress Report states Biodiversity is one of only two sections (with Communities) reporting no quantified progress figure, its ambition statement coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17). The 2023 report separately discloses a CDP Forest score decline across all three tracked commodities (Cattle Products B- to C, Timber B to C, Palm Oil B to D), admitting "the decreased scores have not been fully analysed," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 29). By 2025 the report states no biodiversity-related targets exist for that year, adoption deferred to 2026, recorded as a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING candidate (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87).

Workers and living-wage findings show a recurring gap between wage data reported and living-wage or equity outcomes claimed. The 2022 report states H&M's Wage Strategy, pursued through its ACT (Action, Collaboration, Transformation) membership, "aims to deliver living wages," while the wage tables measure only average wages against the statutory minimum, coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION with a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING flag (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71). An independent gender-pay-gap study led by Professor Raymond Robertson, covering more than 1,300 production units, finds a "statistically significant pay gap between men and women in most... production countries" in 2022, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend), and in 2023 finds gaps "highest in Bangladesh and India" without disclosing magnitude, coded SUBSTANTIVE despite being unquantified (H&M Group, 2022, p. 71; H&M Group, 2023, p. 61). Tier-1 trade-union representation falls from 42% to 37% between 2022 and 2023, and 39% to 36% between 2024 and 2025, both self-disclosed alongside prior-year figures and coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 62; H&M Group, 2025, p. 110). The FY2024 wage-grid disclosure (290 factories) lacks the percentage-of-tier-1-suppliers denominator given for the adjacent enrolment figure in the same paragraph, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 31); by 2025 wage-management-system enrolment reaches 80% against a 100%-by-2028 target, and the report states the target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact," coded MEANS-ENDS DECOUPLING (self-disclosed) (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110).

Within water and chemicals, effluent-treatment-plant functionality at green grade declines across the three tracked years — 90% (2022), 89% (2023), 84.4% (FY2024) — each disclosed in the water performance table, the 2022 figure also stated in the narrative alongside an acknowledgement of "missing our goal of 100%," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) without meeting the text-anchored DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS trigger (H&M Group, 2022, p. 36; H&M Group, 2023, p. 25; H&M Group, 2024b, p. 15). The 2025 report discloses a re-scoped chemicals metric — 92.6% ZDHC Manufacturing Restricted Substances List assurance against a 95%-by-2030 target — while stating "these targets have not been validated by an external party," coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 82–83).

Consumers and communities findings include a rare 2022 instance of a company self-identifying an external challenge to a prior claim: the decade-long "Conscious" product-label program is retired, the report stating the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation," coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE and MIXED (scope-narrowed), as no successor metric or date is given (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14). The same report discloses a 21-percentage-point decline in stakeholder-rated transparency trust, from 71% to 50%, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2022, p. 14). By 2024 and 2025, the report states directly, under each topic's targets-and-actions subheading, that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets have been set for Consumers and end-users and Affected communities; the 2025 report extends this to Business conduct, whereas 2024 reports targets there on preventing corruption and bribery — recorded as POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING alongside the dated, quantified targets given for every environmental topic in the same reports (H&M Group, 2024a, pp. 102–103, 105; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118).

### 4.3 Inditex

Inditex's Statement on Non-Financial Information changes structure partway through the period: the 2022 and 2023 reports (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023) use company-defined "Committed to" headings with no ESRS cross-references, while the 2024 and 2025 reports (Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025) follow the CSRD-mandated ESRS structure — affecting which findings trace continuously and which appear only once the relevant ESRS chapter exists.

On climate, the 2022 Environment section opens with Inditex having "achieved our goal of 100% renewable electricity in our facilities" (Inditex, 2022, p. 197); the emissions chart two pages later shows Scope 1 and 2 combined at well under one percent of total emissions, Scope 3 the remainder (Inditex, 2022, p. 199), coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS: the favorable, small-denominator achievement is narrated without the large-denominator context, though tabulated nearby. The 2024 report repeats the pattern for the Scope 1+2 versus Scope 3 trajectory (down 87.7% versus down 1.3% from 2018), again coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS, the "Towards net-zero emissions" graphic foregrounding the Scope 1+2 trend while the full breakdown sits in a separate table (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163). By contrast, 2023 and 2025 place the unfavorable figure in the same sentence as the favorable: in 2023 the "scope 1 and 2 emissions by 97%" reduction and the target-relevant figure that "increased by 4%" appear together (Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194); in 2025 an 88% Scope 1+2 reduction and a 7% Scope 3 (SBT-relevant) reduction are reported together, "in both cases compared to 2018" (Inditex, 2025, p. 134) — both coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend). Self-consumption follows a related pattern: on-site generation is narrated with a rising figure in every report (7,756 MWh in 2022 to 11,506 MWh in 2025), while total energy consumption — roughly 1.6–1.7 million MWh throughout — appears elsewhere without the resulting share (under one percent every year) in the same passage (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2023, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2024, pp. 157, 165; Inditex, 2025, pp. 129, 132), coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022–2024 and DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS in 2025.

Inditex's headline materials metric changes label across the period. The 2022 "preferred" fibers and materials reach 60% of total use, with a footnote stating the company had "aligned the terminology used in our objectives with that currently being used by benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" while asserting "the meaning and ambition of our goals remains unchanged" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) — coded INCONSISTENT METRIC (self-disclosed realignment, not substantiated with a like-for-like conversion). The same page reports a separate metric: 61% of products met Inditex's group-wide Join Life standard, against the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187); this figure was not flagged and is distinct from the INCONSISTENT METRIC coding, which concerns the adjacent "preferred fibres" realignment. The 2023 report retains the "preferred raw materials" label at 68% (Inditex, 2023, pp. 236–237), while 2024 and 2025 relabel the metric "lower-impact fibres," at 73% (Inditex, 2024, p. 200) and 88% (Inditex, 2025, p. 150); the 2025 table gives only current- and target-year columns, and definitional continuity with 2024 was not verified, so no cross-year comparison is drawn. Within "preferred cotton," the largest single component in 2022 and 2023 is Better Cotton, footnoted in both years as a mass-balance calculation "not physically traceable to the final product" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 188–189; Inditex, 2023, pp. 238–239) — coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022, not coded in 2023 (the caveat sits in a footnote attached to the figure). The Clothing Collection Programme's recovered tonnage recurs every year — 17,015 (2022), 20,259 (2023), 19,484 (2024), 20,495 tonnes (2025) — each with a reuse/recycling split but no denominator expressing tonnage as a share of garments placed on the market (Inditex, 2022, pp. 192–193; Inditex, 2023, p. 243; Inditex, 2024, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2025, pp. 150–152). Coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test in 2022, 2023, and 2025; in 2024 it was treated as a cross-case comparability gap against H&M's percentage-of-turnover resale disclosure.

Biodiversity shows the clearest quantified trajectory. The 2022 report frames biodiversity action under the Science Based Targets Network's "Avoid, Reduce, Restore, Regenerate and Transform" (AR3T) framework and names certifications and restoration projects, but discloses no Group-wide hectare or aggregate metric, unlike the numeric targets given for climate, water, and materials in the same report; coded SYMBOLIC and WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211). The only quantified area that year is a regenerative-agriculture partnership in India spanning 300,000 hectares, without a statement of what share of total sourcing it represents (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–210). The 2023 report introduces a dated, quantified Group-wide target — protect, restore, regenerate, or otherwise improve biodiversity across five million hectares by 2030 — but discloses no cumulative progress figure, the same India partnership remaining the only area figure (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), coded OMISSION. From 2024 the target is tracked with a current-year figure: 758,534 hectares (622,878 restored plus 135,656 regenerated), a sum the coding matrix verified reconciles (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192); by 2025 it reaches 1.5 million hectares (1,107,098 restored plus 398,217 regenerated), again reconciling (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Both units are coded SUBSTANTIVE with no greenwashing indicator.

Within the "Workers at the Centre" strategy, the Living Wages Priority Impact Area shows a consistent pattern across all four years. In 2022 the sub-section names country-specific projects — the ACT collaboration in Bangladesh, Cambodia, and Türkiye; LEAN; SCORE; HERfinance — but discloses no wage-level figure, living-wage-benchmark gap, or even a people-reached figure (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228), coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE decoupling. From 2023 a people-reached figure appears but is consistently the smallest of the strategy's five Priority Impact Areas: 31,318 in 2023, against Health's 1,125,665 (Inditex, 2023, pp. 289–290); 86,268 in 2024, against Health's 1,313,217 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262); 120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028 (Inditex, 2025, p. 178). No wage-level, benchmark, or wage-gap metric appears in any year. The 2023 and 2024 units are coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE (candidate); the 2025 coding records the same reach-only pattern for a second consecutive year as a more confirmed cross-year pattern, noting it concerns disclosure practice, not whether suppliers pay living wages.

On water, the 2022 report discloses both an absolute water-consumption figure and two intensity denominators (240 litres/m² and 55 ml/EUR) for its own facilities (Inditex, 2022, pp. 206–207), a level of denomination that year's Waste sub-section does not match. A related asymmetry appears in 2025 between the Water and Energy chapters: Energy discloses an absolute total plus intensity metrics, while Water reports only a relative intensity metric (litres per kilogram of garment), stating "the amount of water consumed is very low" and treating withdrawal as more representative without an absolute volume (Inditex, 2025, pp. 142–143) — coded a provisional OMISSION candidate, pending verification against the report's unread Annex Methodology section. On communities, the 2025 materiality assessment states "the identified potential impacts and risks linked to ESRS 'S3 - Affected communities' are not material for Inditex" (Inditex, 2025, p. 121); community content is not absent but appears as narrative color across the biodiversity, materials, and workers-in-the-value-chain chapters, without a dedicated metric framework (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148, 151, 175, 179). This exclusion is coded OMISSION under the cross-case topic-omission sub-test.

### 4.4 Cross-Case Comparison

The clearest recurring pattern in both firms' climate disclosure is a structural separation between scope 1+2 emissions, consistently framed with SUBSTANTIVE, SBTi-validated targets, and scope 3 emissions, the large majority of each footprint and on a more uneven trajectory. H&M reports a stable MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern for scope 3 in every coded year: +18% attributed to the Russia exit in 2022, a further +3% attributed to material weight and airfreight in the 2024 Progress Report, and a reversal to -13.6% in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). Inditex shows a narrower, intermittent version: a DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS instance in 2022, where a headline scope 1+2/renewable-electricity figure is stated narratively while the scope 3 figure, present in a chart, is not in the same or adjacent sentence (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199), and a second in 2024 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 162–163). The test is not met in 2023 or 2025, where the favorable and unfavorable figures share a sentence (Inditex, 2023, pp. 190–191, 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). In both firms the scope 3 figures are self-disclosed rather than absent.

A second contrast concerns Living Wages, a theme both firms name after a wage outcome but document with markedly different metrics. H&M's 2022 disclosure pairs an ACT/living-wage aim with a minimum-versus-statutory-wage gap table that is not itself a living-wage benchmark (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71), while its 2024 Progress Report reports a narrow but concrete figure — a seven-factory Mainland China gender-pay-gap reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside an admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 29, 31). By 2025 H&M introduces a coverage-based Wage Management System target (80% enrolment in 2025, 100% by 2028) described by the company as focused on "programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110). Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area reports zero wage-level, living-wage-benchmark, or wage-gap metrics in any of the four coded years, disclosing only a "people reached" count rising to 86,268 in 2024 and 120,050 in 2025 — consistently the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas, well below the 2,159,028 reported for Health in the same year (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). Of the corpus patterns, this most consistently meets the cross-case PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING test: a topic named after a wage outcome, reported with a reach figure but never a wage-level figure, across three consecutive Inditex years, in a document quantifying most other themes to a comparable or greater level of detail. The finding is specific to supply-chain wage data; both firms separately report comparable gender-pay-gap percentages for their own direct workforce, so the asymmetry is not a general wage-transparency gap.

A third contrast concerns Biodiversity, where the two firms reach markedly different quantification over the same four years. H&M's disclosure remains SYMBOLIC throughout: an "aim to make time-bound commitments in 2023" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34), a 2023 statement that a target is "still in the process of being developed" (H&M Group, 2023, p. 27), a 2024 Progress Report in which Biodiversity is one of only two sections carrying no quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17), and a 2025 statement that the company has "not yet set biodiversity-related targets" and intends to adopt them "in 2026" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex's follows a different trajectory: a quantified 2030 target of 5 million hectares present from 2022 (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211), without a Group-level current-year progress figure through 2023 (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), before becoming fully quantified and internally arithmetic-consistent — 758,534 hectares in 2024 and 1.5 million in 2025, anchored to the Science Based Targets Network AR3T framework (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). This is a disclosure-practice asymmetry, not evidence about ecological outcomes.

A fourth divergence appears in the Communities theme in 2025. H&M's 2024 Progress Report lists Communities as one of only two sections without a quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 32), and its 2025 report states the company has "not set any time-bound and outcome-oriented targets related to affected communities" (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112), retaining the topic but attaching no target — a SYMBOLIC target status. Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment concludes instead that "S3 - Affected communities... are not material for Inditex," removing Communities from its material-topic list (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both reduce commitment-level disclosure relative to earlier years but take structurally different forms — a retained, target-less topic at H&M, a materiality-based exclusion at Inditex — that the coding treats as distinct.

Finally, the firms differ in how frequently and how they anchor competitive or peer-benchmarking language. Rows carrying a COMPETITIVE FRAMING or PEER BENCHMARKING tag appear nine times across H&M's four reports against seven across Inditex's, with H&M's count rising from one in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in the 2024 Progress Report before falling to one in the ESRS-structured 2025 report; Inditex's stays flat (two, two, one, two). H&M's instances are more often anchored to verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP climate score of A in the 2024 Progress Report (A− in 2023), a Fashion Revolution Transparency Index rank, a Textile Exchange Material Change Index "Leading" designation (one of six overall leaders of 130 companies in 2023, and one of just 11 to reach "Leading" in 2024), and one direct named-competitor comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 5, 20). Inditex's comparable rows are more often self-referential leadership statements without a named external comparator — on-site self-consumption capacity as an achievement, and Care for Water framed as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2025, p. 144) — with a single exception, an IPE ranking citing the company as "third... globally and in the textile sector" (Inditex, 2023, p. 229). In absolute terms, competitive-framing language remains a secondary feature of both firms' communication.

The cross-case coding also produced one explicit negative result relevant to RQ2: no category of granular data disclosed by Inditex was found conspicuously and consistently absent from H&M's coverage of the same topic. Candidates considered and rejected include hectare-level biodiversity project reporting and named community-investment figures; in each case H&M's own reporting discloses the same underlying information, if in a differently presented or less consolidated form (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148; H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33, 81; Inditex, 2023, pp. 234–235; Inditex, 2025, p. 176). The withholding asymmetries therefore run in one direction only, from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds, most clearly on supply-chain wage data; no comparably supported finding runs the other way.

### 4.5 Summary of Findings

Across the ten disclosure themes coded for both firms over 2022–2025, Sections 4.2 through 4.4 present structural divergence and uneven emphasis rather than a single shared pattern. Both firms' climate reporting shares one structural feature: scope 1 and 2 emissions tracked against SBTi-validated targets coded substantive, while scope 3, the larger share of each footprint, follows a more uneven trajectory. For H&M this recurs as a self-disclosed unfavorable trend across most coded years, reversing only in 2025; for Inditex it surfaces intermittently as disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024 but not in 2023 or 2025, where the favorable and unfavorable figures share a sentence.

On materials and circular economy, both firms report rising headline percentages for recycled or lower-impact materials, and both show a recurring gap between resale, rental, repair, or collection tonnage and any denominator connecting it to overall production or sales volume. Biodiversity diverges more sharply: H&M's reporting remains symbolic and without a quantified group-wide target throughout, while Inditex introduces a dated, quantified hectare target in 2022 and reports increasingly reconciled progress figures from 2024. Within the workers-focused themes, both name living-wage ambitions but neither discloses a wage-level or living-wage-benchmark figure in any coded year: H&M reports statutory-wage comparisons and, from 2024, a coverage-based enrolment target described as focused on program coverage rather than impact, while Inditex reports a rising but comparatively small people-reached figure that remains the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas. The Communities theme diverges further in 2025, where H&M retains the topic without time-bound targets and Inditex excludes it from its material-topic list.

Taken together, the coding identifies patterns traceable to specific documents, years, and pages rather than a single overall label for either firm. With respect to RQ1, both firms' coded material contains a mixture of substantive and symbolic responses rather than one uniform response type across themes and years. With respect to RQ2, the cross-case matrices identify page-cited selective-disclosure patterns — omission, disproportional emphasis, unclear boundary, and inconsistent metric codings — in both firms' reporting, alongside one identified withholding asymmetry running from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds on supply-chain wage data, with no comparably supported instance in the other direction.
