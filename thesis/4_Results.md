### 4.1 Overview of the Document Corpus

The findings presented in this chapter are drawn from a corpus of primary, company-published sustainability and annual reporting documents covering both case firms across the full 2022–2025 study period. For H&M Group, the corpus comprises the Sustainability Disclosure 2022, the Sustainability Disclosure 2023, the Annual & Sustainability Report 2024 together with the accompanying Sustainability Progress Report 2024, and the Annual & Sustainability Report 2025 (H&M Group, 2022; H&M Group, 2023; H&M Group, 2024; H&M Group, 2025). For Inditex, the corpus comprises the sustainability sections of the Inditex Group Annual Report for each of the four years, titled the "Statement on Non-Financial Information" in 2022 and 2023 and restructured as the "Consolidated Statement of Non-Financial Information and Sustainability Information" from 2024 onward (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023; Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025). Across the two firms and four fiscal years, coding produced 237 coded units distributed across ten disclosure themes common to both companies' reporting structures: climate and emissions, water, chemicals and pollution, biodiversity, materials and circular economy, waste, own workforce, workers in the value chain, communities, and consumers. Of these 237 units, 122 were drawn from the H&M Group corpus and 115 from the Inditex corpus.

Two structural features of the corpus bear directly on how the sections that follow should be read and are therefore restated here rather than left implicit. First, the two firms report on non-identical fiscal-year calendars: H&M Group's fiscal year runs from 1 December to 30 November, labeled by the year in which most of that period falls, whereas Inditex's runs from 1 February to 31 January, labeled by the year in which it begins. A given "FY2024" accordingly denotes two different twelve-month windows roughly two months apart, and same-numbered-year comparisons in this chapter refer to each report's own stated covered period rather than to a shared calendar year. Second, the two firms' reporting architectures diverge from 2024 onward: Inditex's 2024 and 2025 statements are organized around the EU's European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS 2, E1–E5, S1–S4, G1), replacing the informally organized subsection headings used in its 2022 and 2023 statements, while H&M Group's reports across the same window contain narrative language anticipating future CSRD compliance without adopting an equivalent ESRS-numbered structure. This asymmetry in the timing and form of structural change is a documented feature of the corpus itself, distinct from the pressure- and response-level findings reported in Sections 4.2 through 4.4. One further scoping note applies to this chapter as a whole: per the data-collection design set out in Methodology Section 3.3, one category of material — external NGO/industry transparency-assessment data (the Fashion Transparency Index and the Changing Markets Foundation's reporting) — was deliberately reserved for triangulation in Chapter 5's discussion rather than coded into the primary matrices reported here, since it originates outside the two firms' own disclosures and serves an interpretive rather than a within-corpus descriptive function.

### 4.2 H&M Group

Across H&M Group's four sustainability disclosures (2022–2025), climate and emissions reporting shows a shift in institutional-pressure framing alongside a recurring transparency pattern in the underlying data. The 2022 report frames H&M as anticipatorily "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded COERCIVE/NORMATIVE and scored SYMBOLIC because no date or metric attaches to the language (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16). By the FY2024 Progress Report, the comparable unit instead states the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY because the claim's scope is not tied to any specific standard-level datapoint (H&M Group, 2024, p. 2). On the underlying emissions figures, a MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern appears in both the earliest and the FY2024 report: the 2022 report discloses a 9,270-tonne (+18%) year-on-year scope 1+2 increase in the same passage as an 8% reduction against the 2019 baseline, attributed to the wind-down of Russian operations (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses a 201,000-tonne (+3%) scope 3 increase alongside a 23.7% reduction against baseline (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 8–9). By 2025 the year-on-year scope 3 figure reverses to a 13.6% decrease (34.6% against the 2019 baseline), coded SUBSTANTIVE rather than MIXED (H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). The matrix also records a comparable-magnitude scope 3 baseline restatement for the same named progress-versus-baseline KPI in both 2022 and 2023 — from -9% to -6% and from -7% to -15% respectively — coded differently: the 2022 restatement as INCONSISTENT METRIC, the 2023 one left uncoded because the report explains the methodology change in the same passage (H&M Group, 2022, p. 28; H&M Group, 2023, pp. 20–21).

The materials and circular-economy theme shows a recurring bundling pattern in H&M's headline "recycled or sustainably sourced materials" percentage. In 2022, the 84% headline figure (23% recycled, the remainder "other more sustainably sourced") is broken out only via an accompanying chart rather than in the same sentence, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY (H&M Group, 2022, p. 43). The 2023 report discloses the same style of aggregate (85%) broken into its 25% recycled and 60% sustainably-sourced components within the same data table and is explicitly left uncoded on this indicator (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 38–39). By FY2024 the aggregate rises to 89% (29.5% recycled), again reported without an in-unit split, though the row was not formally coded with a greenwashing indicator (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 18–20); the 2025 aggregate (91%, 32% recycled) was likewise left uncoded (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 90–91). A related denominator pattern appears in the circular-customer-journey/resale metric: the 2022 report gives no group-wide scale denominator anywhere for resale, rental, or repair activity, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses resale at 0.6% of turnover, flagged only as a DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS candidate pending a page/word-share method, not a confirmed instance (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 21–22); and the 2025 report states 0.8% of turnover in the same sentence as the growth claim, explicitly recorded as not meeting that trigger (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90).

Biodiversity is coded as the thinnest theme across all four years in terms of quantification. The 2022 and 2023 reports present regenerative-agriculture pilot data — farmer counts and tonnage targets for named projects — without a denominator connecting them to H&M's total sourcing volume, both coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33; H&M Group, 2023, p. 28). The FY2024 Progress Report states that Biodiversity is one of only two thematic sections (with Communities) that report no quantified progress figure at all, and its narrative ambition statement is itself coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 16–17). The 2023 report separately discloses a CDP Forest score decline across all three tracked commodities (Cattle Products B- to C, Timber B to C, Palm Oil B to D), stated plainly with an admission that "the decreased scores have not been fully analysed," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 29). By 2025, the report states directly that no biodiversity-related targets exist for that year, with adoption deferred to 2026 — self-disclosed rather than embedded in unqualified ambition language, and recorded on the decoupling axis as a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING candidate (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87).

Workers and living-wage-adjacent findings show a recurring gap between wage data reported and living-wage or equity outcomes claimed. The 2022 report states that H&M's Wage Strategy, pursued through its ACT (Action, Collaboration, Transformation) membership, "aims to deliver living wages," while the detailed wage tables that follow measure only average wages against the statutory minimum wage, coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION with a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING flag (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71). An independent gender-pay-gap study led by Professor Raymond Robertson, covering more than 1,300 production units, finds a "statistically significant pay gap between men and women in most... production countries" in 2022, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend), and in 2023 finds gaps "highest in Bangladesh and India" without disclosing the gap's magnitude, coded SUBSTANTIVE despite the unquantified finding (H&M Group, 2022, p. 71; H&M Group, 2023, p. 61). Freedom-of-association metrics decline in both periods where comparably tracked: tier-1 trade-union representation falls from 42% to 37% between 2022 and 2023, and from 39% to 36% between 2024 and 2025, both self-disclosed in the same table as the prior-year figures and coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 62; H&M Group, 2025, p. 110). The FY2024 wage-grid disclosure (290 factories) lacks the percentage-of-tier-1-suppliers denominator given for the adjacent wage-management-system enrolment figure in the same paragraph, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2024, p. 31); by 2025, wage-management-system enrolment reaches 80% against a 100%-by-2028 target, and the report itself states the target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact," coded MEANS-ENDS DECOUPLING (self-disclosed) (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110).

Within the water and chemicals theme, effluent-treatment-plant functionality assessed at green grade declines across the three years for which the metric is tracked — 90% (2022), 89% (2023), 84.4% (FY2024) — each figure disclosed in the water performance data table, the 2022 figure also stated in the narrative alongside an explicit acknowledgement of "missing our goal of 100%," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) without meeting the text-anchored DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS trigger (H&M Group, 2022, p. 36; H&M Group, 2023, p. 25; H&M Group, 2024, p. 15). The 2025 report discloses a related, re-scoped chemicals metric — 92.6% ZDHC Manufacturing Restricted Substances List assurance against a 95%-by-2030 target — while stating that "these targets have not been validated by an external party," coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 82–83).

Consumers and communities findings include a rare instance, in the 2022 report, of a company self-identifying an external challenge to a prior claim: the decade-long "Conscious" product-label program is retired, with the report stating the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation," coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE and MIXED (scope-narrowed), since no successor metric or date is given for the replacement approach (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14). The same report discloses a 21-percentage-point decline in stakeholder-rated transparency trust, from 71% to 50%, stated in the same sentence as the prior-year comparator, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2022, p. 14). By 2024 and 2025, H&M's own report structure states directly, under each topic's targets-and-actions subheading, that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets have been set for Consumers and end-users and Affected communities; the 2025 report extends this to Business conduct as well, whereas the 2024 report instead reports targets for that topic related to preventing corruption and bribery — recorded on the decoupling axis as POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING and noted alongside the dated, quantified targets given for every environmental topic read in the same reports (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 102–103, 105; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118).

### 4.3 Inditex

Inditex's Statement on Non-Financial Information changes structure partway through the study period: the 2022 and 2023 reports (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023) are organized under company-defined "Committed to" headings with no ESRS cross-references, while the 2024 and 2025 reports (Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025) follow the CSRD-mandated ESRS structure — a shift that affects which findings trace continuously across all four years and which appear only once the relevant ESRS chapter exists.

On climate and emissions, the 2022 Environment section opens with the statement that Inditex "achieved our goal of 100% renewable electricity in our facilities" (Inditex, 2022, p. 197); the accompanying emissions chart, two pages later, shows Scope 1 and Scope 2 combined at well under one percent of total emissions, with Scope 3 accounting for the remainder (Inditex, 2022, p. 199). This unit was coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS: the favorable, small-denominator achievement is narrated without the large-denominator context in the same or an adjacent sentence, though that context is tabulated nearby. The 2024 report shows the same pattern for the Scope 1+2 versus Scope 3 trajectory (down 87.7% versus down 1.3% from 2018), again coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS, with the "Towards net-zero emissions" graphic foregrounding the Scope 1+2 trend while the full breakdown sits in a separate table (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163). By contrast, the 2023 and 2025 reports place the unfavorable figure in the same sentence as the favorable one: in 2023, the "scope 1 and 2 emissions by 97%" reduction and the target-relevant emissions figure that "increased by 4%" appear in one sentence (Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194); in 2025, an 88% Scope 1+2 reduction and a 7% Scope 3 (SBT-relevant) reduction are reported together, "in both cases compared to 2018" (Inditex, 2025, p. 134). Both are coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) rather than DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS, since the operational test requires the unfavorable figure to be absent from the same or adjacent sentence. A related pattern recurs around self-consumption: on-site generation is narrated with a rising year-over-year figure in every report (7,756 MWh in 2022 to 11,506 MWh in 2025), while total energy consumption — roughly 1.6–1.7 million MWh throughout — appears elsewhere without the resulting share (under one percent every year) stated in the same passage (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2023, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2024, pp. 157, 165; Inditex, 2025, pp. 129, 132), coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022–2024 and DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS in 2025.

Inditex's headline materials metric also changes label across the period. The 2022 report's "preferred" fibers and materials reach 60% of total use, with a footnote stating the company had "aligned the terminology used in our objectives with that currently being used by benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" while asserting "the meaning and ambition of our goals remains unchanged" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) — coded INCONSISTENT METRIC because the realignment is self-disclosed but not substantiated with a like-for-like conversion. The same page reports a second, separate materials metric: 61% of products met Inditex's group-wide Join Life sustainability standard, against the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187). This figure was not flagged under any greenwashing indicator; it is analytically distinct from the INCONSISTENT METRIC coding above, which concerns the adjacent "preferred fibres" terminology realignment, not the Join Life compliance figure itself. The 2023 report retains the "preferred raw materials" label at 68% (Inditex, 2023, pp. 236–237), while the 2024 and 2025 reports relabel the metric "lower-impact fibres," at 73% (Inditex, 2024, p. 200) and 88% (Inditex, 2025, p. 150); the 2025 table gives only current- and target-year columns, and definitional continuity with the 2024 figure was not verified, so no cross-year comparison is drawn here. Within "preferred cotton," the largest single component in 2022 and 2023 is Better Cotton, footnoted in both years as a mass-balance calculation "not physically traceable to the final product" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 188–189; Inditex, 2023, pp. 238–239) — coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022, not coded in 2023, since the caveat sits in a footnote attached to the figure it qualifies. The Clothing Collection Programme's recovered tonnage recurs every year — 17,015 (2022), 20,259 (2023), 19,484 (2024), 20,495 tonnes (2025) — each year reporting a reuse/recycling split but no denominator expressing tonnage as a share of garments placed on the market (Inditex, 2022, pp. 192–193; Inditex, 2023, p. 243; Inditex, 2024, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2025, pp. 150–152). This was coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test in 2022, 2023, and 2025; in 2024 it was treated instead as a cross-case comparability gap against H&M's percentage-of-turnover resale disclosure, since no comparable same-company denominator was found that year.

Biodiversity shows the clearest quantified trajectory of the themes examined. The 2022 report frames biodiversity action under the Science Based Targets Network's "Avoid, Reduce, Restore, Regenerate and Transform" (AR3T) framework and names specific certifications and restoration projects, but discloses no Group-wide hectare or other aggregate metric, unlike the numeric targets given for climate, water, and materials in the same report; coded SYMBOLIC and WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211). The only quantified area given that year is a regenerative-agriculture partnership in India spanning 300,000 hectares, without a statement of what share of Inditex's total sourcing footprint this represents (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–210). The 2023 report introduces a dated, quantified Group-wide target — to protect, restore, regenerate, or otherwise improve biodiversity across five million hectares by 2030 — but discloses no cumulative progress figure against it, with the same India partnership remaining the only area figure given (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), coded OMISSION. From 2024 the target is tracked with a current-year figure: 758,534 hectares, reported as 622,878 restored plus 135,656 regenerated, a sum the coding matrix verified reconciles arithmetically (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192); by 2025 the figure reaches 1.5 million hectares (1,107,098 restored plus 398,217 regenerated), again reconciling (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Both units are coded SUBSTANTIVE with no greenwashing indicator applied.

Within the "Workers at the Centre" strategy, the Living Wages Priority Impact Area shows a consistent pattern across all four years. In 2022, the full sub-section names country-specific projects — the ACT collaboration in Bangladesh, Cambodia, and Türkiye; the LEAN project; SCORE; HERfinance — but discloses no wage-level figure, living-wage-benchmark gap, or even a people-reached figure (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228), coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE decoupling. From 2023 a people-reached figure appears, but it is consistently the smallest of the strategy's five Priority Impact Areas: 31,318 people in 2023, against Health's 1,125,665 (Inditex, 2023, pp. 289–290); 86,268 in 2024, against Health's 1,313,217 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262); and 120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028 (Inditex, 2025, p. 178). No wage-level, benchmark, or wage-gap metric appears in any year's Living Wages sub-section. The 2023 and 2024 units are coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE (candidate); the 2025 coding records the same reach-only pattern for a second consecutive year and treats the recurrence as supporting a more confirmed cross-year pattern, while stating that the coded pattern concerns disclosure practice, not a claim about whether Inditex's suppliers pay living wages.

On water, the 2022 report discloses both an absolute water-consumption figure and two intensity denominators (240 litres/m² and 55 ml/EUR) for its own facilities (Inditex, 2022, pp. 206–207), a level of denomination that year's Waste sub-section does not match. A related asymmetry appears in 2025 between the Water and Energy chapters: Energy discloses an absolute total plus intensity metrics, while Water reports only a relative intensity metric (litres per kilogram of garment), stating that "the amount of water consumed is very low" and treating withdrawal as the more representative metric without an absolute volume (Inditex, 2025, pp. 142–143) — coded a provisional OMISSION candidate, pending verification against the report's unread Annex Methodology section. On communities, the 2025 report's own materiality assessment states that "the identified potential impacts and risks linked to ESRS 'S3 - Affected communities' are not material for Inditex" (Inditex, 2025, p. 121); community content is not entirely absent but appears as narrative color distributed across the biodiversity, materials, and workers-in-the-value-chain chapters, without a dedicated metric framework of its own (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148, 151, 175, 179). This exclusion is coded OMISSION under the cross-case topic-omission sub-test, pending comparison against H&M's 2025 materiality structure.

### 4.4 Cross-Case Comparison

Across the themes synthesised in the cross-case coding, the clearest recurring pattern in both companies' climate disclosure is a structural separation between scope 1+2 emissions, consistently framed with SUBSTANTIVE, SBTi-validated targets, and scope 3 emissions, which account for the large majority of each firm's footprint and follow a more uneven trajectory. H&M reports a stable MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern for scope 3 in every coded year: a +18% year-over-year increase attributed to the Russia exit in 2022, a further +3% increase attributed to material weight and airfreight in the 2024 Progress Report, and a reversal to -13.6% in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). Inditex shows a narrower, more intermittent version of the same imbalance: a DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS instance in 2022, where a headline scope 1+2/renewable-electricity figure is stated narratively while the corresponding scope 3 figure, though present in an accompanying chart, is not stated in the same or an adjacent sentence (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199), and a second such instance in 2024 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 162–163). The same test is not met in 2023 or 2025, where the favorable and unfavorable scope figures appear within the same sentence (Inditex, 2023, pp. 190–191, 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). In both firms' coded material the scope 3 figures are self-disclosed rather than absent, a relevant qualification when reading either company's climate framing.

A second cross-case contrast concerns Living Wages, a theme both companies name after a wage outcome but document with markedly different metrics. H&M's 2022 disclosure pairs an ACT/living-wage aim with a minimum-wage-versus-statutory-wage gap table that does not itself constitute a living-wage benchmark (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71), while its 2024 Progress Report reports a narrow but concrete figure — a seven-factory Mainland China gender-pay-gap reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside an admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 29, 31). By 2025, H&M introduces a coverage-based Wage Management System target (80% enrolment in 2025, 100% by 2028) described by the company itself as focused on "programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110). Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area, by contrast, reports zero wage-level, living-wage-benchmark, or wage-gap metrics in any of the four coded years, disclosing only a "people reached" count rising to 86,268 in 2024 and 120,050 in 2025 — consistently the smallest of Inditex's five named Priority Impact Areas, well below the 2,159,028 people reported for Health in the same year (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). Of the patterns identified across the corpus, this one most consistently meets the cross-case PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING test: a topic named after a wage outcome, reported with a reach figure but never a wage-level figure, across three consecutive Inditex reporting years, in a document that quantifies most other themes to a comparable or greater level of detail. The finding is specific to supply-chain wage data; both companies separately report comparable gender-pay-gap percentages for their own direct workforce, so the asymmetry should not be read as a general wage-transparency gap.

A third contrast concerns Biodiversity, where the two companies reach markedly different levels of quantification over the same four years. H&M's Biodiversity disclosure remains SYMBOLIC throughout the corpus: an "aim to make time-bound commitments in 2023" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34), a 2023 statement that a target is "still in the process of being developed" (H&M Group, 2023, p. 27), a 2024 Progress Report in which Biodiversity is one of only two thematic sections carrying no quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024, pp. 16–17), and a 2025 statement that the company has "not yet set biodiversity-related targets" and intends to adopt them "in 2026" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex's disclosure follows a different trajectory: a quantified 2030 target of 5 million hectares to protect, restore, or regenerate is present from at least 2022 (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211), without a Group-level current-year progress figure through 2023 (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), before becoming fully quantified and internally arithmetic-consistent — 758,534 hectares in 2024 and 1.5 million hectares in 2025, anchored to the Science Based Targets Network AR3T framework (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). This is a disclosure-practice asymmetry, not evidence about underlying ecological outcomes at either company.

A fourth divergence appears in the Communities theme in the 2025 reporting cycle. H&M's 2024 Progress Report lists Communities as one of only two sections without a quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024, p. 32), and its 2025 report states that the company has "not set any time-bound and outcome-oriented targets related to affected communities" (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112), retaining the topic in its reporting but attaching no commitment-level target to it — a SYMBOLIC target status. Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment, conducted under its ESRS structure, concludes instead that "S3 - Affected communities... are not material for Inditex," removing Communities from its material-topic list rather than retaining it as an unmet-target theme (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both patterns reduce commitment-level disclosure for this theme relative to earlier reporting years, but take structurally different forms — a retained, target-less topic at H&M, and a materiality-based exclusion at Inditex — that the coding treats as distinct rather than equivalent.

Finally, the two companies differ in how frequently and how they anchor competitive or peer-benchmarking language. Rows carrying a COMPETITIVE FRAMING or PEER BENCHMARKING tag appear nine times across H&M's four reports against seven times across Inditex's, with H&M's count rising from one row in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in the 2024 Progress Report before falling back to one in the fully ESRS-structured 2025 report; Inditex's count stays comparatively flat (two, two, one, two). H&M's instances are more often anchored to independently verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP climate score of A in the 2024 Progress Report (A− in 2023), a Fashion Revolution Transparency Index rank, a Textile Exchange Material Change Index "Leading" designation (naming the company one of six overall leaders of 130 participating companies in 2023, and one of just 11 companies to reach "Leading" status in 2024), and one direct named-competitor comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 5, 20). Inditex's comparable rows are more often self-referential leadership or benchmark-setting statements without a named external comparator — on-site self-consumption capacity described as an achievement, and Care for Water framed as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2025, p. 144) — with a single exception, an IPE ranking citing the company as "third... globally and in the textile sector" (Inditex, 2023, p. 229). In absolute terms, competitive-framing language remains a secondary feature of both companies' communication relative to the size of each year's coded material.

The cross-case coding also produced one explicit negative result relevant to RQ2: no category of granular data disclosed by Inditex was found conspicuously and consistently absent from H&M's coverage of the same topic. Candidates considered and rejected include hectare-level biodiversity project reporting and named community-investment figures for specific initiatives; in each case H&M's own reporting discloses the same underlying information, if in a differently presented or less consolidated form (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148; H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33, 81; Inditex, 2023, pp. 234–235; Inditex, 2025, p. 176). The withholding asymmetries identified in this corpus therefore run in one direction only, from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds, most clearly on supply-chain wage data; no comparably supported finding runs the other way.

### 4.5 Summary of Findings

Across the ten disclosure themes coded for both firms over 2022–2025, the picture that emerges from Sections 4.2 through 4.4 is one of structural divergence and uneven emphasis rather than a single pattern shared uniformly by H&M Group and Inditex. Both companies' climate and emissions reporting shares one structural feature: scope 1 and scope 2 emissions are tracked against SBTi-validated targets coded substantive, while scope 3 emissions, the larger share of each firm's footprint, follow a more uneven trajectory. For H&M this recurs as a self-disclosed unfavorable trend across most coded years, reversing only in 2025; for Inditex the same imbalance surfaces intermittently as disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024 but not in 2023 or 2025, where the favorable and unfavorable figures are stated in the same sentence.

On materials and circular economy, both firms report rising headline percentages for recycled or lower-impact materials, and both show a recurring gap between resale, rental, repair, or collection tonnage and any denominator connecting that tonnage to overall production or sales volume. Biodiversity disclosure diverges more sharply: H&M's reporting remains symbolic and without a quantified group-wide target throughout the study period, while Inditex introduces a dated, quantified hectare target in 2022 and reports increasingly reconciled progress figures against it from 2024 onward. Within the workers-focused themes, both firms name living-wage ambitions, but neither discloses a wage-level or living-wage-benchmark figure tied to that ambition in any coded year: H&M instead reports statutory-wage comparisons and, from 2024, a coverage-based enrolment target described by the company as focused on program coverage rather than impact, while Inditex reports a rising but comparatively small people-reached figure that remains the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas in every year it is tracked. The Communities theme shows a further point of divergence in the 2025 reporting cycle, where H&M retains the topic without time-bound targets, and Inditex's materiality assessment excludes the topic from its material-topic list.

Taken together, the coding identifies patterns that are traceable to specific documents, years, and pages rather than to a single overall label applied to either company. With respect to RQ1, both firms' coded material contains a mixture of substantive and symbolic responses rather than one uniform response type across themes and years. With respect to RQ2, the cross-case matrices identify page-cited selective-disclosure patterns, including omission, disproportional emphasis, unclear boundary, and inconsistent metric codings, in both firms' reporting, alongside one identified withholding asymmetry running from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds on supply-chain wage data, with no comparably supported instance running in the other direction.
