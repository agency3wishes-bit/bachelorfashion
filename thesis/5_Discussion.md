### 5.1 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Responses (RQ1)

The clearest institutional-pressure signal in the coded material is coercive, and its most striking feature is not its presence but its timing. H&M Group's climate disclosure carries anticipatory regulatory language from the first year of the study period — framing the company in 2022 as "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded symbolic because no date or metric attaches to the language (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16) — and sustains that register through the FY2024 Progress Report's carefully hedged statement that the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS (H&M Group, 2024, p. 2). Inditex's coded 2022–2023 material contains comparatively little equivalent anticipatory rhetoric, yet its reporting architecture converts to the full ESRS-numbered structure already in the 2024 statement, a full reporting cycle before H&M's own disclosure takes on a comparably compliance-scoped, ESRS-structured form in 2025 (Section 4.1; Section 4.4). This asymmetry maps directly onto, and partially complicates, Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) finding that non-financial disclosure expands in the anticipatory period before a regulation binds: H&M exemplifies the "talk-first" anticipatory pattern their Spanish evidence describes, absorbing coercive pressure as language for roughly three reporting cycles before absorbing it as structure. Inditex, by contrast, exhibits something the anticipatory-isomorphism account does not predict — early structural conformity with little preceding anticipatory narrative visible in the coded corpus. Coercive pressure, in other words, was reflected in both firms' communication, but through two different absorption sequences: rhetoric preceding architecture in one case, architecture arriving without much rhetoric in the other. This within-pair variation supports Latif et al.'s (2020) premise that institutional pressures are empirically tractable as distinct constructs, while showing that the *same* pressure type can surface in report content through quite different observable traces.

The substantive–symbolic comparison RQ1 calls for resolves most informatively at the level of themes rather than firms. Both companies anchor their scope 1 and 2 emissions to SBTi-validated targets coded substantive — a normative anchoring, since science-based target-setting is a voluntary professional standard rather than a legal mandate — while scope 3, the large majority of each footprint, follows a more uneven trajectory (Section 4.4). For H&M this recurs as the codebook's MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) sub-pattern in nearly every coded year: a +18% year-on-year scope 1+2 increase disclosed in the same passage as an 8% baseline reduction in 2022, a +3% scope 3 increase alongside a 23.7% baseline reduction in the FY2024 Progress Report, reversing to a substantive −13.6% only in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). Two interpretive points follow. First, this is not decoupling in the concealment sense the literature often implies: the unfavorable figures are self-disclosed, merely placed beside a more flattering comparator. Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) observation that symbolic conformity can operate "whether intentionally or not" is apposite here, and the coding deliberately distinguished this transparent-setback pattern from scope manipulation precisely to avoid overstating the greenwashing finding. Second, the persistence of the scope 1+2/scope 3 split in both firms is consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) finding that decoupling under EU reporting regulation is uneven across environmental sub-dimensions rather than uniform: where measurement infrastructure is mature and externally validated, responses are substantive; where it is not, responses thin out.

Biodiversity is where that thinning is most visible, and where the two firms diverge most sharply. H&M's biodiversity disclosure remains symbolic across all four years — an aim to set commitments in 2023, a target "still in the process of being developed," one of only two sections without a quantified progress table in FY2024, and an explicit statement in 2025 that no biodiversity targets exist, with adoption deferred to 2026 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34; H&M Group, 2023, p. 27; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 16–17; H&M Group, 2025, p. 86). Inditex moves in the opposite direction: from a symbolic, certification-listing 2022 section, through a dated five-million-hectare 2030 target without progress data in 2023, to arithmetically reconciled hectare figures in 2024 and 2025 anchored to the Science Based Targets Network framework (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211; Inditex, 2023, p. 230; Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Read through Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) argument that institutional pressure is mediated by internal organizational commitment, the biodiversity divergence extends their firm-level insight inward: mediation apparently operates theme by theme, since the same H&M that quantifies climate and water rigorously leaves biodiversity untargeted, and the same Inditex that quantifies hectares leaves wages unquantified (Section 4.4). Normative pressure also leaves a distinctive trace in this material: Inditex's realignment of its materials terminology to match "benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) reads as convergence on field-level standard vocabulary — behavior with a mimetic-normative character, adopting the field's categories under definitional uncertainty — even though it earned an INCONSISTENT METRIC coding because the continuity claim was asserted rather than demonstrated. Genuinely mimetic conformity, in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) strict original sense of imitation of organizations perceived as successful or legitimate specifically under conditions of uncertainty about the appropriate course of action — rather than benchmarking against a named rival for competitive advantage — is thus better evidenced in this corpus by convergence on shared voluntary frameworks (SBTi, Textile Exchange, ZDHC) than by the peer-ranking language examined next, which belongs analytically to competitive signaling rather than institutional isomorphism — a distinction Section 5.3 returns to.

### 5.2 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure (RQ2)

The most consistent selective-disclosure finding in the corpus is the supply-chain wage asymmetry, and it aligns closely with the proprietary-cost mechanism in the literature. Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area — a program named after a wage outcome — reports no wage-level, living-wage-benchmark, or wage-gap figure in any of the four coded years, disclosing only a people-reached count that is consistently the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas (120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028), in reports that quantify most other themes to a comparable or greater level of detail (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). H&M, by contrast, publishes wage tables against statutory minima, an independent supplier gender-pay-gap study, and a narrow but concrete outcome figure — a seven-factory reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside its own admission that legal minimum wages are "not sufficient... in many countries" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 29, 31). Lee and Lee's (2022) finding that intensified competition depresses voluntary disclosure of competitively sensitive information offers the most parsimonious interpretation: country- and factory-level wage data reveal sourcing-cost structure, a plausibly proprietary category for an apparel buyer. Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) global finding that scrutiny and diffused norms — not mere exposure to reporting pressure — are what limit selective disclosure offers a complementary reading: Inditex's Living Wages gap may persist specifically where wage-level scrutiny and cross-border normative pressure on labor disclosure remain weaker than for environmental metrics such as emissions or biodiversity, which this same corpus shows Inditex quantifying rigorously under an otherwise-comparable reporting regime (Section 5.1). Three qualifications keep this reading within what the coding supports. The asymmetry is domain-specific — both firms disclose own-workforce gender-pay-gap figures, so this is not a general wage-transparency gap (Section 4.4). It is directional: the cross-case coding's explicit negative result found no category that Inditex discloses and H&M comparably withholds (Section 4.4), which strengthens the pattern precisely because disconfirming candidates were sought and rejected. And it is a disclosure finding, not a wage finding: nothing in the coded material speaks to what Inditex's suppliers actually pay, only to what the reports state.

A second family of findings concerns emphasis rather than absence. Inditex's climate narrative in 2022 and 2024 foregrounds favorable small-denominator achievements — the 100% renewable-electricity statement, the −87.7% scope 1+2 trajectory — while the corresponding scope 3 figures, though tabulated nearby, are not stated in the same or an adjacent sentence, meeting the codebook's operational DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS test; in 2023 and 2025 the favorable and unfavorable figures share a sentence and the test is not met (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199; Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163; Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). The recurring self-consumption pattern — a rising on-site generation figure narrated every year while its share of total energy consumption, under one percent throughout, goes unstated in the same passage — behaves identically (Section 4.3). This is selective disclosure in exactly the sense Nemes et al. (2022) operationalize: partial, insufficiently contextualized emphasis on positive performance, distinct from fabrication. It also corresponds to Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) passive, omission-based mode of communication-level greenwashing rather than their active, misleading-disclosure mode — the unfavorable numbers exist in the same documents, so what varies is narrative prominence, the "grey zone" of unaudited voluntary framing that Gatti et al. (2019) argue resists any bright-line classification. The denominator findings carry the same signature on the H&M side: resale activity reported with no group-wide scale figure in 2022, then 0.6% of turnover in FY2024 (flagged only as an emphasis candidate), then 0.8% stated in the same sentence as the growth claim in 2025, explicitly no longer meeting the trigger (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 21–22; H&M Group, 2025, p. 90) — a gap that closes over time, unlike Inditex's collection-tonnage series, which lacks a garments-placed-on-market denominator in nearly every year (Section 4.3).

The Communities divergence shows that selectivity can also be procedural. H&M retains Communities as a topic while stating that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets exist for it, alongside a fully reconciled grievance table (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 110–111); Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment concludes that ESRS "S3 – Affected communities" is "not material for Inditex," removing the theme from its material-topic list altogether (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both reduce commitment-level disclosure relative to earlier years, but the second operates upstream of disclosure itself: under a double-materiality regime, the materiality assessment determines what must be reported at all, so exclusion at that stage is a structurally stronger form of omission than an unmet target. This extends Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) "orphan issues" logic in an unexpected direction — where their concern was material topics receiving thin follow-through, the ESRS architecture creates the possibility of resolving that tension by re-drawing materiality rather than deepening disclosure.

External triangulation, within its limits, points the same direction as the coded asymmetry. Fashion Revolution's Fashion Transparency Index scored H&M at 66% in 2022, tied with The North Face and Timberland in the report's own top-10 table (Fashion Revolution, 2022, p. 13); Zara/Inditex does not appear in that same table, placing its 2022 score below the lowest tied group shown (54%), though no exact figure could be confirmed. In 2023 — the only edition in the window scoring both firms comparably — the Index scored H&M at 71%, confirmed directly against the report's own "Highest Scoring Brands" chart (Fashion Revolution, 2023, p. 19), where H&M sits between United Colors of Benetton (73%) and C&A (68%); Zara/Inditex's reported 50% for the same year does not appear on that chart (it lists only the eleven highest- and roughly twenty lowest-scoring brands) and so remains PARTIAL, verified only at search-snippet level. The narrower climate-only successor editions reportedly score H&M at 61% in 2024 and first of 200 brands at 71% in 2025, with no reliable Inditex figure in either year; these two years' figures likewise remain PARTIAL, since the relevant charts could not be reliably read from a text extraction. In sum, both H&M figures for 2022 and 2023 are now confirmed directly against primary report pages; the Zara/Inditex 2023 figure and both 2024/2025 figures are cited here as awaiting further primary-source confirmation, not as settled numbers. With that caveat carried, the 2023 gap is at least consistent with the one-directional withholding pattern the coding identified independently. Two further nuances complicate any simple "H&M is more transparent" gloss. First, H&M's own reports cite its Fashion Revolution ranking as a competitive credential (Section 4.4), so the index is simultaneously evidence and marketing material. Second, high disclosure and external challenge coexist: the Changing Markets Foundation characterizes a very high share of H&M's assessed claims — roughly 96%, per one secondarily sourced account that likewise awaits primary verification — as misleading, while identifying Inditex as the largest synthetic-fiber user in its sample. That characterization is the NGO's evaluative claim, advocacy-toned and not adopted here as a finding of this thesis; but its coexistence with H&M's transparency ranking illustrates Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) broader point that corporate disclosure volume and externally contested claim quality are separate dimensions. Transparency indices reward disclosure; they do not adjudicate it.

The same selective-emphasis logic shows up at brand level, in the two firms' consumer-facing product labels. H&M's decade-long "Conscious" label was retired in 2022; the company itself said the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), and no successor metric was disclosed at the point of retirement. This is coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE: sustained outside scrutiny — the kind of market and normative pressure Delmas and Burbano (2011) count among greenwashing's principal external drivers — visibly altered a communication choice mid-program. Inditex's Zara-brand "Join Life" label took a different path in the very same reporting year. The 2022 report states that 61% of products complied with the Join Life standard, ahead of the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187). That figure sits on the same page as the separate "preferred fibres" terminology realignment discussed above, but it was not itself flagged under any greenwashing indicator in the coding matrix. Read together, the two labels point to genuinely different corporate responses to comparable brand-level sustainability messaging: one company pulled its label under external pressure and did not say what would replace it; the other reported a figure that beat its own target without apparent challenge in the coded material. The contrast fits Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) finding that it is external scrutiny — clearly present for Conscious, less evident in the coded material for Join Life — that disciplines selective disclosure, not disclosure volume or labeling activity on its own.

### 5.3 Interaction Between Institutional and Competitive Pressures

The findings suggest that competitive signaling layers onto institutional-pressure-driven disclosure during discretionary periods and recedes when coercive structure binds — but only for one of the two firms. H&M's competitive-framing and peer-benchmarking count rises from one coded row in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in the FY2024 Progress Report, then falls back to one in the ESRS-structured 2025 report (Section 4.4). The peak coincides exactly with the CSRD-anticipation window identified in Section 5.1 — the period when H&M's regulatory conformity was still rhetorical rather than architectural. Read alongside Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) evidence that anticipatory periods are when disclosure behavior is most discretionary and peer-oriented, the pattern suggests that the same anticipatory space that accommodates "preparing to comply" language also accommodates differentiation claims; once the standardized, compliance-scoped format arrives, with its explicit no-target admissions and boundary caveats, promotional framing loses room. For Inditex, by contrast, competitive-signal rows stay low and flat across all four years (two, two, one, two), with no spike around its own 2024 ESRS transition (Section 4.4). There, institutional and competitive pressures simply coexist at low intensity — competitive framing appears to be a stable background register, not a report-format-driven one.

The wage finding adds a second interaction mode: domain-specific insulation. Inditex's environmental disclosure was substantially restructured by the coercive ESRS transition — quantified biodiversity progress, standardized topical chapters — yet its Living Wages sub-section reports the identical reach-only metric structure in every year on both sides of that transition (Section 4.3; Section 4.4). Institutional pressure, on this evidence, reorganized the disclosure domains it directly addressed while leaving the competitively sensitive wage domain untouched; the proprietary-cost logic (Lee & Lee, 2022) and the legitimacy-seeking logic operated side by side without one displacing the other. This coexistence is what Chapter 2 anticipated in juxtaposing peer-driven convergence with competitive withholding: firms can converge on headline frameworks while guarding granular detail. Finally, the two firms' anchoring styles reveal that the infrastructure of normative pressure and the instruments of competitive signaling are often the same artifacts used in different directions. H&M's competitive instances are mostly anchored to independently verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP A score, a Textile Exchange "Leading" designation naming it one of 11 of 130 assessed companies, a named comparison with Levi's (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 30–31; H&M Group, 2024, pp. 5–6, 19–20) — that is, it cites the norm-carriers as rankings in which it performs well. Inditex's instances are more often self-referential benchmark-setting ambitions, such as framing Care for Water as an ambition "to make it a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2025, p. 144): aspiring to author the norm rather than to score against it. Neither register is mimetic in the institutional-theory sense (DiMaggio & Powell, 1983) — no imitation under uncertainty is being expressed — and in absolute terms competitive language remains a secondary feature of both firms' communication (Section 4.4).

### 5.4 Theoretical Implications

For Institutional Theory, the case pair's principal contribution is a resolution-level correction: the substantive–symbolic distinction discriminates poorly at firm level but well at theme level. Neither company is uniformly substantive or symbolic; H&M's symbolic responses cluster in biodiversity and the no-target social themes while its climate, water, and materials disclosure is consistently quantified, and Inditex shows the mirror-image cluster, quantified in biodiversity and materials but symbolic on living wages (Sections 4.4–4.5). This extends Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) firm-level mediation argument — that internal commitment determines whether pressure becomes change — into intra-firm territory: whatever mediates institutional pressure appears to operate per theme, plausibly tracking the maturity of each theme's measurement infrastructure, which is also the reading most consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) sub-dimension-uneven decoupling. Studies that assign whole firms to conformity types risk averaging away exactly this variation. The timing evidence adds a second refinement: anticipatory isomorphism (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023) describes one observed absorption path (H&M's rhetoric-then-structure sequence) but not the other (Inditex's structure-without-rhetoric sequence), suggesting the anticipatory-talk phase is a contingent, not necessary, stage in how coercive pressure is metabolized into reporting practice.

For the decoupling and legitimacy literature, the recurrent MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern and the self-labeled target limitations complicate the implicit association between decoupling and concealment. H&M's 2025 report itself states that its wage-management target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110) — the company naming its own means–ends gap — and both firms print their unfavorable scope 3 and effluent-plant trends in the same tables as their favorable baselines (Sections 4.2, 4.4). Loko and Schiehll (2025) measure policy–practice misalignment as something researchers detect; this corpus shows firms increasingly *announcing* it, which suggests legitimacy management through disclosure can incorporate calibrated candor rather than only positive asymmetry, without any inference about motive being required or made here. Where the greenwashing-indicator codings did fire, they concerned emphasis, boundaries, denominators, and metric consistency — never fabricated evidence. That distribution supports the definitional position Chapter 2 distilled from Gatti et al. (2019) and Spaniol et al. (2024): the workable demarcation between misleading and merely positive communication is evidentiary — substantiation, scope, comparability — rather than tonal. It also suggests the selective-disclosure construct (Nemes et al., 2022; Bernini & La Rosa, 2024) needs a procedural extension for the CSRD era: when omission can be produced upstream, through a materiality assessment that removes a topic from the reportable set, selectivity migrates from the text to the process that generates the text. Finally, the wage finding provides qualitative, paired-case corroboration for Lee and Lee's (2022) proprietary-cost mechanism and for Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) global scrutiny-and-norms mechanism outside their original single-country and 45-country panel settings respectively — with the added specification, unavailable to either large-sample design, that the withholding was directional and category-specific rather than general.

### 5.5 Practical Implications

For report preparers, the findings identify an inexpensive drafting discipline with outsized effect on disclosure quality: comparator co-location. The difference between Inditex's climate units that were coded disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024, and those that were not in 2023 and 2025, is not the underlying data — the scope 3 figures were disclosed in all four years — but whether the unfavorable figure shares a sentence with the favorable one (Section 4.3). The same holds for denominators: H&M's resale metric exited the emphasis-candidate category the year its turnover share appeared in the same sentence as the growth claim (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90). Placing the large-denominator or unfavorable figure adjacent to the headline claim, and attaching a scale denominator to every absolute figure, would eliminate most of the indicator codings observed in this corpus without requiring any change in performance.

For regulators and assurance providers, the ESRS transition shows a double edge worth attention. On one side, the standardized format coincided with new candor: H&M's 2025 report states explicitly which topics carry no time-bound targets (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 111–114), admissions absent from the pre-ESRS reports. On the other, the materiality assessment becomes the critical control point: Inditex's exclusion of Affected Communities as non-material (Inditex, 2025, p. 121) is formally compliant behavior that nonetheless removes a previously reported theme from commitment-level disclosure. Scrutiny of materiality determinations — not only of reported content — is therefore where review effort buys the most. The retirement of H&M's "Conscious" label, described by the company as "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), further indicates that consumer-facing green-claims enforcement does register in corporate communication decisions. For investors, NGO raters, and other report users, the practical lessons are to read tables against narratives rather than narratives alone; to check which comparator a headline uses (year-on-year versus baseline, relative versus absolute); to treat outcome-named programs without outcome metrics — a "Living Wages" area reporting only reach counts — as a specific follow-up question; and to remember that transparency rankings measure disclosure rather than performance, as the coexistence of H&M's high index scores with its own self-disclosed unfavorable trends illustrates.

### 5.6 Limitations and Future Research

The design-level limitations set out in Section 3.5 — self-reporting bias inherent in corporate-authored documents, the two-case restriction to analytic rather than statistical generalization, and single-analyst coding without formal inter-coder reliability — all carry forward, and the findings now allow them to be stated more precisely. The single-coder limitation weighs most heavily on exactly the codes this chapter leans on: the disproportional-emphasis operational test, the two MIXED sub-patterns, and the omission sub-tests all require judgment about sentence adjacency, comparator flattery, and internal consistency, and these are the decisions a second coder would most plausibly contest. The audit trail in the coding matrices mitigates but does not replace that check. Second, the NGO triangulation in Section 5.2 rests on figures verified only at search-snippet level because the research environment could not access the primary Fashion Revolution and Changing Markets documents; every such figure remains tagged as partially verified, and the triangulation should be read as directionally suggestive rather than confirmatory until the primary PDFs are checked. Third, several findings are explicitly provisional in the coding itself: the FY2024 resale emphasis instance is a candidate pending a page/word-share measurement method, the Inditex 2025 water-denominator omission is a candidate pending verification against that report's methodology annex, and the H&M 2025 biodiversity decoupling flag is likewise a candidate rather than a confirmed instance. Fourth, the corpus imposed comparability ceilings the analysis could not lift: the firms' fiscal years cover offset twelve-month windows, and the headline materials metrics ("recycled or sustainably sourced" versus "lower-impact fibres") are definitionally non-comparable across companies — and, for Inditex's 2025 figure, not verifiably continuous across years — so no cross-firm headline-percentage comparison was drawn. Finally, document analysis cannot observe motive: the proprietary-cost interpretation of the wage-data gap is the reading most consistent with the pattern and the literature, but alternative explanations, such as differences in data availability or internal measurement practice, cannot be excluded from public reports alone, and no claim about intent is made.

These limitations translate directly into a research agenda. A second coder applied to the existing matrices, with formal inter-coder reliability on the emphasis and MIXED codes specifically, would be the single most valuable robustness step. Direct verification of the NGO index figures against primary documents would either harden or retire the triangulation. Extending the study window into the fully post-CSRD years would test two predictions this analysis generates: that H&M's competitive-framing count remains suppressed under the standardized format, and that Inditex's wage-disclosure gap either persists (supporting the proprietary-cost reading) or closes under widening value-chain reporting obligations (supporting a regulatory-lag reading). Adding a third case headquartered outside the EU's coercive reach — or an ultra-fast online-only competitor — would help separate what in these findings is CSRD-driven from what is sector- or firm-specific. And converting the disproportional-emphasis candidates into measured findings via a quantitative page- or word-share method, following the precedent of Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) action-statement counting, would put the emphasis findings on firmer footing than the sentence-adjacency test alone.

This chapter has interpreted the Results through the framework built in Chapter 2: coercive pressure was absorbed through two different sequences, responses proved substantive or symbolic theme by theme rather than firm by firm, and the selective-disclosure patterns — emphasis, denominators, procedural omission, and one directional withholding asymmetry — were shown to be consistent with proprietary-cost logic operating alongside, not instead of, legitimacy-seeking conformity. It has also drawn out what the case pair contributes to institutional, legitimacy, and decoupling scholarship, what practitioners and regulators might take from it, and where its evidentiary limits lie. Chapter 6 now concludes by answering RQ1 and RQ2 directly, stating the thesis's contribution, and closing the study within the boundaries the coded evidence supports.
