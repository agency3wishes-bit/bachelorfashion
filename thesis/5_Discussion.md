### 5.1 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Responses (RQ1)

The clearest institutional-pressure signal is coercive, and its striking feature is timing. H&M Group's climate disclosure carries anticipatory regulatory language from the first study year — framing the company in 2022 as "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded symbolic because no date or metric attaches (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16) — and sustains it through the FY2024 statement that the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 2). Inditex's 2022–2023 material contains little equivalent rhetoric, yet its architecture converts to the full ESRS-numbered structure already in 2024, a cycle before H&M's disclosure takes a comparably compliance-scoped form in 2025 (Sections 4.1, 4.4). This maps onto, and complicates, Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) finding that non-financial disclosure expands before a regulation binds: H&M exemplifies their "talk-first" pattern, absorbing coercive pressure as language for roughly three cycles before absorbing it as structure, while Inditex shows what that account does not predict — early structural conformity with little preceding narrative. Coercive pressure thus surfaced through two absorption sequences: rhetoric before architecture in one case, architecture without much rhetoric in the other. This within-pair variation supports Latif et al.'s (2020) premise that institutional pressures are tractable as distinct constructs, while showing the *same* pressure type can leave different observable traces.

The substantive–symbolic comparison RQ1 calls for resolves most informatively at theme level, not firm level. Both firms anchor scope 1 and 2 emissions to SBTi-validated targets coded substantive — a normative anchoring, since science-based target-setting is voluntary, not legally mandated — while scope 3, the majority of each footprint, follows a more uneven trajectory (Section 4.4). For H&M this recurs as the codebook's MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) sub-pattern in nearly every year: an unfavorable year-on-year scope 1+2 or scope 3 rise printed beside a favorable baseline reduction (a +18% rise beside an 8% reduction in 2022; a +3% scope 3 rise beside a 23.7% reduction in FY2024), reversing to a substantive −13.6% only in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). This is not decoupling in the concealment sense: the unfavorable figures are self-disclosed, merely placed beside a flattering comparator, a transparent-setback pattern the coding kept distinct from scope manipulation. Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) observation that symbolic conformity can operate "whether intentionally or not" is apposite here. The persistence of the split in both firms is consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) finding that decoupling under EU regulation is uneven across sub-dimensions: where measurement infrastructure is mature and externally validated, responses are substantive; where it is not, they thin out.

Biodiversity is where that thinning is most visible and the firms diverge most sharply. H&M's biodiversity disclosure stays symbolic across all four years — an aim to set commitments in 2023, a target "still in the process of being developed," one of only two FY2024 sections without any quantified progress figure, and an explicit 2025 statement that no biodiversity targets exist, adoption deferred to 2026 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34; H&M Group, 2023, p. 27; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex moves the opposite way, from a symbolic 2022 certification-listing, through a dated five-million-hectare 2030 target without progress data in 2023, to arithmetically reconciled hectare figures in 2024 and 2025 anchored to the Science Based Targets Network framework (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211; Inditex, 2023, p. 230; Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Read through Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) argument that institutional pressure is mediated by internal organizational commitment, the divergence extends their firm-level insight inward: mediation operates theme by theme within each firm, not between firms (Section 4.4). Normative pressure leaves a further trace: Inditex's realignment of its materials terminology to match "benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) reads as convergence on field-level standard vocabulary — a mimetic-normative move under definitional uncertainty — though it earned an INCONSISTENT METRIC coding because the continuity claim was asserted, not demonstrated. Genuinely mimetic conformity in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) strict sense — imitation of organizations perceived as legitimate under uncertainty, not benchmarking against a named rival — is thus better evidenced by convergence on shared voluntary frameworks (SBTi, Textile Exchange, ZDHC) than by the peer-ranking language examined next, which belongs to competitive signaling rather than institutional isomorphism (Section 5.3).

### 5.2 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure (RQ2)

The most consistent selective-disclosure finding is the supply-chain wage asymmetry, aligning closely with the proprietary-cost mechanism. Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area — a program named after a wage outcome — reports no wage-level, benchmark, or wage-gap figure in any of the four years, disclosing only a people-reached count that is consistently the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas (120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028), in reports that quantify most other themes in comparable detail (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). H&M, by contrast, publishes wage tables against statutory minima, an independent supplier gender-pay-gap study, and a concrete outcome figure — a seven-factory reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside its admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 29, 31). Lee and Lee's (2022) finding that intensified competition depresses voluntary disclosure of competitively sensitive information gives the most parsimonious reading: country- and factory-level wage data reveal sourcing-cost structure, a plausibly proprietary category for an apparel buyer. Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) scrutiny-and-norms finding adds a complement: the gap may persist where wage-level scrutiny and normative pressure remain weaker than for the environmental metrics this corpus shows Inditex quantifying rigorously under an otherwise-comparable regime (Section 5.1). Three qualifications keep this within what the coding supports. It is domain-specific — both firms disclose own-workforce gender-pay-gap figures, so it is not a general wage-transparency gap. It is directional: the cross-case coding found no category that Inditex discloses and H&M comparably withholds (Section 4.4), which strengthens the pattern because disconfirming candidates were sought and rejected. And it is a disclosure finding, not a wage finding: nothing in the coded material speaks to what Inditex's suppliers actually pay, only to what the reports state.

A second family of findings concerns emphasis rather than absence. Inditex's climate narrative in 2022 and 2024 foregrounds favorable small-denominator achievements — the 100% renewable-electricity statement, the −87.7% scope 1+2 trajectory — while the corresponding scope 3 figures, though tabulated nearby, do not share the same or an adjacent sentence, meeting the codebook's DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS test; in 2023 and 2025 the figures share a sentence and the test is not met (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199; Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163; Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). The self-consumption energy pattern behaves identically (Section 4.3). This is selective disclosure in Nemes et al.'s (2022) sense — partial, insufficiently contextualized emphasis on positive performance, distinct from fabrication — and Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) passive, omission-based mode rather than their active, misleading one: the unfavorable numbers exist in the same documents, so only narrative prominence varies, the "grey zone" of unaudited voluntary framing that Gatti et al. (2019) argue resists any bright-line classification. The denominator findings carry the same signature on the H&M side: resale reported with no group-wide scale figure in 2022, then 0.6% of turnover in FY2024 (an emphasis candidate only), then 0.8% stated in the same sentence as the growth claim in 2025, no longer triggering (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 21–22; H&M Group, 2025, p. 90) — a gap that closes over time, unlike Inditex's collection-tonnage series, which lacks a garments-placed-on-market denominator in nearly every year (Section 4.3).

Selectivity can also be procedural. H&M retains Communities as a topic while stating that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets exist for it (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112); Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment concludes that ESRS "S3 – Affected communities" is "not material for Inditex," removing the theme from its material-topic list (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both reduce commitment-level disclosure, but the second operates upstream: under a double-materiality regime the materiality assessment determines what must be reported at all, so exclusion at that stage is a structurally stronger form of omission than an unmet target. This extends Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) "orphan issues" logic in an unexpected direction — where their concern was material topics receiving thin follow-through, the ESRS architecture creates the option of resolving that tension by re-drawing materiality rather than deepening disclosure.

External triangulation, within its limits, points the same way as the coded asymmetry. Fashion Revolution's Fashion Transparency Index scored H&M at 66% in 2022, tied with The North Face and Timberland in the report's top-10 table (Fashion Revolution, 2022, p. 13); Zara/Inditex does not appear there, placing its 2022 score below the lowest tied group shown (54%), though no exact figure could be confirmed. In 2023 — the only edition scoring both firms comparably — the Index scored H&M at 71%, confirmed directly against the report's "Highest Scoring Brands" chart (Fashion Revolution, 2023, p. 19), where H&M sits between United Colors of Benetton (73%) and C&A (68%); Zara/Inditex's reported 50% does not appear on that chart and remains PARTIAL, verified only at search-snippet level. The narrower climate-only successor editions score H&M at 61% in 2024, confirmed against the "What Fuels Fashion?" 2024 results (Fashion Revolution, 2024, p. 21), with Zara/Inditex at 41% in the full distribution (Fashion Revolution, 2024, p. 20) — a directly-confirmed twenty-point gap in the same direction as 2023's, though climate-and-energy-only and thus narrower than the full-scope 2023 Index; H&M is further reported first of 200 brands at 71% in 2025, still verified only at search-snippet level, with no comparable 2025 Zara/Inditex figure located. With the 50% and 71% snippet-level figures awaiting primary confirmation, the 2023 gap is consistent with the withholding pattern the coding identified independently. Two nuances complicate a simple "H&M is more transparent" gloss. First, H&M's reports cite its Fashion Revolution ranking as a competitive credential (Section 4.4), so the index is at once evidence and marketing material. Second, high disclosure and external challenge coexist: the Changing Markets Foundation's Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 report notes that H&M Group "has been reprimanded for its vague claims," "has been forced to remove 'sustainability' labelling," and "faces multiple legal cases," while identifying Inditex as reporting the second-highest disclosed volume of synthetic fibres in its sample — 178,030 tonnes, behind only Nike (Changing Markets Foundation, 2023). These advocacy-toned NGO observations, not adopted here as findings, illustrate Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) point that disclosure volume and contested claim quality are separate dimensions. Transparency indices reward disclosure; they do not adjudicate it.

The same logic appears at brand level. H&M's decade-long "Conscious" label was retired in 2022; the company said the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), disclosing no successor metric at retirement. This is coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE: sustained outside scrutiny — the market and normative pressure Delmas and Burbano (2011) count among greenwashing's principal external drivers — visibly altered a communication choice mid-program. Inditex's group-wide "Join Life" label, used across its brands including Zara, took a different path in the same year: the 2022 report states that 61% of products complied with the Join Life standard, ahead of the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187), and was not flagged under any greenwashing indicator. The contrast fits Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) finding that external scrutiny — present for Conscious, less evident for Join Life — disciplines selective disclosure, not disclosure volume or labeling activity alone: one firm pulled its label under pressure without naming a replacement; the other reported a target-beating figure without apparent challenge in the coded material.

### 5.3 Interaction Between Institutional and Competitive Pressures

Competitive signaling appears to layer onto institutional-pressure-driven disclosure during discretionary periods and recede when coercive structure binds — but only for one firm. H&M's competitive-framing and peer-benchmarking count rises from one coded row in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in FY2024, then falls back to one in the ESRS-structured 2025 report (Section 4.4). The peak coincides exactly with the CSRD-anticipation window of Section 5.1, when H&M's conformity was still rhetorical rather than architectural. Read alongside Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) evidence that anticipatory periods are when disclosure is most discretionary and peer-oriented, the same space that accommodates "preparing to comply" language also accommodates differentiation claims; once the standardized format arrives, promotional framing loses room. For Inditex, competitive-signal rows stay low and flat across all four years (two, two, one, two), with no spike around its 2024 ESRS transition (Section 4.4) — a stable background register, not a format-driven one.

The wage finding adds a second interaction mode: domain-specific insulation. Inditex's environmental disclosure was substantially restructured by the coercive ESRS transition, yet its Living Wages sub-section reports the identical reach-only metric structure on both sides of that transition (Sections 4.3, 4.4). Institutional pressure reorganized the domains it directly addressed while leaving the competitively sensitive wage domain untouched; the proprietary-cost logic (Lee & Lee, 2022) and the legitimacy-seeking logic operated side by side, as Chapter 2 anticipated: firms can converge on headline frameworks while guarding granular detail. Finally, normative infrastructure and competitive instruments are often the same artifacts used in different directions. H&M's competitive instances mostly anchor to independently verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP A score (2024; A− in 2023), a Textile Exchange "Leading" designation (one of six overall leaders of 130 companies in 2023, one of 11 in 2024), a named comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 5, 20) — citing norm-carriers as rankings in which it performs well. Inditex's instances are more often self-referential benchmark-setting ambitions, such as framing Care for Water as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2025, p. 144): aspiring to author the norm rather than score against it. Neither register is mimetic in the institutional-theory sense (DiMaggio & Powell, 1983), and competitive language remains a secondary feature of both firms' communication (Section 4.4).

### 5.4 Theoretical Implications

For Institutional Theory, the case pair's principal contribution is a resolution-level correction: the substantive–symbolic distinction discriminates poorly at firm level but well at theme level, since neither company is uniformly one or the other and each shows a mirror-image cluster (Sections 4.4–4.5). This extends Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) firm-level mediation argument into intra-firm territory: whatever mediates institutional pressure appears to operate per theme, plausibly tracking each theme's measurement-infrastructure maturity — the reading most consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) sub-dimension-uneven decoupling. Studies that assign whole firms to conformity types risk averaging away this variation. The timing evidence adds a second refinement: anticipatory isomorphism (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023) describes H&M's rhetoric-then-structure sequence but not Inditex's structure-without-rhetoric one, suggesting the anticipatory-talk phase is contingent, not necessary.

For the decoupling and legitimacy literature, the recurrent MIXED pattern and the self-labeled target limitations complicate the implicit association between decoupling and concealment. H&M's 2025 report itself states that its wage-management target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110) — naming its own means–ends gap — and both firms print unfavorable scope 3 and effluent-plant trends beside their favorable baselines (Sections 4.2, 4.4). Loko and Schiehll (2025) measure policy–practice misalignment as something researchers detect; this corpus shows firms increasingly *announcing* it, suggesting legitimacy management through disclosure can incorporate calibrated candor rather than only positive asymmetry, without any inference about motive. Where the greenwashing-indicator codings fired, they concerned emphasis, boundaries, denominators, and metric consistency — never fabricated evidence — supporting the definitional position Chapter 2 distilled from Gatti et al. (2019) and Spaniol et al. (2024): the workable demarcation between misleading and merely positive communication is evidentiary (substantiation, scope, comparability), not tonal. It also suggests the selective-disclosure construct (Nemes et al., 2022; Bernini & La Rosa, 2024) needs a procedural extension for the CSRD era: when omission can be produced upstream, through a materiality assessment that removes a topic from the reportable set, selectivity migrates from the text to the process that generates it. Finally, the wage finding corroborates Lee and Lee's (2022) proprietary-cost and Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) scrutiny-and-norms mechanisms in a paired-case setting outside their original single-country and 45-country designs, adding that the withholding was directional and category-specific, not general.

### 5.5 Practical Implications

For report preparers, the findings identify an inexpensive drafting discipline with outsized effect: comparator co-location. The difference between Inditex's climate units coded disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024 and those not coded in 2023 and 2025 is not the underlying data — the scope 3 figures were disclosed in all four years — but whether the unfavorable figure shares a sentence with the favorable one (Section 4.3). The same holds for denominators: H&M's resale metric exited the emphasis-candidate category the year its turnover share appeared in the same sentence as the growth claim (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90). Placing the unfavorable or large-denominator figure adjacent to the headline claim, and attaching a scale denominator to every absolute figure, would eliminate most of the codings observed here without any change in performance.

For regulators and assurance providers, the ESRS transition shows a double edge. On one side, the standardized format coincided with new candor: H&M's 2025 report states explicitly which topics carry no time-bound targets (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118), admissions absent from the pre-ESRS reports. On the other, the materiality assessment becomes the critical control point: Inditex's exclusion of Affected Communities as non-material (Inditex, 2025, p. 121) is formally compliant behavior that nonetheless removes a previously reported theme from commitment-level disclosure. Scrutiny of materiality determinations — not only reported content — is therefore where review effort buys the most. The retirement of H&M's "Conscious" label, described by the company as "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), further indicates that consumer-facing green-claims enforcement registers in communication decisions. For investors, NGO raters, and other report users, the lessons are to read tables against narratives; to check which comparator a headline uses (year-on-year versus baseline, relative versus absolute); to treat outcome-named programs without outcome metrics — a "Living Wages" area reporting only reach counts — as a specific follow-up question; and to remember that transparency rankings measure disclosure, not performance, as the coexistence of H&M's high index scores with its own self-disclosed unfavorable trends illustrates.

### 5.6 Limitations and Future Research

The design-level limitations of Section 3.5 — self-reporting bias in corporate-authored documents, the two-case restriction to analytic rather than statistical generalization, and single-analyst coding without formal inter-coder reliability — all carry forward, and can now be stated more precisely. The single-coder limitation weighs most on the codes this chapter leans on: the disproportional-emphasis test, the two MIXED sub-patterns, and the omission sub-tests all turn on judgment about sentence adjacency, comparator flattery, and internal consistency — the decisions a second coder would most plausibly contest, mitigated but not replaced by the audit trail in the coding matrices. Second, the NGO triangulation in Section 5.2 was checked against primary sources: H&M's Index scores for 2022 (66%) and 2023 (71%), both firms' What Fuels Fashion? 2024 scores (H&M 61%, Zara/Inditex 41%), and the Changing Markets Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 observations are confirmed directly, while Zara/Inditex's 2023 Index score (50%) and H&M's 2025 ranking (first of 200 at 71%) remain verified only at search-snippet level and should be read as directionally suggestive; a previously cited "96% of H&M's assessed claims" figure, absent from the Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 primary, was removed. Third, several findings are explicitly provisional in the coding — the FY2024 resale emphasis instance, the Inditex 2025 water-denominator omission, and the H&M 2025 biodiversity decoupling flag are all candidates pending further measurement or verification. Fourth, the corpus imposed comparability ceilings the analysis could not lift: the firms' fiscal years cover offset twelve-month windows, and the headline materials metrics ("recycled or sustainably sourced" versus "lower-impact fibres") are definitionally non-comparable across companies — and, for Inditex's 2025 figure, not verifiably continuous across years — so no cross-firm headline-percentage comparison was drawn. Finally, document analysis cannot observe motive: the proprietary-cost interpretation of the wage-data gap is the reading most consistent with the pattern and the literature, but alternatives such as differences in data availability or internal measurement practice cannot be excluded from public reports alone, and no claim about intent is made.

These limitations translate into a research agenda. A second coder applied to the existing matrices, with formal inter-coder reliability on the emphasis and MIXED codes specifically, would be the single most valuable robustness step. Direct verification of the remaining NGO index figures against primary documents would either harden or retire the triangulation. Extending the window into the fully post-CSRD years would test two predictions this analysis generates: that H&M's competitive-framing count remains suppressed under the standardized format, and that Inditex's wage-disclosure gap either persists (supporting the proprietary-cost reading) or closes under widening value-chain reporting obligations (supporting a regulatory-lag reading). Adding a third case headquartered outside the EU's coercive reach — or an ultra-fast online-only competitor — would help separate what is CSRD-driven from what is sector- or firm-specific. And converting the disproportional-emphasis candidates into measured findings via a quantitative page- or word-share method, following Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) action-statement counting, would put the emphasis findings on firmer footing than the sentence-adjacency test alone.

This chapter has interpreted the Results through the Chapter 2 framework: coercive pressure was absorbed through two different sequences, responses proved substantive or symbolic theme by theme rather than firm by firm, and the selective-disclosure patterns — emphasis, denominators, procedural omission, and one directional withholding asymmetry — proved consistent with proprietary-cost logic operating alongside, not instead of, legitimacy-seeking conformity. Chapter 6 now concludes by answering RQ1 and RQ2 directly, stating the thesis's contribution, and closing the study within the boundaries the coded evidence supports.
