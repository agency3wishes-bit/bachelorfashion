### 3.1 Research Design

This thesis adopts a qualitative research design to examine how H&M Group and Inditex construct environmental communication under differing configurations of institutional and competitive pressure. A qualitative approach is warranted because the phenomenon under study — the framing, emphasis, and omission patterns within corporate sustainability disclosure — is interpretive by nature and cannot be reduced to a predetermined set of quantifiable indicators without losing the contextual meaning that institutional theory requires the analyst to recover. Bengtsson (2016) frames this interpretive demand directly through the distinction between manifest content, the visible surface structure of what a text states, and latent content, the deeper structure of what a text intends or implies; both, the source establishes, involve interpretation, differing only in depth rather than in kind. This distinction supports treating corporate disclosures not merely as a record of stated commitments but as material whose underlying framing must be read alongside its explicit claims, a premise the present design accepts as foundational.

Case study methodology, and specifically a multiple-case variant, follows from this interpretive orientation and from the bounded, comparative nature of the research problem. Baxter and Jack (2008) is used, within the restricted scope its own card supports, to situate the design within the broader case-study tradition associated with Stake and Yin, and — at the level the evidence permits — to justify examining H&M Group and Inditex together as a means of exploring similarities and differences between two comparable, bounded organizational cases rather than as an aggregated single-case account. This comparative logic aligns with the thesis's aim of contrasting institutional responses and disclosure patterns across two matched fast-fashion multinationals over a shared reporting period, rather than producing a generalisable single-firm narrative.

Documentary research constitutes the primary data-collection strategy, treating corporate sustainability reports, press communications, and related disclosure documents as the empirical record from which meaning units are drawn for analysis. This strategy follows from the qualitative, interpretive commitments already established and positions the documents themselves, rather than interview or survey data, as the site where institutional pressures and disclosure choices become observable.

The analytic approach adopted is directed qualitative content analysis, a deductive method in which coding categories are derived in advance from an established theoretical framework rather than allowed to emerge purely inductively from the data. Kibiswa (2019), while confirmed only at the level of its general logic rather than its specific procedural steps, supports the broader principle that deriving codes from an existing theory before engaging with new empirical material serves to test, corroborate, or extend that theory's applicability to a context in which it was not originally developed — here, the application of institutional theory's coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures, together with substantive and symbolic response categories, to fast-fashion corporate communication. A short methodological caveat applies: the detailed step-by-step procedure Kibiswa's article reportedly proposes cannot yet be confirmed from available evidence and awaits full-text access to the source. Having established the qualitative, comparative, and document-analytic logic of the design, the following subsection turns to the rationale for selecting H&M Group and Inditex as the thesis's two bounded cases.

### 3.2 Case Selection and Study Period

H&M Group and Inditex are selected as the two cases for this comparative study primarily because they are the phenomenon under investigation rather than merely convenient examples of it: both are leading, globally visible fast-fashion multinationals whose environmental communication practices are subject to sustained public, regulatory, and academic attention. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) treat both firms as central members of the industry's competitive set in their comparative analysis of transparency practices among the four largest fast-fashion retailers, which lends independent scholarly precedent to isolating H&M and Inditex as analytically significant units within the sector. Beyond salience, the two firms offer strong comparability on dimensions that matter for a controlled qualitative comparison: both operate at a similar global scale, rely on comparable multi-tier global sourcing models, are domiciled within the European Union, and compete in substantially overlapping consumer markets. This shared EU domicile is analytically important, since it places both firms under a common coercive regulatory environment—most notably the EU's evolving sustainability-disclosure requirements—allowing observed differences in communication behavior to be attributed more plausibly to firm-level variation than to divergent external regulatory pressure.

The multinational and global-value-chain (GVC) character of both firms further justifies analysis at the MNE level. Asmussen, Fosfuri, Larsen and Santangelo (2023) argue that IB research on corporate social responsibility has been dominated by an MNE-centric perspective and propose instead a bargaining view in which the visibility of CSR breaches at supplier sites depends on the negotiated relationship between lead firm and supplier; brand-owning MNEs such as H&M Group and Inditex are, in this account, incentivized to protect their "social brand" from reputational damage originating deeper in the value chain. This bargaining perspective, while general rather than sector-specific, suggests that studying brand-level communication of large, GVC-embedded fast-fashion MNEs is analytically appropriate precisely because information asymmetry and selective visibility are structural features of such chains, not incidental oversights.

Regarding variation in external scrutiny, Fraser and van der Ven (2022) directly report that H&M ranked as the most transparent of the four fast-fashion retailers examined in their pre-2022 comparison, while Gap and Inditex exhibited lower transparency because only one of three explanatory conditions—reputational risk, domestic norms, and leadership commitment—was present in each case. This finding, restricted to their pre-2022 study window, supports treating H&M and Inditex as a meaningfully contrastable pair rather than establishing any claim about their current disclosure behavior. Both firms additionally publish detailed annual sustainability and non-financial disclosures, ensuring adequate documentary data availability for the 2022–2025 period, a window whose final confirmed document set awaits the corpus table developed in Stage 4 but which is expected to capture a period of intensifying EU sustainability-disclosure regulation relevant to institutional-pressure analysis. ### 3.4 Data Analysis

This thesis analyzes its document corpus through directed qualitative content analysis, a deductive approach in which coding categories are established from theory before the data are read, rather than allowed to emerge inductively from the text. Kibiswa (2019), the primary methodological source for this design, characterizes directed content analysis as a procedure in which the researcher draws categories from existing theory to set up the categories that guide the research — here, the coercive, normative, and mimetic pressure categories and the substantive, symbolic, and mixed response categories derived from Institutional Theory in Chapter 2, together with the eight greenwashing-indicator categories drawn from the wider greenwashing literature (Nemes et al., 2022; Spaniol et al., 2024). Directed content analysis is used, in Kibiswa's framing, to test, corroborate, or extend an existing theory's applicability to a context in which it was not originally developed; applying institutional-pressure and legitimacy-based categories to fast-fashion corporate communication is precisely this kind of extension. A short methodological caveat applies throughout this section: several of Kibiswa's specific procedural claims — the exact step sequence and the precise handling of text that does not fit a predefined category — are confirmed only at the level of general principle in the available evidence and await full-text confirmation; the analysis described below therefore follows the general logic Kibiswa establishes rather than asserting a specific numbered procedure as confirmed article content.

The coding process follows the four analytic stages Bengtsson (2016) identifies for qualitative content analysis: decontextualisation, recontextualisation, categorisation, and compilation. In the decontextualisation stage, each document in the corpus is read in full and broken into meaning units — discrete passages that make a claim, commitment, target, or disclosure-relevant statement — rather than coded at the level of isolated words or fixed sentence counts, consistent with the unit-of-analysis definition set out in the codebook (`research/coding/codebook_v1.md`). Recontextualisation follows: each meaning unit is checked against the codebook's a priori categories and, where a unit does not correspond to any predefined category, it is logged as a residual observation rather than forced into an ill-fitting code, preserving the possibility — flagged as plausible but not yet confirmed in Kibiswa's own text — that the coding scheme is refined as unanticipated disclosure patterns are encountered. Categorisation groups coded units along three simultaneous dimensions: institutional-pressure type and corporate-response type (answering RQ1), and greenwashing-indicator and competitive-pressure-signal codes (answering RQ2), following the codebook's explicit rule that a single unit may carry codes on more than one dimension at once. Compilation, the final stage, draws the coded material together into within-case findings for each company and a cross-case comparison, addressing both research questions through the same coding matrix rather than through separate analytic procedures.

This deductive process is further grounded in Elo et al.'s (2014) description of the organization phase of content analysis as the development and application of a categorization matrix against which all data are reviewed for correspondence — the coding matrix set out in the codebook operationalizes exactly this logic, with each row recording the coded passage, its assigned codes, the evidence supporting each code, and any counter-evidence found in the same document. Bengtsson's (2016) distinction between manifest content ("what has been said," the visible surface of a claim) and latent content ("what was intended to be said," requiring interpretation of underlying framing) further justifies why coding for corporate response type and greenwashing indicators cannot rest on manifest wording alone: judging whether a response is substantive or symbolic, or whether a disclosure pattern constitutes disproportional emphasis or omission, requires interpreting the relationship between what is stated and what is surrounded, contextualized, or left unstated — a latent-level judgment the codebook's evidence and counter-evidence fields are designed to make transparent and auditable rather than impressionistic.

### 3.5 Trustworthiness and Limitations

Because this thesis uses a qualitative, comparative document analysis rather than a quantitative design, trustworthiness is evaluated using the vocabulary Elo et al. (2014) establish as the current consensus for qualitative research generally: credibility, dependability, confirmability, and transferability. Each criterion is pursued through a specific, describable procedural choice rather than asserted as a general property of the design. Credibility is supported by triangulating across multiple document types per company — annual and sustainability reports, and, where available, press communications and third-party coverage — following Wood, Sebar, and Vecchio's (2020) argument that using multiple sources or document types for triangulation is a specific technique for enhancing the credibility of qualitative document analysis, rather than a claim resting on a single report per firm per year. Dependability and confirmability are pursued jointly through the coding matrix's audit-trail structure: every coded unit records not only the code applied but the specific textual evidence supporting it and any counter-evidence present in the same document, allowing the coding decisions behind the Results chapter to be traced and checked rather than taken on the analyst's authority alone — consistent with Wood et al.'s (2020) broader argument that rigour in document analysis is achieved substantially through explicitness about assumptions and procedures rather than through claims of neutrality. Transferability is necessarily limited by the two-case design: findings describe how institutional and competitive pressures manifest in H&M Group's and Inditex's environmental communication specifically, and are not presented as generalizable to fast fashion as an industry, though the paired-case logic established in Section 3.2 supports analytic (theory-informed) rather than statistical generalization.

A specific and unavoidable limitation of document analysis as a method is the self-reporting bias inherent in corporate-authored sustainability disclosures. Morgan (2022) argues that documents produced by commercial organizations are typically intended to create a positive impression, and that internal actors have an incentive to conceal mistakes, producing a gap between what a document states and what actually occurs inside the organization — though this specific characterization is drawn from search-level evidence rather than a directly confirmed verbatim passage and is treated here with corresponding caution. This is not a peripheral concern for this thesis but a central one: because RQ1 and RQ2 both investigate the possibility that corporate environmental communication diverges from underlying practice, the corpus itself must be read as a strategically constructed artifact rather than a neutral record, which is precisely why the codebook (Section 3.4; `research/coding/codebook_v1.md`) requires counter-evidence logging alongside every applied code and restricts greenwashing-indicator codes to units with an identifiable textual or cross-referential basis, never a tone judgment alone.

Further limitations follow from the state of the evidence base at the time of this draft. The literature review underlying the codebook's theoretical categories was built primarily from abstract- and snippet-level evidence rather than full-text sources (see `resources/source_access_checklist.md`), and the codebook itself remains a first version pending pilot testing on a small sample of matched report sections from both companies (Stage 9), which may refine category boundaries — particularly around the empirically documented entanglement of coercive and mimetic pressure (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023; Manshoven & Van Opstal, 2022) and the practical codeability of the OMISSION and PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING indicators, both of which require a constructed reference point (a materiality matrix or cross-firm comparison) rather than being identifiable from a single passage in isolation. Finally, coding was conducted by a single analyst; without a second coder, inter-coder reliability could not be formally assessed, and the audit-trail approach described above is offered as a partial, transparency-based substitute for that check rather than an equivalent to it.
