### 2.1 Conceptualizing Greenwashing

Definitions of greenwashing converge on a core idea—a mismatch between a firm's environmental communication and its actual performance—while diverging on scope and mechanism. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) characterize it as an organization either falsely promoting its environmental efforts or spending disproportionately more on green promotion than on genuine practice. De Freitas Netto et al. (2020) offer firmer ground: their systematic review establishes greenwashing as the co-occurrence of poor performance and positive, misleading communication, and proposes a fourfold typology crossing level of analysis (firm- versus product-level) with communicative form. Claim greenwashing operates through textual assertions of environmental benefit; executional greenwashing works through non-textual cues such as green color palettes or nature imagery. The typology distinguishes where a misleading impression is located from how it is conveyed, a distinction later chapters draw on when classifying disclosure content.

Where these sources establish what greenwashing is, Delmas and Burbano (2011) address why it occurs. Their foundational framework locates greenwashing at the intersection of poor performance and positive communication, then decomposes its drivers into external (institutional pressure from regulators and NGOs, and market pressure from consumers, investors, and competitors), organizational, and individual levels, the last including psychological tendencies such as narrow framing and optimistic bias. This driver taxonomy is distinct from the definitional typologies above; Sections 2.3 and 2.5 return to its external-institutional and external-market categories.

Whether deceptive intent is definitionally necessary remains contested. Spaniol et al. (2024), synthesizing roughly seventy-nine scholarly definitions, arrive at six constitutive attributes—an environmental claim, made by a private firm, about a product marketed to consumers, that is unsubstantiated, made with deceptive intent, and pursued for competitive advantage—treating intent as required. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) are more ambivalent: they describe greenwashing as a deliberate attempt to mislead, yet elsewhere allow that the symbolic-conformity mechanism can occur "whether intentionally or not." The field has thus not settled whether greenwashing is a discrete deceptive act or a broader, potentially unintentional decoupling between communicated values and practice.

A narrower related construct is selective disclosure. Nemes et al. (2022) treat it as a thematic indicator in their integrated assessment framework, describing partial or insufficiently contextualized statements that emphasize positive performance while omitting negative information. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) similarly distinguish, within communication-level greenwashing, a passive "selective-disclosure" strategy of omission from a more active "misleading-disclosure" strategy of exaggeration or fabrication. Together they position selective disclosure as an asymmetric-emphasis mechanism distinct from outright fabrication.

Separating misleading communication from ordinary positive communication is the field's unresolved crux, sharpened by increasingly forward-looking claims. Gatti et al. (2019) frame this as a "grey zone": because voluntary, unaudited CSR disclosure is not independently verified, firms occupy ambiguous space between legitimate promotion and misleading claims. Montgomery et al. (2024) locate an intensifying instance in a "Greenwash 3.0" stage concerned with net-zero pledges that cannot yet be verified against delivered outcomes. Combined with Spaniol et al.'s (2024) substantiation criterion, this suggests the workable dividing line is evidentiary rather than tonal: positivity alone does not make a claim greenwashing, but its unsubstantiated, selectively emphasized, or not-yet-verifiable character does. The next section turns to the fast-fashion business model, whose structure conditions why such ambiguity arises so persistently.

### 2.2 The Fast-Fashion Business Model and Sustainability Tensions

Fast fashion is best defined not as a product category but as a business logic built on compressed cycle time, high-volume output, low unit price, and geographically dispersed sourcing. Bick et al. (2018) characterize the model through the sale of inexpensive, rapidly produced clothing that has driven a sharp rise in garment consumption, while Dzhengiz et al. (2023) show that speed-to-market has intensified markedly and global clothing production has nearly doubled in fifteen years. Wren (2022) situates this within its climate footprint, attributing roughly half of the sector's 2018 greenhouse-gas emissions of about 2.1 billion metric tons to fast fashion specifically. Together these sources show that fast fashion's advantage to consumers—speed and affordability—is generated by the same mechanisms of volume and turnover that create its environmental burden, not by a separable, correctable defect.

This logic places the model in persistent tension with sustainability claims. Bick et al. (2018) argue the costs are unevenly distributed, with water-intensive cultivation, untreated-dye pollution, and hazardous labor conditions concentrated in low- and middle-income manufacturing countries while consumption concentrates in the Global North—separating where impact is generated from where it is narrated. Manshoven and Van Opstal (2022) add that firms face a prisoner's-dilemma logic in global value chains, in which no single firm can unilaterally absorb the cost of deeper circularity without a competitive penalty, so even willing actors gravitate toward regulation as a shared solution. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) likewise describe an industry under sustained public scrutiny, in need of more circular practice.

A further tension concerns whether improvement on a single indicator reduces total impact. Dzhengiz et al. (2023) suggest firms in the fast and ultra-fast segment adopt "eco-efficiency" framings—improvements in per-unit measures such as recycled-fiber share or emissions per garment—while overall production volume, and therefore absolute impact, keeps rising, linking this to a risk of decoupling between language and action and illustrating it with an ultra-fast entrant facing greenwashing accusations. Inês et al. (2023) offer a complementary perspective, framing environmental responsibility as a potential competitive advantage across supply chains while cautioning that greenwashing risk here is a structural supply-chain problem rather than merely a communication failure.

Two studies moved beyond conceptual argument to content-analyze the sector's disclosures. Garcia-Torres et al. (2017) analyzed the two largest fast-fashion companies' sustainability reporting and found it analytically rich but thin on reported action, producing "orphan issues" in which materially important topics receive disproportionately little follow-through. Feng and Ngai (2020), studying twelve global fashion companies including both firms studied here, found CSR reporting comprehensiveness increased industry-wide between 2013 and 2016, with labor and environmental themes predominant. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) add that transparency uptake nonetheless remained markedly uneven across major retailers despite shared external pressure—indicating a common environment does not produce uniform disclosure, which motivates turning next to institutional pressures.

### 2.3 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Environmental Communication

Institutional theory, in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) foundational formulation, explains organizational conformity to sustainability expectations through three distinct pressures: coercive, arising from dependence on other organizations and the need for legitimacy with regulators and resource-holders; normative, arising from professionalization and the diffusion of shared standards; and mimetic, whereby firms imitate peers perceived as legitimate under conditions of uncertainty rather than as general competitive benchmarking. Delmas and Burbano's (2011) external-driver taxonomy maps onto this triad's coercive and normative categories, treating regulators and NGOs as distinct external pressures. Latif et al. (2020) provide the clearest validated operationalization: modeling coercive, normative, and mimetic pressure as separately measurable constructs, they find each independently predicts adoption of environmental management accounting among manufacturing firms. Although their survey-based Pakistani setting is far from document analysis of European fast fashion, the finding demonstrates that the triad is empirically tractable rather than purely heuristic, supporting treating the three pressures as separately codeable categories. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) situate this typology within the greenwashing literature, arguing firms respond to coercive, mimetic, and normative pressures by adopting communication strategies intended to secure legitimacy.

Coercive pressure interacts temporally with mimetic dynamics rather than operating in isolation. Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023) trace non-financial disclosure among Spanish firms across the transposition of EU Directive 2014/95/EU, finding disclosure increased in the anticipatory period before the law took effect—particularly among environmentally sensitive sectors and previously lagging reporters—before reversing after enactment for all but already-leading firms, which they read as mimetic and normative isomorphism operating ahead of coercive regulation. A structurally comparable, sector-specific dynamic emerges from Manshoven and Van Opstal (2022), whose stakeholder survey across the European fashion system finds broad support, including from industry, for mandatory circularity regulation, justified through a prisoner's-dilemma logic in which a regulatory "level playing field" is preferred over voluntary first-moving. Read together, mimetic and coercive pressures are frequently entangled rather than cleanly separable in practice.

Within fast fashion specifically, Fraser and van der Ven (2022) offer the most direct account of how institutional pressure translates unevenly into response. Comparing H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing, they find sustainability scandals are necessary but insufficient for increased transparency, becoming effective only alongside senior-management commitment and alignment with domestic norms; where only one condition holds, as in Gap and Inditex, transparency did not shift, whereas H&M's combination of scandal exposure, favorable institutions, and leadership commitment produced higher transparency. Identical exposure does not guarantee identical response, because internal organizational agency mediates whether pressure becomes genuine change or symbolic conformity. Gatti et al. (2019) argue a wholly voluntary CSR regime is a "grey zone" that facilitates greenwashing, proposing combined voluntary-mandatory governance and implying coercive pressure alone is not self-enforcing. Consistent with this, Papa et al. (2024) find mandatory EU non-financial reporting coincided with improved narrative disclosure without matching improvement in most environmental KPIs, and Forliano et al. (2025) describe decoupling as symbolic communication accompanied by minimal substantive change, corroborating institutional and legitimacy theory as the dominant lenses for this divergence.

Notably, no source in this corpus documents institutional-pressure indicators observable inside the content of sustainability reports themselves, as distinct from survey instruments (Latif et al., 2020), disclosure-level indices (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023), or talk-versus-KPI proxies (Papa et al., 2024)—an acknowledged gap this thesis's directed content analysis must address methodologically. The next section turns to legitimacy theory and decoupling to examine how institutional pressure is converted into substantive or merely symbolic communication.

### 2.4 Legitimacy, Symbolic Responses, and Environmental Decoupling

Legitimacy Theory supplies this thesis's principal supporting concept, treating environmental communication as a resource through which firms align with prevailing societal expectations rather than as a neutral report of underlying performance. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) situate legitimacy theory alongside institutional, stakeholder, and signaling theories, framing legitimacy management as securing or safeguarding a "social license to operate" through disclosure practices rather than demonstrable changes in performance. This thesis uses the term legitimacy gap as a working label for that same idea: the space between the environmental image a firm's communication projects and what its underlying practice can substantiate—the gap that widens whenever that social license comes under strain. The label is adopted for convenience rather than attributed to any corpus source; it names a divergence that Bernini and La Rosa (2024) and Forliano et al. (2025) below already describe without using the phrase. Forliano et al. (2025), in their field-level mapping, find legitimacy theory ranks among the dominant macro-level frameworks across the ninety-seven studies reviewed, alongside institutional and stakeholder theories. The classic Suchman-style distinction between legitimacy-seeking, -maintaining, and -repairing strategies, however, is not established by any corpus source—a possible reference surfaces only as an unconfirmed, likely misattributed mention linked to Montgomery et al. (2024)—so this thesis refers to legitimacy management only in the general terms its cited sources substantiate.

The mechanism most consistently linked to legitimacy-driven communication is decoupling, a divergence between formally adopted environmental policy and its operational implementation. Papa et al. (2024), examining Polish listed firms under EU non-financial reporting, find improvements in qualitative, narrative disclosure were not matched by gains in measurable environmental KPIs outside carbon emissions, indicating coercive pressure can produce a "talk" response without a commensurate "action" response, unevenly across environmental sub-dimensions. Loko and Schiehll (2025) extend this logic into an explicit measurement framework distinguishing internally oriented ESG actions from externally oriented endorsement activity across a large, cross-industry sample; they find policy-practice misalignment affects over four-fifths of sampled firms and persists across the panel, though recent years show some tendency to prioritize internal action over external endorsement. Neither examines fast fashion, limiting direct transferability, but together they anchor policy-practice decoupling as both theoretically expected and empirically demonstrable at scale.

This decoupling logic converges with a broader concern with the gap between environmental communication and performance. Montgomery et al. (2024) chart greenwash scholarship across three phases culminating in a concern with forward-looking narratives, identifying selective disclosure and symbolic commitment without substantive follow-through as persistent mechanisms. Nemes et al. (2022) operationalize related concerns through named criteria—selective disclosure, vagueness, and lack of substantiation—which this thesis adopts directly as anchors for its own codebook of greenwashing indicators. Janik and Ryszko (2026) reinforce this, pointing to decoupling between symbol and substance as one recurring manifestation among the content-analysis-based approaches used to detect divergence between disclosure and organizational reality. These communication-performance-gap concerns are frequently intertwined with competitive dynamics, to which the discussion now turns.

### 2.5 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure

Where Section 2.4 situated legitimacy-seeking as the dominant motive behind environmental communication, the literature also identifies a countervailing dynamic: firms in competitive product markets do not necessarily disclose more as legitimacy pressure intensifies, but may instead disclose selectively. Montgomery et al. (2024) treat selective disclosure as among the most persistent and well-documented forms of greenwashing, defining it as the omission or downplaying of unfavorable information alongside emphasis on favorable claims. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) sharpen this by distinguishing, within communication-level greenwashing, a passive-deception mode built on omission ("selective disclosure") from an active-deception mode built on exaggeration ("misleading disclosure"), analytically useful for separating what firms leave out from what they overstate. Forliano et al. (2025), mapping ninety-seven peer-reviewed studies, corroborate that legitimacy and institutional theory remain the field's dominant macro-level lenses and that decoupling—symbolic communication paired with minimal substantive change—recurs as the mechanism linking institutional pressure to disclosure behavior.

The clearest evidence that competition itself shapes disclosure comes from Lee and Lee (2022), whose study of South Korean listed firms found a negative relationship between industry competition and voluntary greenhouse-gas disclosure: as rivalry intensifies, managers reduce disclosure to protect competitively sensitive information—a proprietary-cost mechanism that governance quality moderated but did not eliminate. This complicates any assumption that competitive rivalry produces a straightforward "race to the top" in sustainability reporting. Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou (2016) offer a larger-scale precedent: in a panel of 4,750 firms across 45 countries, more environmentally damaging firms are, if anything, less likely to selectively disclose once national scrutiny and global normative exposure are sufficiently intense—scrutiny and norms functioning empirically as constraints on selective disclosure rather than enablers. Read alongside Lee and Lee's (2022) proprietary-cost finding, this suggests selective disclosure is best understood as a default tendency that external scrutiny and diffused norms can suppress rather than a fixed firm-level trait. Competitive dynamics also operate through imitation: Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023) documented mimetic disclosure convergence toward sector leaders before non-financial-disclosure regulation took effect, followed by reversal for laggard firms once compliance became coercive. Peer-driven convergence and competitive withholding are not contradictory but can coexist—firms may imitate rivals' disclosure framing and headline commitments while withholding the granular detail that would carry competitive cost, a pattern consistent with Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) finding that fast-fashion firms frequently mention Sustainable Development Goals yet provide little target-level substantiation, with independent media coverage surfacing negative practices absent from those reports.

Within fast fashion specifically, Garcia-Torres et al. (2017) content-analyzed H&M's and Inditex's own reporting and found it richly descriptive but comparatively thin on reported action, producing "orphan issues"—material topics a company's own assessment identifies as important yet that receive disproportionately little disclosed follow-through (in one example, innovation emerged as the most material issue while accounting for a reported 3.54% of coded action statements). This action-level thinness is a different axis from Feng and Ngai's (2020) contemporaneous finding that reporting comprehensiveness—the breadth of issues addressed—was rising across the sector's largest firms, including both cases, over roughly the same pre-2022 period: a report can cover more topics while following through on any one only thinly, which is precisely the gap this thesis's directed content analysis is designed to trace. This sets up the international-business dimension examined next.

### 2.6 International Business and Global Value Chains

The fragmentation of production across borders is not merely a logistical feature of the fast-fashion model; it is a structural condition shaping what a brand-owner can know, and choose to reveal, about its own supply chain. Asmussen et al. (2023) modeled this formally, showing that in globally dispersed value chains a supplier's CSR breaches "may be revealed by the MNE, external agents, or remain hidden," building information asymmetry directly into the lead-firm–supplier relationship rather than treating it as incidental. Bick et al. (2018) offered a complementary, geographically grounded account of the same asymmetry: consumption concentrates in the Global North while chemically intensive manufacturing and waste-disposal stages are outsourced to the Global South, where environmental regulation is comparatively weaker and less consistently enforced. Together they suggest the multinational-supplier relationship is not only economically but also jurisdictionally uneven, supplying the structural opportunity for the selective-disclosure patterns this thesis investigates in RQ2.

Within that asymmetric relationship, responsibility for sustainability outcomes is bargained rather than fixed. Asmussen et al.'s (2023) coalitional model indicates large multinationals are incentivized to protect their "social brand" from reputational contagion originating at supplier sites—a plausible economic mechanism beneath the legitimacy-seeking discussed earlier—and points to a monitoring paradox whereby increased supplier oversight, if unaccompanied by a coherent strategy, can weaken the MNE's bargaining position rather than strengthen substantive compliance, suggesting why audit and certification commitments may function symbolically rather than substantively. Inês et al.'s (2023) systematic review situates greenwashing as a supply-chain-management problem, proposing drivers spanning supplier relationships, certification and reporting mechanisms, and leadership commitment; its treatment of the specific question of brand–supplier information asymmetry is best read as general framing of where supply-chain greenwashing risk arises rather than confirming evidence on that mechanism.

Regulatory variation across borders compounds this asymmetry. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) found heterogeneous transparency practices among the largest retailers, including H&M and Inditex, explained by the joint presence of scandal-driven reputational risk, home-country institutional norms, and senior-management commitment, with the absence of any single condition sufficient to explain lower uptake as in Inditex. Domestic regulatory and normative environments do not travel evenly across a firm's global footprint, reinforcing Bick et al.'s (2018) argument that oversight is comparatively lax at the production end even where stringent in the home market. Taken together, the international-business literature locates the chapter's preceding threads within a globally fragmented, jurisdictionally uneven value chain. The following section draws these strands into the conceptual framework and research gap.

### 2.7 Research Gap and Conceptual Framework

The preceding sections establish that greenwashing is best understood not as a single act but as a family of related mismatches—between claim and performance, policy and practice, emphasis and omission—whose boundary with ordinary promotional communication remains genuinely contested (Gatti et al., 2019; Spaniol et al., 2024; de Freitas Netto et al., 2020). Institutional Theory supplies the explanatory architecture for why firms produce such communication at all: coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures push organizations toward conformity, a triad originating in DiMaggio and Powell (1983), empirically validated by Latif et al. (2020), and traced temporally by Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023), whose evidence of mimetic anticipation preceding coercive enactment shows these pressures interacting rather than operating in isolation. Legitimacy Theory and decoupling explain the mechanism by which conformity is delivered: firms may satisfy institutional pressure symbolically, through narrative disclosure, without matching substantive change, a pattern Papa et al. (2024) and Loko and Schiehll (2025) confirm directly, if outside the fast-fashion sector itself.

Within fast fashion, this general architecture meets a structurally distinct industry. The volume-and-speed logic documented by Bick et al. (2018), Dzhengiz et al. (2023), and Wren (2022) generates a sustainability burden that isolated efficiency gains cannot resolve, and Fraser and van der Ven (2022) demonstrate that identical institutional exposure across H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing produced markedly different transparency outcomes depending on internal leadership commitment. Garcia-Torres et al. (2017), analyzing both firms' reporting, add that disclosure can be descriptively rich yet thin on reported action, while Lee and Lee (2022) show that competitive rivalry independently pushes firms toward withholding rather than volunteering sensitive information, complicating any assumption that legitimacy and competitive pressure pull in the same direction.

The international-business literature extends this by locating the asymmetry structurally: Asmussen et al. (2023) and Bick et al. (2018) show that fragmented, jurisdictionally uneven value chains give lead firms both the opportunity and the incentive to communicate selectively about conditions they only partially observe.

What the literature has not yet done is bring institutional and competitive pressures together within a focused, theory-driven comparison of two closely matched multinationals over a defined recent period. Existing institutional-pressure evidence is single-firm, large-sample, or drawn from non-fashion sectors (Latif et al., 2020; Loko & Schiehll, 2025), and no source documents institutional-pressure indicators observable inside report content itself. Competitive-disclosure evidence is similarly aggregate or single-country (Lee & Lee, 2022), and the only source analyzing H&M and Inditex together, Garcia-Torres et al. (2017), predates the 2022–2025 period of intensifying EU sustainability-disclosure regulation. A comparative, qualitative case study tracing institutional and competitive pressures jointly across H&M Group and Inditex during exactly this period remains absent.

Conceptually, this thesis draws the pressures and mechanisms traced above into a four-step framework—Drivers, IB Filter, Strategy, Outcome—that organizes the analysis as this thesis's own synthesis of the preceding review rather than a model borrowed from a single source. The Drivers are the coercive, normative, and mimetic institutional pressures reviewed in Section 2.3, joined by the competitive, market-based pressures that Lee and Lee (2022) and Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou (2016) document. Before becoming communication, these pass through an IB filter specific to fast fashion's globally fragmented value chains: the jurisdictionally uneven, information-asymmetric buyer-supplier relationships that Asmussen et al. (2023) and Bick et al. (2018) describe, which limit what a lead firm can know, let alone choose to reveal, about conditions in its own supply chain. What emerges is a Strategy—substantive or symbolic response, paired with a selective-disclosure orientation toward omission, disproportional emphasis, or inconsistent metrics—through which a firm manages the legitimacy gap introduced in Section 2.4. The Outcome is the environmental communication actually observed in each firm's reporting, sitting between substantive conformity and residual legitimacy gap. RQ1 traces the Drivers-to-Strategy sequence for institutional pressure specifically; RQ2 traces the same sequence for competitive pressure and the IB filter's disclosure consequences.

In sum, this chapter has traced greenwashing's conceptual boundaries, fast fashion's structural tensions, and the institutional, legitimacy, and competitive mechanisms shaping disclosure, identifying a gap this thesis addresses through paired case comparison. Chapter 3 now sets out the methodology developed to operationalize this framework.
