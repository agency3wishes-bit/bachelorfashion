Abstract

This bachelor's thesis examines the drivers of greenwashing in the fast-fashion industry — specifically how competitive and institutional pressures shape environmental communication within international business — through a comparative case study of H&M Group and Inditex across their 2022–2025 reporting years, a period spanning the European Union's shift from voluntary to mandatory sustainability disclosure. Using Institutional Theory as its primary lens, it asks how coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures produce substantive, symbolic, or mixed corporate conformity, and how selective disclosure functions as a legitimacy-seeking mechanism under market competition and information asymmetry. A directed qualitative content analysis of both firms' company-published annual and sustainability reports produced 237 page-cited coded units across ten disclosure themes. The analysis finds institutional pressure was absorbed through two distinct sequences — rhetorical anticipation preceding structural change at one firm, structural change with comparatively little preceding rhetoric at the other — and that substantive and symbolic conformity resolve more clearly by disclosure theme than by firm. Selective-disclosure patterns appear in both firms' reporting, alongside one directional asymmetry in the withholding of supply-chain wage data. The thesis contributes a theme-level refinement to institutional-conformity classification and a case-based account of selective disclosure under an evolving EU regulatory regime.

1 Introduction

1.1 Background of the Study

Fast fashion has become one of the most visible test cases for the credibility of corporate sustainability communication. The business model rests on compressed design-to-shelf cycles, high-volume output, and low unit prices, sustained by geographically dispersed supply chains; Bick et al. (2018) trace how the widespread availability of inexpensive, rapidly produced clothing has driven a sharp and sustained rise in garment consumption, while the environmental and social costs of production — water-intensive cultivation, chemical pollution, hazardous working conditions — remain concentrated in low- and middle-income manufacturing countries far from the consumer markets in which the clothing is sold. The scale of the model has continued to grow rather than stabilize: Dzhengiz et al. (2023) document a marked intensification of speed-to-market over the past two decades, with global clothing production almost doubling in only fifteen years and an ultra-fast segment accelerating the cycle further. The environmental burden of fast fashion is therefore not an incidental defect of the model but a product of the same volume-and-turnover logic that constitutes its competitive advantage — a structural tension that any sustainability claim made by a fast-fashion firm must ultimately confront.

Against this backdrop, the industry's sustainability communication has expanded considerably, and with it the concern that such communication may outrun the underlying practice. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) describe a fashion industry under sustained public scrutiny, in which the promotion of environmental credentials has become commercially important while the gap between promoted image and production reality remains difficult for outsiders to assess — the condition under which greenwashing concerns arise. Notably, exposure to this shared scrutiny has not produced uniform corporate behavior: Fraser and van der Ven (2022), comparing the transparency practices of the largest fast-fashion retailers, find that disclosure uptake varies markedly even among firms facing broadly similar external pressure, which suggests that how a firm communicates about sustainability is a corporate response worth studying in its own right rather than a mechanical reflection of its environment. A vivid instance of this sits at product level: H&M's "Conscious" collection label and Inditex's group-wide "Join Life" label (applied across its brands, including Zara) both promise a higher share of preferred or lower-impact materials, yet the two labels took very different paths over the study period — one retired under external pressure, the other still reporting a compliance figure that beats its own target, as Chapters 4 and 5 show in full.

The years 2022 to 2025 make this question unusually well defined, because they span a rapid hardening of the European regulatory environment within which both firms studied here are headquartered. The EU Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (European Commission, 2022, COM(2022) 141 final) announced a sector-specific policy agenda in March 2022; the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, Directive (EU) 2022/2464), which entered into force in January 2023, began converting voluntary sustainability reporting into a mandated, standardized disclosure regime through the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), applying to large EU companies from fiscal year 2024; and a proposed Green Claims Directive (COM(2023) 166 final), tabled in March 2023, signaled forthcoming substantiation requirements for environmental marketing claims — though its status became contested toward the end of the study window, with the European Commission indicating in June 2025 that it might withdraw the proposal amid opposition to its verification requirements, a development whose outcome remains unresolved at the time of writing and is noted here only as part of the regulatory backdrop, not as a settled fact about the Directive's final form. Enforcement activity reached the sector directly within the window as well: in September 2022 the Dutch Authority for Consumers and Markets concluded a commitment decision concerning H&M's sustainability claims (ACM, 2022). The study period thus straddles the transition from a largely voluntary to an increasingly mandatory disclosure regime — a setting in which how the industry's two largest firms, H&M Group and Inditex, communicated about their environmental performance can be observed before, during, and after the regime change. That observation task defines the research problem to which the next section turns.

1.2 Research Problem and Questions

The core analytical problem is that the line between legitimate positive communication and misleading communication is genuinely difficult to draw. Gatti et al. (2019) characterize voluntary, largely unaudited CSR disclosure as a "grey zone" in which firms occupy an ambiguous space between promotion and deception, and argue that this ambiguity is a structural feature of a voluntary regime rather than a failure of individual firms — which is precisely why the ongoing voluntary-to-mandatory transition described above matters analytically. At the same time, the greenwashing literature has grown into a large, theoretically plural field without resolving how such communication should be detected in practice: Montgomery et al. (2024), reviewing the field, find selective disclosure and symbolic commitment to be among its most persistent concerns and see "no end in sight" to the phenomenon, while Bernini and La Rosa (2024) show that greenwashing research draws on institutional, legitimacy, and related theoretical lenses that link corporate communication to the external pressures firms face.

What this literature has not yet supplied — the gap developed in full in Section 2.7 — is a theory-driven comparison of institutional and competitive pressures operating jointly within the environmental communication of two closely matched fast-fashion multinationals over a defined recent period. Existing evidence on institutional pressures is largely survey-based, large-sample, or drawn from other sectors; evidence on competition and disclosure is similarly aggregate; and the small body of work analyzing H&M and Inditex together predates the 2022–2025 regulatory transition. There is, in short, no focused account of how the same institutional environment and the same competitive rivalry are reflected, and differently absorbed, in the disclosure content of these two firms during exactly the period in which European disclosure rules changed around them.

This thesis addresses that gap through two research questions:

RQ1: How do cross-border coercive, normative, and mimetic institutional pressures shape the environmental communication of H&M Group and Inditex (2022–2025), and how do their corporate responses compare in terms of substantive, symbolic, or mixed conformity?

RQ2: How is selective disclosure (e.g., omission, disproportional emphasis, inconsistent metrics) used as a legitimacy-seeking mechanism in the environmental communication of H&M Group and Inditex (2022–2025), and how do market competition and information asymmetry shape these patterns across the two firms?

Institutional Theory serves as the primary theoretical lens: the triad of coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures structures RQ1 and anchors the analytical framework throughout. Legitimacy Theory, the concept of decoupling between policy and practice, and the construct of selective disclosure are used as supporting concepts — they specify the mechanisms through which institutional pressure may be converted into substantive change, symbolic conformity, or selectively framed communication, but they do not displace Institutional Theory as the organizing framework.

1.3 Objectives, Scope, and Contribution

The objective of this thesis is to analyze and compare the environmental communication of H&M Group and Inditex across their 2022–2025 reporting years, in order to answer RQ1 and RQ2. The study is designed as a qualitative comparative multiple-case study based on document analysis. Its empirical material consists of the two firms' company-published annual and sustainability reporting for all four fiscal years — six H&M Group documents and the sustainability statements embedded in four Inditex Group Annual Reports — analyzed through directed qualitative content analysis using a deductive codebook derived from the theoretical framework. The codebook operationalizes both the institutional-pressure and response categories required by RQ1 and a set of greenwashing indicators (among them omission, disproportional emphasis, unclear boundary, and inconsistent metric) required by RQ2; every reported finding is anchored to a specific document, year, and page. Chapter 3 sets out this design, the case-selection logic, and the document corpus in detail, including the comparability caveats that follow from the two firms' non-identical fiscal calendars.

The scope of the thesis is deliberately bounded, and the boundary should be stated at the outset. The analysis concerns communication patterns and disclosure quality in corporate documents: what the two firms state, emphasize, omit, quantify, and leave unquantified in their published reporting. It does not assert legal wrongdoing, fraud, or deceptive intent on the part of either company, and no passage is treated as greenwashing merely because it sounds promotional — a coding is applied only where a specific, evidence-based indicator is met, and all claims about the companies remain within what the coded documentary material supports. Where external assessments by regulators or NGOs are referenced, their evaluative claims are attributed to their authors rather than adopted as findings of this thesis. Equally, the study examines disclosure, not underlying environmental or social performance: a finding that a topic is thinly documented is a finding about reporting practice, not about outcomes at either firm.

Because this introduction is written with the analysis complete, the thesis's contribution can be stated concretely rather than speculatively, though its full development belongs to later chapters. Three results give the comparison its analytical payoff. First, the substantive-versus-symbolic distinction at the center of RQ1 turns out to discriminate at the level of disclosure themes rather than whole firms — each company is substantive in some domains and symbolic in others, in near mirror-image patterns — a resolution-level refinement to how institutional conformity is typically classified. Second, the two firms absorbed the same coercive regulatory pressure through visibly different sequences, one rhetorical before structural and the other structural with little preceding rhetoric. Third, on RQ2, the analysis identifies page-cited selective-disclosure patterns in both firms' reporting, together with one directional asymmetry in the withholding of supply-chain wage data that is consistent with competitive-pressure explanations in the literature. These findings are developed with full documentary evidence in Chapter 4 and interpreted against the theoretical framework in Chapter 5; they are previewed here only to indicate where the thesis ends up.

The remainder of the thesis proceeds in five chapters. Chapter 2 reviews the literature on greenwashing, the fast-fashion business model, institutional pressures, legitimacy and decoupling, competitive disclosure dynamics, and global value chains, and closes by specifying the research gap and conceptual framework. Chapter 3 presents the methodology: the comparative case-study design, case selection, document corpus, directed content-analysis procedure, and the study's trustworthiness measures and limitations. Chapter 4 reports the empirical results for each firm and across the two cases, without interpretation. Chapter 5 discusses those results through the institutional-theoretical framework, answering RQ1 and RQ2 and drawing out theoretical and practical implications. Chapter 6 concludes. In sum, this chapter has situated the study in the structural tension between fast fashion's business model and its sustainability communication, motivated the 2022–2025 window through the European regulatory transition, and stated the research questions, scope, and contribution; Chapter 2 now builds the conceptual foundation on which the analysis rests, beginning with the contested definition of greenwashing itself.

2 Literature Review

2.1 Conceptualizing Greenwashing

Definitions of greenwashing in the literature converge on a core idea while diverging sharply on scope and mechanism. At the most general level, greenwashing is understood as a mismatch between a firm's environmental communication and its actual environmental performance or practice; Adamkiewicz et al. (2022), for instance, characterize it as an organization either falsely promoting its environmental efforts or spending disproportionately more on green promotion than on genuinely sustainable practice, though this framing rests on partially verified evidence and is best treated as one converging definition among several rather than a settled formulation. De Freitas Netto et al. (2020) offer firmer, more structurally developed ground: their systematic review establishes greenwashing as the co-occurrence of poor environmental performance and positive, misleading environmental communication, and proposes a fourfold typology that crosses level of analysis (firm-level versus product-level) with communicative form (claim versus executional). Claim greenwashing operates through explicit or implicit textual assertions of environmental benefit, whereas executional greenwashing works through suggestive non-textual cues such as green color palettes or nature imagery, without any accompanying substantiated claim. This typology matters analytically because it distinguishes where a misleading impression is located (the whole firm or a single product) from how it is conveyed (words versus visual association), a distinction that later chapters draw on when classifying disclosure content.

Where Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) and de Freitas Netto et al. (2020) establish what greenwashing is, Delmas and Burbano (2011) address a distinct question: why it occurs. Their earlier, foundational framework locates greenwashing at the intersection of poor environmental performance and positive environmental communication, then decomposes its drivers into three levels — external (institutional pressure from regulators and NGOs, and market pressure from consumers, investors, and competitors), organizational, and individual (psychological tendencies such as narrow framing, hyperbolic discounting, and optimistic bias). This driver-level taxonomy is a distinct contribution from the definitional typologies above, not a restatement of them, and this chapter returns to its external-institutional and external-market categories in Sections 2.3 and 2.5 respectively, when situating institutional and competitive pressure specifically.

Whether deceptive intent is a necessary element of the definition remains contested rather than resolved. Spaniol et al. (2024) take an explicit position: their concept analysis, synthesizing roughly seventy-nine existing scholarly definitions, arrives at six constitutive attributes — an environmental claim, made by a private-sector firm, about a product or service marketed to consumers, that is unsubstantiated, made with deceptive intent, and pursued for competitive advantage — treating intent as definitionally required. Bernini and La Rosa (2024), by contrast, are internally ambivalent on this point: their integrative framework at times describes greenwashing as a corporation's deliberate attempt to mislead stakeholders, yet elsewhere, in discussing decoupling, allows that the symbolic-conformity mechanism underlying greenwashing can occur "whether intentionally or not." This unresolved tension suggests the literature has not settled whether greenwashing should be read as a discrete deceptive act or as a broader, potentially unintentional decoupling between communicated values and organizational practice.

A related, narrower construct is selective disclosure. Nemes et al. (2022) list selective disclosure as a formally assigned keyword and thematic indicator of their integrated assessment framework, describing it as partial, incomplete, or insufficiently contextualized statements that emphasize positive environmental or social performance while omitting negative information. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) similarly distinguish, within communication-level greenwashing, a "selective-disclosure" strategy of passive omission from a more actively deceptive "misleading-disclosure" strategy of exaggeration or fabrication, though the exact provenance of this pairing requires further verification. Taken together, the evidence, while not fully confirmed at the level of exact wording, points toward selective disclosure functioning as an asymmetric-emphasis mechanism distinct from outright fabrication.

Separating misleading communication from ordinary positive corporate communication is the conceptual crux the field has not fully resolved, and this difficulty is sharpened by an increasingly forward-looking form of the claims themselves. Gatti et al. (2019) frame the boundary problem directly through the notion of a "grey zone": because voluntary, unaudited CSR disclosure is not independently verified, firms occupy an ambiguous space between legitimate promotional communication and misleading claims, making a bright-line distinction analytically difficult. Montgomery et al. (2024) locate a specific and intensifying instance of this same ambiguity in what they describe as a "Greenwash 3.0" stage of the field's evolution, concerned with forward-looking narratives such as net-zero pledges that cannot yet be verified against delivered outcomes; this framing rests on a single source at the level of direct evidence available here and should be read as tentative rather than fully established, but it fits naturally within Gatti et al.'s grey-zone logic, since an unverifiable future claim is, definitionally, a claim whose truth value cannot yet be evidentially settled. This grey-zone framing, combined with Spaniol et al.'s (2024) substantiation criterion, suggests that the more workable dividing line is evidentiary rather than tonal: a claim's positivity alone does not make it greenwashing, but its unsubstantiated, selectively emphasized, or — in the case of forward-looking pledges — not-yet-verifiable character does. Having established this conceptual and definitional groundwork, the following section turns to the fast-fashion business model itself, whose structural characteristics condition why such disclosure ambiguity arises so persistently in this sector.

2.2 The Fast-Fashion Business Model and Sustainability Tensions

Fast fashion is most usefully defined not as a product category but as a business logic built on compressed cycle time, high-volume output, low unit price, and geographically dispersed sourcing. Bick et al. (2018) characterized the model through the widespread sale of inexpensive, rapidly produced clothing that has driven a sharp and sustained rise in garment consumption, while Dzhengiz et al. (2023) demonstrated that speed-to-market has intensified markedly over the past two decades and that global clothing production has almost doubled in only fifteen years. Wren (2022) situated this expansion within its climate footprint, attributing roughly half of the fashion sector's 2018 greenhouse-gas emissions of approximately 2.1 billion metric tons to fast fashion specifically, alongside high volumes of synthetic, petroleum-based garments manufactured predominantly in developing countries. Read together, these sources converge on a single structural picture: the competitive advantage fast fashion offers to consumers—speed and affordability—is generated by the same mechanisms of volume and turnover that create its environmental burden, rather than by a separable, correctable production defect.

This volume-and-speed logic places the business model in persistent tension with sustainability claims. Bick et al. (2018) argued that the environmental and social costs of this model are unevenly distributed, with water-intensive cultivation, untreated-dye pollution, and hazardous labor conditions concentrated in low- and middle-income manufacturing countries while consumption remains concentrated in the Global North—an asymmetry that separates where impact is generated from where it is narrated. Manshoven and Van Opstal (2022) added a further structural dimension: their stakeholder research suggested, albeit at the level of sector-wide survey evidence rather than firm-specific analysis, that individual companies face something resembling a prisoner's-dilemma logic in global value chains, in which no single firm can unilaterally absorb the cost of deeper circularity without a competitive penalty, so that even willing actors gravitate toward regulation as a shared solution rather than voluntary first-moving. Adamkiewicz et al. (2022) likewise pointed to an industry under sustained public scrutiny, in need of more circular and conscientious practice, though the precise theoretical grounding of this claim could not be fully verified and is treated here as background motivation rather than an established causal account.

A further, more specific tension concerns whether improvements on a single sustainability indicator translate into reduced total environmental impact. The evidence here is more tentative. Dzhengiz et al. (2023) suggested that firms operating within the fast and ultra-fast segment can adopt "eco-efficiency" framings—improvements in per-unit measures such as recycled-fiber share or emissions per garment—while overall production volume, and therefore absolute impact, continues to rise; the authors linked this pattern to a risk of decoupling between communicated language and underlying action, illustrating it with the case of an ultra-fast entrant facing greenwashing accusations. Because this argument rests on partial, not fully verified, textual evidence, it should be read as an indicative rather than confirmed feature of the literature: it points toward a plausible rebound-type logic—relative gains coexisting with absolute growth—without establishing this as a settled finding. Inês et al. (2023) offered a complementary, if similarly cautious, perspective, framing environmental responsibility as a potential source of competitive advantage across supply chains while cautioning that greenwashing risk in this context is a structural supply-chain problem rather than merely a communication failure.

Two sources in this literature moved beyond conceptual argument to content-analyze the sustainability disclosures of the industry in which H&M Group and Inditex operate, and their findings on the sector—not on this thesis's own cases—are informative background. Garcia-Torres et al. (2017) analyzed the sustainability reporting of the two largest fast-fashion companies and found that such reporting tends to be analytically rich but comparatively thin on reported action, producing "orphan issues" in which materially important topics receive disproportionately little disclosed follow-through. Feng and Ngai (2020), studying a larger sample of twelve global fashion companies that included both firms studied here, found that CSR reporting comprehensiveness increased industry-wide between 2013 and 2016, with labor and environmental themes predominant and rising attention to human rights and resource use, though only at an aggregate, industry-level resolution rather than firm by firm. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) added that transparency uptake nonetheless remained markedly uneven across major fast-fashion retailers despite shared external pressure, indicating that a common sustainability environment does not produce uniform disclosure behavior. This unevenness in how otherwise similarly positioned firms respond to a shared structural tension is precisely what motivates turning next to institutional pressures as an explanatory lens.

2.3 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Environmental Communication

Institutional theory, in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) foundational formulation, explains organizational conformity to environmental and sustainability expectations through three analytically distinct pressures: coercive pressure, arising from formal and informal dependence on other organizations and from a firm's need to secure legitimacy with regulators and resource-holders; normative pressure, arising from professionalization — the diffusion of shared standards through formal education and cross-organizational professional networks; and mimetic pressure, whereby firms imitate peers perceived as successful or legitimate specifically under conditions of uncertainty about how best to respond, rather than as general competitive benchmarking. Delmas and Burbano's (2011) external-driver taxonomy (Section 2.1) maps closely onto this triad's coercive and normative categories, treating regulators and NGOs respectively as distinct external pressures on greenwashing-relevant behavior — a complementary, independent anchor for the same distinction. Latif et al. (2020) provide the corpus's clearest validated operationalization of this triad: modeling coercive, normative, and mimetic pressure as separately measurable constructs, they find that each one independently and significantly predicts the adoption of environmental management accounting among manufacturing firms. Although their quantitative, survey-based design in a Pakistani manufacturing context is far removed from qualitative document analysis of European fast-fashion disclosures, the finding is nonetheless useful precedent: it demonstrates that the coercive/normative/mimetic distinction is empirically tractable rather than a purely heuristic typology, which supports treating the three pressures as separately codeable categories later in this thesis's own framework. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) situate this typology more directly within the greenwashing literature, suggesting, albeit at the level of a conceptual synthesis, that firms respond to coercive, mimetic, and normative pressures from regulators, competitors, and societal expectations by adopting communication strategies intended to secure legitimacy, a claim that must be read with appropriate caution given its partial evidentiary basis but which nonetheless anchors institutional theory as the dominant lens for explaining sustainability-disclosure behavior.

Regulatory (coercive) pressure has been shown to interact temporally with mimetic dynamics rather than operating in isolation. Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023) trace non-financial disclosure among Spanish firms across the transposition of EU Directive 2014/95/EU, finding that disclosure increased in the anticipatory period before the law took effect, particularly among environmentally sensitive sectors and previously lagging reporters, before the trend reversed after enactment for all but already-leading firms; the authors read this pattern as evidence of the prevalence of mimetic and normative isomorphism operating ahead of, and interacting with, coercive regulation. A structurally comparable, sector-specific dynamic emerges from Manshoven and Van Opstal (2022), whose stakeholder survey across the European fashion system finds broad support, including from industry itself, for mandatory circularity regulation, justified through what respondents describe as a prisoner's-dilemma logic: no single firm can unilaterally absorb the cost of going beyond compliance without suffering a competitive penalty, so a regulatory "level playing field" is preferred over voluntary first-moving. This source does not itself invoke institutional-theory vocabulary, and its relevance here is best treated as corroborating, fashion-sector-specific evidence of a mimetic-competitive trap rather than a theory-grounded claim in its own right; read alongside Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., it suggests that mimetic and coercive pressures are frequently entangled rather than cleanly separable in practice.

Within fast fashion specifically, Fraser and van der Ven (2022) offer the corpus's most direct empirical account of how institutional pressure translates unevenly into corporate response. Comparing H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing, they find that sustainability scandals are a necessary but insufficient condition for increased transparency, becoming effective only when accompanied by senior-management commitment and alignment with domestic norms about appropriate corporate conduct; where only one condition is present, as in Gap and Inditex, transparency practices did not shift, whereas H&M's combination of scandal exposure, favorable domestic institutions, and leadership commitment produced comparatively higher transparency. This finding, reported here as the authors' own, is the section's central conceptual payoff: identical exposure to institutional pressure does not guarantee identical corporate response, because internal organizational agency mediates whether pressure is absorbed as genuine change or deflected into more limited conformity. That mediation is precisely what separates substantive change from symbolic conformity. Gatti et al. (2019) argue that a wholly voluntary CSR regime constitutes a "grey zone" that facilitates greenwashing, proposing a shift toward combined voluntary-mandatory governance as a remedy — implying that coercive pressure alone is not self-enforcing. Consistent with this, Papa et al. (2024) find, with appropriate hedging given the partial nature of the evidence, that mandatory EU non-financial reporting coincided with improved narrative disclosure without a matching improvement in most environmental KPIs, suggesting decoupling persists unevenly across environmental sub-dimensions even under coercive regulation. Forliano et al. (2025) similarly describe decoupling, at a field-mapping level, as symbolic communication accompanied by minimal substantive change, corroborating institutional and legitimacy theory as the dominant lenses for this divergence.

It should be noted, however, that no source in this corpus documents institutional-pressure indicators observable inside the content of sustainability reports themselves, as distinct from survey instruments (Latif et al.), disclosure-level indices (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.), or talk-versus-KPI proxies (Papa et al.); this is an acknowledged gap in the literature that this thesis's own directed content analysis must address methodologically, rather than a question the existing scholarship already resolves. The next section turns to legitimacy theory and decoupling to examine more closely the mechanism through which institutional pressure is converted into substantive or merely symbolic corporate communication.

2.4 Legitimacy, Symbolic Responses, and Environmental Decoupling

Legitimacy Theory supplies this thesis's principal supporting concept, treating environmental communication as a resource through which firms align themselves with prevailing societal expectations rather than as a neutral report of underlying performance. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) situate legitimacy theory alongside institutional, stakeholder, and signaling theories as one of several lenses through which greenwashing research approaches corporate disclosure; their evidence, while not confirmed at the verbatim level, points to legitimacy management as a matter of securing or safeguarding a broadly defined "social license to operate" through disclosure practices rather than through demonstrable changes in environmental performance. This thesis uses the term legitimacy gap as a working label for that same idea: the space between the environmental image a firm's communication projects and what its underlying practice can actually substantiate, and the thing that widens whenever that social license comes under strain. The label is adopted here for convenience rather than attributed to any particular source in the approved corpus — it names a divergence that Bernini and La Rosa (2024), and Forliano et al. (2025) below, already describe without using this exact phrase themselves. Forliano et al. (2025), in their field-level mapping of the greenwashing literature, lend indirect corroboration to Bernini and La Rosa's positioning: their evidence suggests legitimacy theory ranks among the dominant macro-level frameworks used across the ninety-seven studies they reviewed, alongside institutional and stakeholder theories. Taken together, these sources support treating legitimacy-seeking as a general, ongoing communicative orientation rather than a single discrete act; the classic Suchman-style tripartite distinction between legitimacy-seeking, -maintaining, and -repairing strategies, however, is not established by any source in this corpus. A possible reference to such a typology surfaces only as an unconfirmed, likely misattributed mention associated with Montgomery et al.'s (2024) review, so this thesis refers to legitimacy management only in the general terms its cited sources actually substantiate, without adopting the three-part strategy typology as a confirmed analytical category.

The mechanism most consistently linked to legitimacy-driven communication in this literature is decoupling, understood broadly as a divergence between formally adopted environmental policy and its operational implementation. Papa et al. (2024) offer more directly grounded support for this distinction: examining Polish listed firms under EU non-financial reporting regulation, they find that improvements in qualitative, narrative disclosure were not matched by corresponding gains in measurable environmental KPIs outside carbon emissions, indicating that coercive regulatory pressure can produce a "talk" response without a commensurate "action" response, and that this gap appears unevenly across environmental sub-dimensions rather than uniformly. Loko and Schiehll (2025) extend this logic into an explicit measurement framework, distinguishing internally oriented ESG actions from externally oriented endorsement activity across a large, cross-industry sample; they find that policy-practice misalignment affects over four-fifths of sampled firms and persists across the panel period studied, although more recent years show some tendency toward prioritizing internal action over external endorsement. Read together, these two sources anchor policy-practice, means-ends decoupling as both theoretically expected and empirically demonstrable at scale, even though neither examines the fast fashion sector specifically, which limits the direct transferability of their findings to the H&M Group-Inditex comparison while still supporting the general expectation that policy-practice gaps are a plausible, well-documented phenomenon worth coding for.

This decoupling logic converges with a broader concern in the literature with the gap between environmental communication and environmental performance. Montgomery et al. (2024) chart the evolution of greenwash scholarship across three phases, culminating in a concern with forward-looking narratives, and identify selective disclosure and symbolic commitment without substantive follow-through as persistent mechanisms spanning this evolution. Nemes et al.'s (2022) integrated assessment framework operationalizes closely related concerns through named criteria - selective disclosure, vagueness, and lack of substantiation - that this thesis adopts directly as anchors for its own codebook of greenwashing indicators, a debt that is acknowledged explicitly here rather than left implicit. Janik and Ryszko (2026) reinforce this picture, with the evidence pointing to decoupling between symbol and substance as one recurring manifestation among the content-analysis-based measurement approaches used to detect divergence between disclosure and organizational reality. These communication-performance-gap and selective-disclosure concerns, in turn, are frequently intertwined with competitive dynamics among rival firms, to which the discussion now turns.

2.5 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure

Where Section 2.4 situated legitimacy-seeking as the dominant motive behind corporate environmental communication, the literature also identifies a countervailing dynamic: firms operating in competitive product markets do not necessarily disclose more as legitimacy pressure intensifies, but may instead disclose selectively. Montgomery et al. (2024) treat selective disclosure as one of the most persistent and well-documented forms of greenwashing across the field's evolution, defining it broadly as the omission or downplaying of unfavorable information alongside emphasis on favorable claims. Bernini and La Rosa (2024) sharpen this by distinguishing, within communication-level greenwashing, a passive-deception mode built on omission ("selective disclosure") from an active-deception mode built on exaggeration or fabrication ("misleading disclosure"); although the precise attribution of this typology to their own framework rather than to prior literature could not be fully confirmed and is therefore treated cautiously, the distinction is analytically useful for separating what firms leave out from what firms overstate. Forliano et al. (2025), in mapping ninety-seven peer-reviewed studies, corroborate that legitimacy and institutional theory remain the field's dominant macro-level lenses and that decoupling, understood as symbolic communication paired with minimal substantive change, recurs as the mechanism linking institutional pressure to disclosure behavior — a definitional backbone against which competitive dynamics can now be layered.

The clearest evidence that competition itself shapes disclosure comes from Lee and Lee (2022), whose study of South Korean listed firms found a negative relationship between industry competition and the level of voluntary greenhouse-gas disclosure: as rivalry intensifies, managers reduce disclosure to protect competitively sensitive information, a proprietary-cost mechanism that governance quality was found to moderate but not eliminate. This finding complicates any assumption that competitive rivalry between fast-fashion firms produces a straightforward "race to the top" in sustainability reporting; instead, competitive pressure may push firms toward guarding, rather than volunteering, certain categories of information, sitting in direct tension with the legitimacy-seeking incentive to disclose more discussed in the preceding section. Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou (2016) offer a complementary, larger-scale precedent for this same selective-disclosure construct: in a panel of 4,750 firms across 45 countries, they find that more environmentally damaging firms are, if anything, less likely to selectively disclose once national scrutiny and global normative exposure are sufficiently intense — scrutiny and norms function empirically as constraints that limit selective disclosure rather than conditions that enable it. Read alongside Lee and Lee's (2022) single-country proprietary-cost finding, this suggests selective disclosure is best understood as a default tendency that external scrutiny and diffused norms can suppress, rather than a fixed firm-level trait — a large-N, cross-country precedent this thesis's own paired qualitative case comparison is positioned to complement rather than replicate. Competitive dynamics also operate through imitation rather than withholding. Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023) documented mimetic isomorphism among Spanish firms anticipating non-financial-disclosure regulation, showing disclosure convergence toward sector leaders before the law took effect, followed by a reversal for laggard firms once compliance became coercive rather than anticipatory. Read alongside Lee and Lee's proprietary-cost logic, this suggests that peer-driven convergence and competitive withholding are not contradictory but can coexist: firms may imitate rivals' disclosure framing and headline commitments while still withholding the granular detail that would carry competitive cost, a pattern consistent with Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) recent finding that fast-fashion firms frequently mention Sustainable Development Goals in corporate reports yet provide little target-level substantiation, with independent media coverage surfacing negative practices absent from those same reports. Together, these findings indicate that competitive pressure can push environmental communication toward selective rather than comprehensive transparency, complicating the legitimacy-driven expectation of expanding disclosure.

Within the fast-fashion sector specifically, Garcia-Torres et al. (2017) content-analyzed the sustainability reporting of H&M and Inditex themselves and found that both firms' reports were richly descriptive but comparatively thin on reported action, producing what the authors term "orphan issues" — material topics that a company's own materiality assessment identifies as important yet that receive disproportionately little disclosed follow-through. Their illustrative example, in which innovation emerged as the most material issue in a constructed fast-fashion materiality matrix while accounting for a reported 3.54% of coded action statements, offers a concrete, if pre-2022 and evidentially provisional, precedent for exactly the disclosure-versus-materiality gap this thesis's own directed content analysis seeks to trace in the 2022–2025 corpus. This action-level thinness is a different axis from, and so not contradicted by, Feng and Ngai's (2020) contemporaneous industry-wide finding that CSR reporting comprehensiveness — the breadth of issues addressed — was rising across the fashion sector's largest firms, including H&M and Inditex, over roughly the same pre-2022 period: a report can cover more topics over time while still following through on any one of them only thinly, which is precisely the gap this thesis's directed content analysis is designed to trace rather than assume resolved. This sets up the international-business and global-value-chain dimension examined next.

2.6 International Business and Global Value Chains

The fragmentation of production across borders is not merely a logistical feature of the fast-fashion business model; it is also a structural condition that shapes what a brand-owner can know, and choose to reveal, about its own supply chain. Asmussen et al. (2023) modeled this condition formally, showing that in globally dispersed value chains a supplier's CSR breaches "may be revealed by the MNE, external agents, or remain hidden," which builds information asymmetry directly into the bargaining relationship between lead firm and supplier rather than treating it as an incidental oversight. Bick et al. (2018) offered a complementary, geographically grounded account of the same asymmetry: fast-fashion consumption concentrates in the Global North while the chemically intensive manufacturing and waste-disposal stages are increasingly outsourced to the Global South, where environmental regulation is comparatively weaker and less consistently enforced. Read together, these two sources suggest that the multinational-supplier relationship in fast fashion is not only economically but also jurisdictionally uneven, and that this unevenness supplies the structural opportunity for the selective-disclosure patterns this thesis investigates in RQ2.

Within that asymmetric relationship, responsibility for sustainability outcomes is bargained rather than fixed. Asmussen et al.'s (2023) coalitional model indicates, at least at the level of theoretical inference, that large multinationals are incentivized to protect their "social brand" from reputational contagion originating at supplier sites, which offers a plausible economic mechanism underneath the legitimacy-seeking behavior discussed earlier in this chapter. The same model further points to a monitoring paradox, whereby increased supplier oversight, if unaccompanied by a coherent strategy, can weaken the MNE's bargaining position rather than strengthen substantive compliance—an argument that, while resting on formal modeling rather than document-based evidence, is suggestive of why audit and certification commitments in corporate reporting may function as symbolic rather than substantive responses. Inês et al.'s (2023) systematic review situates greenwashing explicitly as a supply-chain-management problem and proposes drivers spanning supplier relationships, certification and reporting mechanisms, and leadership commitment; however, the review's own treatment of the specific question of brand–supplier information asymmetry could not be confirmed as directly addressed in the available evidence, and its contribution here should therefore be read as general framing of where greenwashing risk in supply chains can arise rather than as confirming evidence on the information-asymmetry mechanism itself.

Regulatory variation across borders compounds this asymmetry. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) found that heterogeneous transparency practices among the largest fast-fashion retailers, including H&M and Inditex, are explained by the joint presence of scandal-driven reputational risk, home-country institutional norms, and senior-management commitment, with the absence of any single condition sufficient to explain lower disclosure uptake in cases such as Inditex. This finding indicates that domestic regulatory and normative environments do not travel evenly across a firm's global footprint, reinforcing Bick et al.'s (2018) argument that oversight is comparatively lax at the production end of the chain even where it is stringent in the home market.

Taken together, the international-business literature reviewed in this section extends the chapter's preceding threads—the definitional boundaries of greenwashing, the structural tension of the fast-fashion business model, institutional pressure, legitimacy and decoupling, and competitive disclosure behavior—by locating them within a globally fragmented, jurisdictionally uneven value chain. The following section draws these strands together into the conceptual framework and research gap that guide the present study.

2.7 Research Gap and Conceptual Framework

The preceding sections establish that greenwashing is best understood not as a single act but as a family of related mismatches—between claim and performance, between policy and practice, between what is emphasized and what is omitted—whose boundary with ordinary promotional communication remains genuinely contested (Gatti et al., 2019; Spaniol et al., 2024; de Freitas Netto et al., 2020). Institutional Theory supplies the explanatory architecture for why firms produce such communication at all: coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures push organizations toward conformity with external expectations, a triad originating in DiMaggio and Powell (1983), empirically validated by Latif et al. (2020), and traced temporally by Álvarez-Etxeberria et al. (2023), whose evidence of mimetic anticipation preceding coercive enactment shows these pressures interacting rather than operating in isolation. Legitimacy Theory and decoupling explain the mechanism by which conformity is delivered: firms may satisfy institutional pressure symbolically, through narrative disclosure, without matching substantive change in underlying practice, a pattern Papa et al. (2024) and Loko and Schiehll (2025) confirm directly, if outside the fast-fashion sector itself.

Within fast fashion, this general architecture meets a structurally distinct industry. The volume-and-speed logic documented by Bick et al. (2018), Dzhengiz et al. (2023), and Wren (2022) generates a sustainability burden that cannot be resolved by isolated efficiency gains alone, and Fraser and van der Ven (2022) demonstrate that identical institutional exposure across H&M, Inditex, Gap, and Fast Retailing produced markedly different transparency outcomes depending on internal leadership commitment—evidence that pressure alone does not determine response. Garcia-Torres et al. (2017), analyzing H&M's and Inditex's own reporting, add that disclosure can be descriptively rich yet thin on reported action, while Lee and Lee (2022) show that competitive rivalry independently pushes firms toward withholding rather than volunteering sensitive information, complicating any assumption that legitimacy pressure and competitive pressure pull in the same direction.

The international-business literature extends this picture by locating the asymmetry structurally: Asmussen et al. (2023) and Bick et al. (2018) show that fragmented, jurisdictionally uneven value chains give lead firms both the opportunity and the incentive to communicate selectively about conditions they only partially observe.

What the literature has not yet done, however, is bring institutional and competitive pressures together within a focused, theory-driven comparison of two closely matched multinationals over a defined recent period. Existing institutional-pressure evidence is either single-firm, large-sample, or drawn from non-fashion sectors (Latif et al., 2020; Loko & Schiehll, 2025), and no source documents institutional-pressure indicators observable inside report content itself, as distinct from surveys or disclosure indices. Competitive-disclosure evidence is similarly aggregate or single-country (Lee & Lee, 2022), and the only source analyzing H&M and Inditex together, Garcia-Torres et al. (2017), predates the 2022–2025 period of intensifying EU sustainability-disclosure regulation. A comparative, qualitative case study tracing institutional and competitive pressures jointly across H&M Group and Inditex during exactly this period remains absent.

Conceptually, this thesis draws the pressures and mechanisms traced above into a four-step framework — Drivers, IB Filter, Strategy, Outcome — that organizes the analysis without treating any one step as a citation-bearing construct in its own right; it is this thesis's own synthesis of the preceding review, not an external model borrowed from a single source. The Drivers are the coercive, normative, and mimetic institutional pressures reviewed in Section 2.3, joined by the competitive, market-based pressures that Lee and Lee (2022) and Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou (2016) document. Before any of this becomes communication, it passes through an IB filter specific to fast fashion's globally fragmented value chains: the jurisdictionally uneven, information-asymmetric buyer-supplier relationships that Asmussen et al. (2023) and Bick et al. (2018) describe, which limit what a lead firm can know, let alone choose to reveal, about conditions in its own supply chain. What emerges on the other side is a Strategy — substantive or symbolic response, paired with a selective-disclosure orientation toward omission, disproportional emphasis, or inconsistent metrics — through which a firm manages the legitimacy gap introduced in Section 2.4. The Outcome is simply the environmental communication actually observed in each firm's reporting, sitting somewhere between substantive conformity and residual legitimacy gap. RQ1 traces the Drivers-to-Strategy sequence for institutional pressure specifically; RQ2 traces the same sequence for competitive pressure and the IB filter's disclosure consequences.

In sum, this chapter has traced greenwashing's conceptual boundaries, fast fashion's structural tensions, and the institutional, legitimacy, and competitive mechanisms shaping disclosure, identifying a gap this thesis addresses through paired case comparison. Chapter 3 now sets out the methodology developed to operationalize this framework.

3 Research Methodology

3.1 Research Design

This thesis adopts a qualitative research design to examine how H&M Group and Inditex construct environmental communication under differing configurations of institutional and competitive pressure. A qualitative approach is warranted because the phenomenon under study — the framing, emphasis, and omission patterns within corporate sustainability disclosure — is interpretive by nature and cannot be reduced to a predetermined set of quantifiable indicators without losing the contextual meaning that institutional theory requires the analyst to recover. Bengtsson (2016) frames this interpretive demand directly through the distinction between manifest content, the visible surface structure of what a text states, and latent content, the deeper structure of what a text intends or implies; both, the source establishes, involve interpretation, differing only in depth rather than in kind. This distinction supports treating corporate disclosures not merely as a record of stated commitments but as material whose underlying framing must be read alongside its explicit claims, a premise the present design accepts as foundational.

Case study methodology, and specifically a multiple-case variant, follows from this interpretive orientation and from the bounded, comparative nature of the research problem. Baxter and Jack (2008) is used, within the restricted scope its own card supports, to situate the design within the broader case-study tradition associated with Stake and Yin, and — at the level the evidence permits — to justify examining H&M Group and Inditex together as a means of exploring similarities and differences between two comparable, bounded organizational cases rather than as an aggregated single-case account. This comparative logic aligns with the thesis's aim of contrasting institutional responses and disclosure patterns across two matched fast-fashion multinationals over a shared reporting period, rather than producing a generalisable single-firm narrative.

Documentary research constitutes the primary data-collection strategy, treating corporate sustainability reports, press communications, and related disclosure documents as the empirical record from which meaning units are drawn for analysis. This strategy follows from the qualitative, interpretive commitments already established and positions the documents themselves, rather than interview or survey data, as the site where institutional pressures and disclosure choices become observable.

The analytic approach adopted is directed qualitative content analysis, a deductive method in which coding categories are derived in advance from an established theoretical framework rather than allowed to emerge purely inductively from the data. Kibiswa (2019), while confirmed only at the level of its general logic rather than its specific procedural steps, supports the broader principle that deriving codes from an existing theory before engaging with new empirical material serves to test, corroborate, or extend that theory's applicability to a context in which it was not originally developed — here, the application of institutional theory's coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures, together with substantive and symbolic response categories, to fast-fashion corporate communication. A short methodological caveat applies: the detailed step-by-step procedure Kibiswa's article reportedly proposes cannot yet be confirmed from available evidence and awaits full-text access to the source. Having established the qualitative, comparative, and document-analytic logic of the design, the following subsection turns to the rationale for selecting H&M Group and Inditex as the thesis's two bounded cases.

3.2 Case Selection and Study Period

H&M Group and Inditex were selected as the two cases for this comparative study primarily because they are the phenomenon under investigation rather than merely convenient examples of it: both are leading, globally visible fast-fashion multinationals whose environmental communication practices are subject to sustained public, regulatory, and academic attention. Fraser and van der Ven (2022) treat both firms as central members of the industry's competitive set in their comparative analysis of transparency practices among the four largest fast-fashion retailers, which lends independent scholarly precedent to isolating H&M and Inditex as analytically significant units within the sector. Beyond salience, the two firms offer strong comparability on dimensions that matter for a controlled qualitative comparison: both operate at a similar global scale, rely on comparable multi-tier global sourcing models, are domiciled within the European Union, and compete in substantially overlapping consumer markets. This shared EU domicile is analytically important, since it places both firms under a common coercive regulatory environment—most notably the EU's evolving sustainability-disclosure requirements—allowing observed differences in communication behavior to be attributed more plausibly to firm-level variation than to divergent external regulatory pressure.

The multinational and global-value-chain (GVC) character of both firms further justifies analysis at the MNE level. Asmussen et al. (2023) argue that IB research on corporate social responsibility has been dominated by an MNE-centric perspective and propose instead a bargaining view in which the visibility of CSR breaches at supplier sites depends on the negotiated relationship between lead firm and supplier; brand-owning MNEs such as H&M Group and Inditex are, in this account, incentivized to protect their "social brand" from reputational damage originating deeper in the value chain. This bargaining perspective, while general rather than sector-specific, suggests that studying brand-level communication of large, GVC-embedded fast-fashion MNEs is analytically appropriate precisely because information asymmetry and selective visibility are structural features of such chains, not incidental oversights.

Regarding variation in external scrutiny, Fraser and van der Ven (2022) directly report that H&M ranked as the most transparent of the four fast-fashion retailers examined in their pre-2022 comparison, while Gap and Inditex exhibited lower transparency because only one of three explanatory conditions—reputational risk, domestic norms, and leadership commitment—was present in each case. This finding, restricted to their pre-2022 study window, supports treating H&M and Inditex as a meaningfully contrastable pair rather than establishing any claim about their current disclosure behavior. Both firms additionally publish detailed annual sustainability and non-financial disclosures, ensuring adequate documentary data availability for the 2022–2025 period, a window whose final confirmed document set awaits the corpus table developed in Stage 4 but which is expected to capture a period of intensifying EU sustainability-disclosure regulation relevant to institutional-pressure analysis. The following subsection turns to the documentary corpus and time frame through which this comparison will be operationalized.

3.3 Data Collection and Document Selection

The document corpus comprises the primary, company-published annual and sustainability disclosure documents of H&M Group and Inditex covering the four fiscal years overlapping the 2022–2025 study window, supplemented by two categories of external material used strictly as empirical context rather than as analytic units in their own right. The full corpus, including exact titles, page ranges for each thematic subsection, and local storage locations, is documented in resources/document_corpus.md; this section summarizes the collection logic and the screening criteria applied before any document was admitted to the coding stage.

For H&M Group, six documents were collected across the four fiscal years: a dedicated "Sustainability Disclosure" for 2022 and 2023, and, from fiscal year 2024 onward, a renamed "Sustainability Progress Report" issued alongside the combined Annual and Sustainability Report. This naming change is itself a documented, confirmed fact rather than an inconsistency introduced by the corpus-building process — both the 2023 and 2024 documents independently state the company's own reporting-structure rationale, including explicit anticipatory language about aligning with forthcoming EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) requirements. For Inditex, the sustainability content ("Statement on Non-Financial Information" in 2022–2023, retitled "Consolidated Statement of Non-Financial Information and Sustainability Information" from 2024) is embedded as a section within the combined Inditex Group Annual Report rather than issued as a standalone document, and the full annual report — financial statements, directors' report, sustainability statement, and governance/remuneration reports together — runs to roughly 475–525 pages per year. Because the sustainability-relevant sections of these reports were the analytic target, each year's Inditex report was split into multiple PDF parts purely for file-handling purposes at the point of upload; this splitting has no bearing on document authenticity or content, since each part is a direct, unedited excerpt of the single official filing, and the printed page numbers embedded in the report itself (not the arbitrary PDF-part boundaries) were used throughout coding to preserve continuous, citable pagination.

A further structural difference between the two firms' reports, confirmed directly from the documents rather than assumed, is the 2024 transition point at which Inditex's disclosure adopts the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) architecture (General disclosures, E1–E5 environmental topics, S1–S4 social topics, G1 governance), replacing the company's own informally organized subsection headings used in 2022 and 2023. H&M's reports, by contrast, do not adopt an equivalent ESRS-numbered structure within the 2022–2025 window even while their own text explicitly anticipates CSRD compliance from as early as the 2022 report. This asymmetry — one firm restructuring its report architecture around the mandatory EU standard a year before the other, while both firms discuss the same forthcoming regulation in their narrative text well before either does so — is treated in this thesis as itself a piece of RQ1-relevant evidence about the timing and form institutional pressure takes for each firm, not as a data-quality problem to be normalized away.

Fiscal-year comparability caveat. H&M Group's fiscal year runs 1 December–30 November, labeled by the year it mostly falls within (e.g., "FY2024" spans December 2023–November 2024); Inditex's fiscal year runs 1 February–31 January, labeled by the year it begins (e.g., "FY2024" spans February 2024–January 2025). A same-numbered fiscal year therefore denotes two different, only partially overlapping twelve-month windows, roughly two months apart. This is treated throughout the coding matrices and Results chapter as a documented comparability caveat rather than a coding error: each report's actual covered period, not merely its label, is what the analysis relies on when comparing "the same year" across the two firms.

Inclusion and exclusion criteria. Included in the core coding corpus are primary, company-published annual or sustainability disclosure documents in English covering fiscal years overlapping the 2022–2025 window, for both firms. Excluded from the core coding corpus are press releases, marketing microsites, and social media content, which are not treated as equivalent in evidentiary weight to a formal annual/sustainability disclosure; such material may be referenced qualitatively in the Discussion chapter where directly relevant (for example, where a report itself references external criticism of a marketing claim) but is not coded using the matrix in research/coding/codebook_v2.md.

Document screening. Before any document was admitted to coding, each was checked against Morgan's (2022) document-analysis screening criteria of authenticity, credibility, representativeness, and meaning. Authenticity was assessed by cross-validating the Board of Directors composition, named executives, and Ernst & Young audit-signature pattern for internal consistency across each firm's own successive years' documents (for example, Inditex's Marta Ortega Pérez as Chair and Óscar García Maceiras as CEO appear consistently across all four years' filings, each independently audited by the same firm) — a concrete, evidence-based check rather than an assumption of genuineness from the file name or source alone. Representativeness and meaning were addressed by relying exclusively on each firm's own primary, full-length filing for a given year rather than a third-party excerpt, translation, or summary.

External/context documents. Two categories of material outside the two firms' own disclosures are used as empirical context rather than as coded analytic units and are never cited as academic literature, consistent with the scope boundary in CLAUDE.md §1: (1) regulatory and policy documents — the European Commission's Strategy for Sustainable and Circular Textiles (COM(2022) 141 final) and the Dutch Authority for Consumers and Markets' 2022 commitment decision regarding H&M's sustainability claims — used to situate coercive-pressure coding (RQ1) and, for the ACM decision specifically, as an external-challenge reference point for RQ2; and (2) two independent NGO/industry assessments used as triangulation sources for RQ2's selective-disclosure analysis. The primary triangulation source is Fashion Revolution's Fashion Transparency Index, a purpose-built external instrument scoring brands on the transparency of their own social and environmental disclosure rather than on underlying environmental performance — a direct fit for RQ2. Notably, H&M's own sustainability reports cite their Fashion Revolution ranking as a self-reported external benchmark in several of the coded years (see research/coding/HM_coding_matrix_2022-2025.md), making it a source already organically present in the corpus rather than one introduced purely from outside it. The secondary, supplementary source is the Changing Markets Foundation's "Synthetics Anonymous" report series, used narrowly as EXTERNAL CHALLENGE context; because this source is advocacy-toned and makes its own evaluative claims about the two firms, any use of it in Chapter 4/5 attributes claims explicitly to the NGO rather than adopting them as this thesis's own finding, consistent with the scope boundary against asserting wrongdoing or intent.

Both NGO sources carry an access limitation specific to this thesis's working environment: direct retrieval of the primary Index/report pages was blocked at the network level, so the specific scores recorded in resources/document_corpus.md rest on cross-checked search-snippet evidence rather than a directly read primary document, and are tagged PARTIAL rather than DIRECT following the same verification-tagging convention used for the academic source corpus (research/source_notes/). A further limitation specific to the Transparency Index is that a full, broad-scope edition exists for 2022 and 2023 (in which both H&M and Zara/Inditex are scored — 66%/no data in 2022, 71%/50% in 2023) but was not published in 2024 or 2025; those years' closest equivalent is a narrower "What Fuels Fashion?" edition scoring climate/energy disclosure only, in which a reliable comparable Inditex figure could not be located. Consequently, the Index provides a genuine cross-company RQ2 triangulation point primarily for 2023, with H&M-only context available for the other years — a limitation stated here rather than papered over with an unverified number.

3.4 Data Analysis

This thesis analyzes its document corpus through directed qualitative content analysis, a deductive approach in which coding categories are established from theory before the data are read, rather than allowed to emerge inductively from the text. Kibiswa (2019), the primary methodological source for this design, characterizes directed content analysis as a procedure in which the researcher draws categories from existing theory to set up the categories that guide the research — here, the coercive, normative, and mimetic pressure categories and the substantive, symbolic, and mixed response categories derived from Institutional Theory in Chapter 2, together with the eight greenwashing-indicator categories drawn from the wider greenwashing literature (Nemes et al., 2022; Spaniol et al., 2024). Directed content analysis is used, in Kibiswa's framing, to test, corroborate, or extend an existing theory's applicability to a context in which it was not originally developed; applying institutional-pressure and legitimacy-based categories to fast-fashion corporate communication is precisely this kind of extension. A short methodological caveat applies throughout this section: several of Kibiswa's specific procedural claims — the exact step sequence and the precise handling of text that does not fit a predefined category — are confirmed only at the level of general principle in the available evidence and await full-text confirmation; the analysis described below therefore follows the general logic Kibiswa establishes rather than asserting a specific numbered procedure as confirmed article content.

The coding process follows the four analytic stages Bengtsson (2016) identifies for qualitative content analysis: decontextualisation, recontextualisation, categorisation, and compilation. In the decontextualisation stage, each document in the corpus was read in full and broken into meaning units — discrete passages that make a claim, commitment, target, or disclosure-relevant statement — rather than coded at the level of isolated words or fixed sentence counts, consistent with the unit-of-analysis definition set out in the codebook (research/coding/codebook_v1.md). Recontextualisation followed: each meaning unit was checked against the codebook's a priori categories and, where a unit did not correspond to any predefined category, it was logged as a residual observation rather than forced into an ill-fitting code — precisely the process through which the pilot coding (research/coding/pilot_coding_report.md) surfaced the refinements later incorporated into Codebook v2. Categorisation groups coded units along three simultaneous dimensions: institutional-pressure type and corporate-response type (answering RQ1), and greenwashing-indicator and competitive-pressure-signal codes (answering RQ2), following the codebook's explicit rule that a single unit may carry codes on more than one dimension at once. Compilation, the final stage, draws the coded material together into within-case findings for each company and a cross-case comparison, addressing both research questions through the same coding matrix rather than through separate analytic procedures.

This deductive process is further grounded in Elo et al.'s (2014) description of the organization phase of content analysis as the development and application of a categorization matrix against which all data are reviewed for correspondence — the coding matrix set out in the codebook operationalizes exactly this logic, with each row recording the coded passage, its assigned codes, the evidence supporting each code, and any counter-evidence found in the same document. Bengtsson's (2016) distinction between manifest content ("what has been said," the visible surface of a claim) and latent content ("what was intended to be said," requiring interpretation of underlying framing) further justifies why coding for corporate response type and greenwashing indicators cannot rest on manifest wording alone: judging whether a response is substantive or symbolic, or whether a disclosure pattern constitutes disproportional emphasis or omission, requires interpreting the relationship between what is stated and what is surrounded, contextualized, or left unstated — a latent-level judgment the codebook's evidence and counter-evidence fields are designed to make transparent and auditable rather than impressionistic.

3.5 Trustworthiness and Limitations

Because this thesis uses a qualitative, comparative document analysis rather than a quantitative design, trustworthiness was evaluated using the vocabulary Elo et al. (2014) establish as the current consensus for qualitative research generally: credibility, dependability, confirmability, and transferability. Each criterion was pursued through a specific, describable procedural choice rather than asserted as a general property of the design. Credibility was supported by triangulating across multiple document types per company — annual and sustainability reports, and, where available, press communications and third-party coverage — following Wood et al.'s (2020) argument that using multiple sources or document types for triangulation is a specific technique for enhancing the credibility of qualitative document analysis, rather than a claim resting on a single report per firm per year. Dependability and confirmability were pursued jointly through the coding matrix's audit-trail structure: every coded unit records not only the code applied but the specific textual evidence supporting it and any counter-evidence present in the same document, allowing the coding decisions behind the Results chapter to be traced and checked rather than taken on the analyst's authority alone — consistent with Wood et al.'s (2020) broader argument that rigour in document analysis is achieved substantially through explicitness about assumptions and procedures rather than through claims of neutrality. Transferability is necessarily limited by the two-case design: findings describe how institutional and competitive pressures manifest in H&M Group's and Inditex's environmental communication specifically, and are not presented as generalizable to fast fashion as an industry, though the paired-case logic established in Section 3.2 supports analytic (theory-informed) rather than statistical generalization.

A specific and unavoidable limitation of document analysis as a method is the self-reporting bias inherent in corporate-authored sustainability disclosures. Morgan (2022) argues that documents produced by commercial organizations are typically intended to create a positive impression, and that internal actors have an incentive to conceal mistakes, producing a gap between what a document states and what actually occurs inside the organization — though this specific characterization is drawn from search-level evidence rather than a directly confirmed verbatim passage and is treated here with corresponding caution. This is not a peripheral concern for this thesis but a central one: because RQ1 and RQ2 both investigate the possibility that corporate environmental communication diverges from underlying practice, the corpus itself must be read as a strategically constructed artifact rather than a neutral record, which is precisely why the codebook (Section 3.4; research/coding/codebook_v1.md) requires counter-evidence logging alongside every applied code and restricts greenwashing-indicator codes to units with an identifiable textual or cross-referential basis, never a tone judgment alone.

Further limitations follow from the state of the evidence base underlying the codebook's theoretical categories, which was built primarily from abstract- and snippet-level evidence rather than full-text sources (see resources/source_access_checklist.md). The codebook itself was piloted on a small, matched sample of both companies' FY2024 disclosures (research/coding/pilot_coding_report.md) before full coding began, and was refined into a second version (research/coding/codebook_v2.md) that resolved the pilot's open questions on the entanglement of coercive and mimetic pressure (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023; Manshoven & Van Opstal, 2022) and on the practical codeability of the OMISSION indicator by splitting it into two narrower, independently applicable sub-tests; the cross-case PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING judgment, by contrast, could only be meaningfully tested once the full four-year, two-company coding matrices were complete, since it requires a cross-firm comparison rather than being identifiable from a single passage in isolation. Finally, coding was conducted by a single analyst; without a second coder, inter-coder reliability could not be formally assessed, and the audit-trail approach described above is offered as a partial, transparency-based substitute for that check rather than an equivalent to it.

In sum, this chapter has set out a qualitative, comparative case-study design grounded in directed content analysis of H&M Group's and Inditex's own disclosures, justified the selection of these two firms and the 2022–2025 study period, described the document corpus and its collection criteria, detailed the deductive coding procedure and its theoretical grounding, and identified the trustworthiness measures and limitations that bound what the analysis can claim. Chapter 4 now reports the results this design and coding procedure produced.

4 Results

4.1 Overview of the Document Corpus

The findings presented in this chapter are drawn from a corpus of primary, company-published sustainability and annual reporting documents covering both case firms across the full 2022–2025 study period. For H&M Group, the corpus comprises the Sustainability Disclosure 2022, the Sustainability Disclosure 2023, the Annual & Sustainability Report 2024 together with the accompanying Sustainability Progress Report 2024, and the Annual & Sustainability Report 2025 (H&M Group, 2022; H&M Group, 2023; H&M Group, 2024; H&M Group, 2025). For Inditex, the corpus comprises the sustainability sections of the Inditex Group Annual Report for each of the four years, titled the "Statement on Non-Financial Information" in 2022 and 2023 and restructured as the "Consolidated Statement of Non-Financial Information and Sustainability Information" from 2024 onward (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023; Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025). Across the two firms and four fiscal years, coding produced 237 coded units distributed across ten disclosure themes common to both companies' reporting structures: climate and emissions, water, chemicals and pollution, biodiversity, materials and circular economy, waste, own workforce, workers in the value chain, communities, and consumers. Of these 237 units, 122 were drawn from the H&M Group corpus and 115 from the Inditex corpus.

Two structural features of the corpus bear directly on how the sections that follow should be read and are therefore restated here rather than left implicit. First, the two firms report on non-identical fiscal-year calendars: H&M Group's fiscal year runs from 1 December to 30 November, labeled by the year in which most of that period falls, whereas Inditex's runs from 1 February to 31 January, labeled by the year in which it begins. A given "FY2024" accordingly denotes two different twelve-month windows roughly two months apart, and same-numbered-year comparisons in this chapter refer to each report's own stated covered period rather than to a shared calendar year. Second, the two firms' reporting architectures diverge from 2024 onward: Inditex's 2024 and 2025 statements are organized around the EU's European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS 2, E1–E5, S1–S4, G1), replacing the informally organized subsection headings used in its 2022 and 2023 statements, while H&M Group's reports across the same window contain narrative language anticipating future CSRD compliance without adopting an equivalent ESRS-numbered structure. This asymmetry in the timing and form of structural change is a documented feature of the corpus itself, distinct from the pressure- and response-level findings reported in Sections 4.2 through 4.4. One further scoping note applies to this chapter as a whole: per the data-collection design set out in Methodology Section 3.3, one category of material — external NGO/industry transparency-assessment data (the Fashion Transparency Index and the Changing Markets Foundation's reporting) — was deliberately reserved for triangulation in Chapter 5's discussion rather than coded into the primary matrices reported here, since it originates outside the two firms' own disclosures and serves an interpretive rather than a within-corpus descriptive function.

4.2 H&M Group

Across H&M Group's four sustainability disclosures (2022–2025), climate and emissions reporting shows a shift in institutional-pressure framing alongside a recurring transparency pattern in the underlying data. The 2022 report frames H&M as anticipatorily "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded COERCIVE/NORMATIVE and scored SYMBOLIC because no date or metric attaches to the language (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16). By the FY2024 Progress Report, the comparable unit instead states the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY because the claim's scope is not tied to any specific standard-level datapoint (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 2). On the underlying emissions figures, a MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern appears in both the earliest and the FY2024 report: the 2022 report discloses a 9,270-tonne (+18%) year-on-year scope 1+2 increase in the same passage as an 8% reduction against the 2019 baseline, attributed to the wind-down of Russian operations (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses a 201,000-tonne (+3%) scope 3 increase alongside a 23.7% reduction against baseline (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9). By 2025 the year-on-year scope 3 figure reverses to a 13.6% decrease (34.6% against the 2019 baseline), coded SUBSTANTIVE rather than MIXED (H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). The matrix also records a comparable-magnitude scope 3 baseline restatement for the same named progress-versus-baseline KPI in both 2022 and 2023 — from -9% to -6% and from -7% to -15% respectively — coded differently: the 2022 restatement as INCONSISTENT METRIC, the 2023 one left uncoded because the report explains the methodology change in the same passage (H&M Group, 2022, p. 28; H&M Group, 2023, pp. 20–21).

The materials and circular-economy theme shows a recurring bundling pattern in H&M's headline "recycled or sustainably sourced materials" percentage. In 2022, the 84% headline figure (23% recycled, the remainder "other more sustainably sourced") is broken out only via an accompanying chart rather than in the same sentence, coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY (H&M Group, 2022, p. 43). The 2023 report discloses the same style of aggregate (85%) broken into its 25% recycled and 60% sustainably-sourced components within the same data table and is explicitly left uncoded on this indicator (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 38–39). By FY2024 the aggregate rises to 89% (29.5% recycled), again reported without an in-unit split, though the row was not formally coded with a greenwashing indicator (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 18–20); the 2025 aggregate (91%, 32% recycled) was likewise left uncoded (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 90–91). A related denominator pattern appears in the circular-customer-journey/resale metric: the 2022 report gives no group-wide scale denominator anywhere for resale, rental, or repair activity, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56); the FY2024 Progress Report discloses resale at 0.6% of turnover, flagged only as a DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS candidate pending a page/word-share method, not a confirmed instance (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 21–22); and the 2025 report states 0.8% of turnover in the same sentence as the growth claim, explicitly recorded as not meeting that trigger (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90).

Biodiversity is coded as the thinnest theme across all four years in terms of quantification. The 2022 and 2023 reports present regenerative-agriculture pilot data — farmer counts and tonnage targets for named projects — without a denominator connecting them to H&M's total sourcing volume, both coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33; H&M Group, 2023, p. 28). The FY2024 Progress Report states that Biodiversity is one of only two thematic sections (with Communities) that report no quantified progress figure at all, and its narrative ambition statement is itself coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17). The 2023 report separately discloses a CDP Forest score decline across all three tracked commodities (Cattle Products B- to C, Timber B to C, Palm Oil B to D), stated plainly with an admission that "the decreased scores have not been fully analysed," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 29). By 2025, the report states directly that no biodiversity-related targets exist for that year, with adoption deferred to 2026 — self-disclosed rather than embedded in unqualified ambition language, and recorded on the decoupling axis as a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING candidate (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87).

Workers and living-wage-adjacent findings show a recurring gap between wage data reported and living-wage or equity outcomes claimed. The 2022 report states that H&M's Wage Strategy, pursued through its ACT (Action, Collaboration, Transformation) membership, "aims to deliver living wages," while the detailed wage tables that follow measure only average wages against the statutory minimum wage, coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION with a POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING flag (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71). An independent gender-pay-gap study led by Professor Raymond Robertson, covering more than 1,300 production units, finds a "statistically significant pay gap between men and women in most... production countries" in 2022, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend), and in 2023 finds gaps "highest in Bangladesh and India" without disclosing the gap's magnitude, coded SUBSTANTIVE despite the unquantified finding (H&M Group, 2022, p. 71; H&M Group, 2023, p. 61). Freedom-of-association metrics decline in both periods where comparably tracked: tier-1 trade-union representation falls from 42% to 37% between 2022 and 2023, and from 39% to 36% between 2024 and 2025, both self-disclosed in the same table as the prior-year figures and coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2023, p. 62; H&M Group, 2025, p. 110). The FY2024 wage-grid disclosure (290 factories) lacks the percentage-of-tier-1-suppliers denominator given for the adjacent wage-management-system enrolment figure in the same paragraph, coded OMISSION (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 31); by 2025, wage-management-system enrolment reaches 80% against a 100%-by-2028 target, and the report itself states the target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact," coded MEANS-ENDS DECOUPLING (self-disclosed) (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110).

Within the water and chemicals theme, effluent-treatment-plant functionality assessed at green grade declines across the three years for which the metric is tracked — 90% (2022), 89% (2023), 84.4% (FY2024) — each figure disclosed in the water performance data table, the 2022 figure also stated in the narrative alongside an explicit acknowledgement of "missing our goal of 100%," coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) without meeting the text-anchored DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS trigger (H&M Group, 2022, p. 36; H&M Group, 2023, p. 25; H&M Group, 2024b, p. 15). The 2025 report discloses a related, re-scoped chemicals metric — 92.6% ZDHC Manufacturing Restricted Substances List assurance against a 95%-by-2030 target — while stating that "these targets have not been validated by an external party," coded WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 82–83).

Consumers and communities findings include a rare instance, in the 2022 report, of a company self-identifying an external challenge to a prior claim: the decade-long "Conscious" product-label program is retired, with the report stating the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation," coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE and MIXED (scope-narrowed), since no successor metric or date is given for the replacement approach (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14). The same report discloses a 21-percentage-point decline in stakeholder-rated transparency trust, from 71% to 50%, stated in the same sentence as the prior-year comparator, coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) (H&M Group, 2022, p. 14). By 2024 and 2025, H&M's own report structure states directly, under each topic's targets-and-actions subheading, that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets have been set for Consumers and end-users and Affected communities; the 2025 report extends this to Business conduct as well, whereas the 2024 report instead reports targets for that topic related to preventing corruption and bribery — recorded on the decoupling axis as POLICY-PRACTICE DECOUPLING and noted alongside the dated, quantified targets given for every environmental topic read in the same reports (H&M Group, 2024a, pp. 102–103, 105; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118).

4.3 Inditex

Inditex's Statement on Non-Financial Information changes structure partway through the study period: the 2022 and 2023 reports (Inditex, 2022; Inditex, 2023) are organized under company-defined "Committed to" headings with no ESRS cross-references, while the 2024 and 2025 reports (Inditex, 2024; Inditex, 2025) follow the CSRD-mandated ESRS structure — a shift that affects which findings trace continuously across all four years and which appear only once the relevant ESRS chapter exists.

On climate and emissions, the 2022 Environment section opens with the statement that Inditex "achieved our goal of 100% renewable electricity in our facilities" (Inditex, 2022, p. 197); the accompanying emissions chart, two pages later, shows Scope 1 and Scope 2 combined at well under one percent of total emissions, with Scope 3 accounting for the remainder (Inditex, 2022, p. 199). This unit was coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS: the favorable, small-denominator achievement is narrated without the large-denominator context in the same or an adjacent sentence, though that context is tabulated nearby. The 2024 report shows the same pattern for the Scope 1+2 versus Scope 3 trajectory (down 87.7% versus down 1.3% from 2018), again coded DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS, with the "Towards net-zero emissions" graphic foregrounding the Scope 1+2 trend while the full breakdown sits in a separate table (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163). By contrast, the 2023 and 2025 reports place the unfavorable figure in the same sentence as the favorable one: in 2023, the "scope 1 and 2 emissions by 97%" reduction and the target-relevant emissions figure that "increased by 4%" appear in one sentence (Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194); in 2025, an 88% Scope 1+2 reduction and a 7% Scope 3 (SBT-relevant) reduction are reported together, "in both cases compared to 2018" (Inditex, 2025, p. 134). Both are coded MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) rather than DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS, since the operational test requires the unfavorable figure to be absent from the same or adjacent sentence. A related pattern recurs around self-consumption: on-site generation is narrated with a rising year-over-year figure in every report (7,756 MWh in 2022 to 11,506 MWh in 2025), while total energy consumption — roughly 1.6–1.7 million MWh throughout — appears elsewhere without the resulting share (under one percent every year) stated in the same passage (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2023, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2024, pp. 157, 165; Inditex, 2025, pp. 129, 132), coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022–2024 and DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS in 2025.

Inditex's headline materials metric also changes label across the period. The 2022 report's "preferred" fibers and materials reach 60% of total use, with a footnote stating the company had "aligned the terminology used in our objectives with that currently being used by benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" while asserting "the meaning and ambition of our goals remains unchanged" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) — coded INCONSISTENT METRIC because the realignment is self-disclosed but not substantiated with a like-for-like conversion. The same page reports a second, separate materials metric: 61% of products met Inditex's group-wide Join Life sustainability standard, against the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187). This figure was not flagged under any greenwashing indicator; it is analytically distinct from the INCONSISTENT METRIC coding above, which concerns the adjacent "preferred fibres" terminology realignment, not the Join Life compliance figure itself. The 2023 report retains the "preferred raw materials" label at 68% (Inditex, 2023, pp. 236–237), while the 2024 and 2025 reports relabel the metric "lower-impact fibres," at 73% (Inditex, 2024, p. 200) and 88% (Inditex, 2025, p. 150); the 2025 table gives only current- and target-year columns, and definitional continuity with the 2024 figure was not verified, so no cross-year comparison is drawn here. Within "preferred cotton," the largest single component in 2022 and 2023 is Better Cotton, footnoted in both years as a mass-balance calculation "not physically traceable to the final product" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 188–189; Inditex, 2023, pp. 238–239) — coded UNCLEAR BOUNDARY in 2022, not coded in 2023, since the caveat sits in a footnote attached to the figure it qualifies. The Clothing Collection Programme's recovered tonnage recurs every year — 17,015 (2022), 20,259 (2023), 19,484 (2024), 20,495 tonnes (2025) — each year reporting a reuse/recycling split but no denominator expressing tonnage as a share of garments placed on the market (Inditex, 2022, pp. 192–193; Inditex, 2023, p. 243; Inditex, 2024, pp. 197–198; Inditex, 2025, pp. 150–152). This was coded OMISSION under the metric-denominator sub-test in 2022, 2023, and 2025; in 2024 it was treated instead as a cross-case comparability gap against H&M's percentage-of-turnover resale disclosure, since no comparable same-company denominator was found that year.

Biodiversity shows the clearest quantified trajectory of the themes examined. The 2022 report frames biodiversity action under the Science Based Targets Network's "Avoid, Reduce, Restore, Regenerate and Transform" (AR3T) framework and names specific certifications and restoration projects, but discloses no Group-wide hectare or other aggregate metric, unlike the numeric targets given for climate, water, and materials in the same report; coded SYMBOLIC and WEAK SUBSTANTIATION (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211). The only quantified area given that year is a regenerative-agriculture partnership in India spanning 300,000 hectares, without a statement of what share of Inditex's total sourcing footprint this represents (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–210). The 2023 report introduces a dated, quantified Group-wide target — to protect, restore, regenerate, or otherwise improve biodiversity across five million hectares by 2030 — but discloses no cumulative progress figure against it, with the same India partnership remaining the only area figure given (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), coded OMISSION. From 2024 the target is tracked with a current-year figure: 758,534 hectares, reported as 622,878 restored plus 135,656 regenerated, a sum the coding matrix verified reconciles arithmetically (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192); by 2025 the figure reaches 1.5 million hectares (1,107,098 restored plus 398,217 regenerated), again reconciling (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Both units are coded SUBSTANTIVE with no greenwashing indicator applied.

Within the "Workers at the Centre" strategy, the Living Wages Priority Impact Area shows a consistent pattern across all four years. In 2022, the full sub-section names country-specific projects — the ACT collaboration in Bangladesh, Cambodia, and Türkiye; the LEAN project; SCORE; HERfinance — but discloses no wage-level figure, living-wage-benchmark gap, or even a people-reached figure (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228), coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE decoupling. From 2023 a people-reached figure appears, but it is consistently the smallest of the strategy's five Priority Impact Areas: 31,318 people in 2023, against Health's 1,125,665 (Inditex, 2023, pp. 289–290); 86,268 in 2024, against Health's 1,313,217 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262); and 120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028 (Inditex, 2025, p. 178). No wage-level, benchmark, or wage-gap metric appears in any year's Living Wages sub-section. The 2023 and 2024 units are coded SYMBOLIC, WEAK SUBSTANTIATION, and POLICY-PRACTICE (candidate); the 2025 coding records the same reach-only pattern for a second consecutive year and treats the recurrence as supporting a more confirmed cross-year pattern, while stating that the coded pattern concerns disclosure practice, not a claim about whether Inditex's suppliers pay living wages.

On water, the 2022 report discloses both an absolute water-consumption figure and two intensity denominators (240 litres/m² and 55 ml/EUR) for its own facilities (Inditex, 2022, pp. 206–207), a level of denomination that year's Waste sub-section does not match. A related asymmetry appears in 2025 between the Water and Energy chapters: Energy discloses an absolute total plus intensity metrics, while Water reports only a relative intensity metric (litres per kilogram of garment), stating that "the amount of water consumed is very low" and treating withdrawal as the more representative metric without an absolute volume (Inditex, 2025, pp. 142–143) — coded a provisional OMISSION candidate, pending verification against the report's unread Annex Methodology section. On communities, the 2025 report's own materiality assessment states that "the identified potential impacts and risks linked to ESRS 'S3 - Affected communities' are not material for Inditex" (Inditex, 2025, p. 121); community content is not entirely absent but appears as narrative color distributed across the biodiversity, materials, and workers-in-the-value-chain chapters, without a dedicated metric framework of its own (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148, 151, 175, 179). This exclusion is coded OMISSION under the cross-case topic-omission sub-test, pending comparison against H&M's 2025 materiality structure.

4.4 Cross-Case Comparison

Across the themes synthesised in the cross-case coding, the clearest recurring pattern in both companies' climate disclosure is a structural separation between scope 1+2 emissions, consistently framed with SUBSTANTIVE, SBTi-validated targets, and scope 3 emissions, which account for the large majority of each firm's footprint and follow a more uneven trajectory. H&M reports a stable MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern for scope 3 in every coded year: a +18% year-over-year increase attributed to the Russia exit in 2022, a further +3% increase attributed to material weight and airfreight in the 2024 Progress Report, and a reversal to -13.6% in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). Inditex shows a narrower, more intermittent version of the same imbalance: a DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS instance in 2022, where a headline scope 1+2/renewable-electricity figure is stated narratively while the corresponding scope 3 figure, though present in an accompanying chart, is not stated in the same or an adjacent sentence (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199), and a second such instance in 2024 (Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 162–163). The same test is not met in 2023 or 2025, where the favorable and unfavorable scope figures appear within the same sentence (Inditex, 2023, pp. 190–191, 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). In both firms' coded material the scope 3 figures are self-disclosed rather than absent, a relevant qualification when reading either company's climate framing.

A second cross-case contrast concerns Living Wages, a theme both companies name after a wage outcome but document with markedly different metrics. H&M's 2022 disclosure pairs an ACT/living-wage aim with a minimum-wage-versus-statutory-wage gap table that does not itself constitute a living-wage benchmark (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71), while its 2024 Progress Report reports a narrow but concrete figure — a seven-factory Mainland China gender-pay-gap reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside an admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 29, 31). By 2025, H&M introduces a coverage-based Wage Management System target (80% enrolment in 2025, 100% by 2028) described by the company itself as focused on "programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110). Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area, by contrast, reports zero wage-level, living-wage-benchmark, or wage-gap metrics in any of the four coded years, disclosing only a "people reached" count rising to 86,268 in 2024 and 120,050 in 2025 — consistently the smallest of Inditex's five named Priority Impact Areas, well below the 2,159,028 people reported for Health in the same year (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). Of the patterns identified across the corpus, this one most consistently meets the cross-case PROPRIETARY WITHHOLDING test: a topic named after a wage outcome, reported with a reach figure but never a wage-level figure, across three consecutive Inditex reporting years, in a document that quantifies most other themes to a comparable or greater level of detail. The finding is specific to supply-chain wage data; both companies separately report comparable gender-pay-gap percentages for their own direct workforce, so the asymmetry should not be read as a general wage-transparency gap.

A third contrast concerns Biodiversity, where the two companies reach markedly different levels of quantification over the same four years. H&M's Biodiversity disclosure remains SYMBOLIC throughout the corpus: an "aim to make time-bound commitments in 2023" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34), a 2023 statement that a target is "still in the process of being developed" (H&M Group, 2023, p. 27), a 2024 Progress Report in which Biodiversity is one of only two thematic sections carrying no quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17), and a 2025 statement that the company has "not yet set biodiversity-related targets" and intends to adopt them "in 2026" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex's disclosure follows a different trajectory: a quantified 2030 target of 5 million hectares to protect, restore, or regenerate is present from at least 2022 (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211), without a Group-level current-year progress figure through 2023 (Inditex, 2023, p. 230), before becoming fully quantified and internally arithmetic-consistent — 758,534 hectares in 2024 and 1.5 million hectares in 2025, anchored to the Science Based Targets Network AR3T framework (Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). This is a disclosure-practice asymmetry, not evidence about underlying ecological outcomes at either company.

A fourth divergence appears in the Communities theme in the 2025 reporting cycle. H&M's 2024 Progress Report lists Communities as one of only two sections without a quantified progress figure (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 32), and its 2025 report states that the company has "not set any time-bound and outcome-oriented targets related to affected communities" (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112), retaining the topic in its reporting but attaching no commitment-level target to it — a SYMBOLIC target status. Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment, conducted under its ESRS structure, concludes instead that "S3 - Affected communities... are not material for Inditex," removing Communities from its material-topic list rather than retaining it as an unmet-target theme (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both patterns reduce commitment-level disclosure for this theme relative to earlier reporting years, but take structurally different forms — a retained, target-less topic at H&M, and a materiality-based exclusion at Inditex — that the coding treats as distinct rather than equivalent.

Finally, the two companies differ in how frequently and how they anchor competitive or peer-benchmarking language. Rows carrying a COMPETITIVE FRAMING or PEER BENCHMARKING tag appear nine times across H&M's four reports against seven times across Inditex's, with H&M's count rising from one row in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in the 2024 Progress Report before falling back to one in the fully ESRS-structured 2025 report; Inditex's count stays comparatively flat (two, two, one, two). H&M's instances are more often anchored to independently verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP climate score of A in the 2024 Progress Report (A− in 2023), a Fashion Revolution Transparency Index rank, a Textile Exchange Material Change Index "Leading" designation (naming the company one of six overall leaders of 130 participating companies in 2023, and one of just 11 companies to reach "Leading" status in 2024), and one direct named-competitor comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 5, 20). Inditex's comparable rows are more often self-referential leadership or benchmark-setting statements without a named external comparator — on-site self-consumption capacity described as an achievement, and Care for Water framed as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2022, pp. 202–203; Inditex, 2025, p. 144) — with a single exception, an IPE ranking citing the company as "third... globally and in the textile sector" (Inditex, 2023, p. 229). In absolute terms, competitive-framing language remains a secondary feature of both companies' communication relative to the size of each year's coded material.

The cross-case coding also produced one explicit negative result relevant to RQ2: no category of granular data disclosed by Inditex was found conspicuously and consistently absent from H&M's coverage of the same topic. Candidates considered and rejected include hectare-level biodiversity project reporting and named community-investment figures for specific initiatives; in each case H&M's own reporting discloses the same underlying information, if in a differently presented or less consolidated form (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–148; H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32–33, 81; Inditex, 2023, pp. 234–235; Inditex, 2025, p. 176). The withholding asymmetries identified in this corpus therefore run in one direction only, from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds, most clearly on supply-chain wage data; no comparably supported finding runs the other way.

4.5 Summary of Findings

Across the ten disclosure themes coded for both firms over 2022–2025, the picture that emerges from Sections 4.2 through 4.4 is one of structural divergence and uneven emphasis rather than a single pattern shared uniformly by H&M Group and Inditex. Both companies' climate and emissions reporting shares one structural feature: scope 1 and scope 2 emissions are tracked against SBTi-validated targets coded substantive, while scope 3 emissions, the larger share of each firm's footprint, follow a more uneven trajectory. For H&M this recurs as a self-disclosed unfavorable trend across most coded years, reversing only in 2025; for Inditex the same imbalance surfaces intermittently as disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024 but not in 2023 or 2025, where the favorable and unfavorable figures are stated in the same sentence.

On materials and circular economy, both firms report rising headline percentages for recycled or lower-impact materials, and both show a recurring gap between resale, rental, repair, or collection tonnage and any denominator connecting that tonnage to overall production or sales volume. Biodiversity disclosure diverges more sharply: H&M's reporting remains symbolic and without a quantified group-wide target throughout the study period, while Inditex introduces a dated, quantified hectare target in 2022 and reports increasingly reconciled progress figures against it from 2024 onward. Within the workers-focused themes, both firms name living-wage ambitions, but neither discloses a wage-level or living-wage-benchmark figure tied to that ambition in any coded year: H&M instead reports statutory-wage comparisons and, from 2024, a coverage-based enrolment target described by the company as focused on program coverage rather than impact, while Inditex reports a rising but comparatively small people-reached figure that remains the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas in every year it is tracked. The Communities theme shows a further point of divergence in the 2025 reporting cycle, where H&M retains the topic without time-bound targets, and Inditex's materiality assessment excludes the topic from its material-topic list.

Taken together, the coding identifies patterns that are traceable to specific documents, years, and pages rather than to a single overall label applied to either company. With respect to RQ1, both firms' coded material contains a mixture of substantive and symbolic responses rather than one uniform response type across themes and years. With respect to RQ2, the cross-case matrices identify page-cited selective-disclosure patterns, including omission, disproportional emphasis, unclear boundary, and inconsistent metric codings, in both firms' reporting, alongside one identified withholding asymmetry running from H&M-discloses to Inditex-withholds on supply-chain wage data, with no comparably supported instance running in the other direction. Chapter 5 now turns to interpreting these findings against the institutional-theoretical framework developed in Chapter 2, answering RQ1 and RQ2 directly and drawing out their theoretical and practical implications.

5 Discussion

5.1 Institutional Pressures and Corporate Responses (RQ1)

The clearest institutional-pressure signal in the coded material is coercive, and its most striking feature is not its presence but its timing. H&M Group's climate disclosure carries anticipatory regulatory language from the first year of the study period — framing the company in 2022 as "supporting development of" the EU Taxonomy, CSDDD, and CSRD, coded symbolic because no date or metric attaches to the language (H&M Group, 2022, p. 16) — and sustains that register through the FY2024 Progress Report's carefully hedged statement that the report is "aligned with" but does not "claim to be compliant with" CSRD/ESRS (H&M Group, 2024b, p. 2). Inditex's coded 2022–2023 material contains comparatively little equivalent anticipatory rhetoric, yet its reporting architecture converts to the full ESRS-numbered structure already in the 2024 statement, a full reporting cycle before H&M's own disclosure takes on a comparably compliance-scoped, ESRS-structured form in 2025 (Section 4.1; Section 4.4). This asymmetry maps directly onto, and partially complicates, Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) finding that non-financial disclosure expands in the anticipatory period before a regulation binds: H&M exemplifies the "talk-first" anticipatory pattern their Spanish evidence describes, absorbing coercive pressure as language for roughly three reporting cycles before absorbing it as structure. Inditex, by contrast, exhibits something the anticipatory-isomorphism account does not predict — early structural conformity with little preceding anticipatory narrative visible in the coded corpus. Coercive pressure, in other words, was reflected in both firms' communication, but through two different absorption sequences: rhetoric preceding architecture in one case, architecture arriving without much rhetoric in the other. This within-pair variation supports Latif et al.'s (2020) premise that institutional pressures are empirically tractable as distinct constructs, while showing that the same pressure type can surface in report content through quite different observable traces.

The substantive–symbolic comparison RQ1 calls for resolves most informatively at the level of themes rather than firms. Both companies anchor their scope 1 and 2 emissions to SBTi-validated targets coded substantive — a normative anchoring, since science-based target-setting is a voluntary professional standard rather than a legal mandate — while scope 3, the large majority of each footprint, follows a more uneven trajectory (Section 4.4). For H&M this recurs as the codebook's MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) sub-pattern in nearly every coded year: a +18% year-on-year scope 1+2 increase disclosed in the same passage as an 8% baseline reduction in 2022, a +3% scope 3 increase alongside a 23.7% baseline reduction in the FY2024 Progress Report, reversing to a substantive −13.6% only in 2025 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 28–29; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 8–9; H&M Group, 2025, p. 79). Two interpretive points follow. First, this is not decoupling in the concealment sense the literature often implies: the unfavorable figures are self-disclosed, merely placed beside a more flattering comparator. Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) observation that symbolic conformity can operate "whether intentionally or not" is apposite here, and the coding deliberately distinguished this transparent-setback pattern from scope manipulation precisely to avoid overstating the greenwashing finding. Second, the persistence of the scope 1+2/scope 3 split in both firms is consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) finding that decoupling under EU reporting regulation is uneven across environmental sub-dimensions rather than uniform: where measurement infrastructure is mature and externally validated, responses are substantive; where it is not, responses thin out.

Biodiversity is where that thinning is most visible, and where the two firms diverge most sharply. H&M's biodiversity disclosure remains symbolic across all four years — an aim to set commitments in 2023, a target "still in the process of being developed," one of only two sections without any quantified progress figure in FY2024, and an explicit statement in 2025 that no biodiversity targets exist, with adoption deferred to 2026 (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 32, 34; H&M Group, 2023, p. 27; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 16–17; H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87). Inditex moves in the opposite direction: from a symbolic, certification-listing 2022 section, through a dated five-million-hectare 2030 target without progress data in 2023, to arithmetically reconciled hectare figures in 2024 and 2025 anchored to the Science Based Targets Network framework (Inditex, 2022, pp. 209–211; Inditex, 2023, p. 230; Inditex, 2024, pp. 190–192; Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147). Read through Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) argument that institutional pressure is mediated by internal organizational commitment, the biodiversity divergence extends their firm-level insight inward: mediation apparently operates theme by theme, since the same H&M that quantifies climate and water rigorously leaves biodiversity untargeted, and the same Inditex that quantifies hectares leaves wages unquantified (Section 4.4). Normative pressure also leaves a distinctive trace in this material: Inditex's realignment of its materials terminology to match "benchmark industry organisations such as Textile Exchange" (Inditex, 2022, p. 187) reads as convergence on field-level standard vocabulary — behavior with a mimetic-normative character, adopting the field's categories under definitional uncertainty — even though it earned an INCONSISTENT METRIC coding because the continuity claim was asserted rather than demonstrated. Genuinely mimetic conformity, in DiMaggio and Powell's (1983) strict original sense of imitation of organizations perceived as successful or legitimate specifically under conditions of uncertainty about the appropriate course of action — rather than benchmarking against a named rival for competitive advantage — is thus better evidenced in this corpus by convergence on shared voluntary frameworks (SBTi, Textile Exchange, ZDHC) than by the peer-ranking language examined next, which belongs analytically to competitive signaling rather than institutional isomorphism — a distinction Section 5.3 returns to.

5.2 Competitive Pressures and Selective Disclosure (RQ2)

The most consistent selective-disclosure finding in the corpus is the supply-chain wage asymmetry, and it aligns closely with the proprietary-cost mechanism in the literature. Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area — a program named after a wage outcome — reports no wage-level, living-wage-benchmark, or wage-gap figure in any of the four coded years, disclosing only a people-reached count that is consistently the smallest of its five Priority Impact Areas (120,050 in 2025, against Health's 2,159,028), in reports that quantify most other themes to a comparable or greater level of detail (Inditex, 2022, pp. 226–228; Inditex, 2024, pp. 260–262; Inditex, 2025, p. 178). H&M, by contrast, publishes wage tables against statutory minima, an independent supplier gender-pay-gap study, and a narrow but concrete outcome figure — a seven-factory reduction from 15.3% to 4.6% — alongside its own admission that "in many countries... the legal minimum wage is not sufficient" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 67–71; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 29, 31). Lee and Lee's (2022) finding that intensified competition depresses voluntary disclosure of competitively sensitive information offers the most parsimonious interpretation: country- and factory-level wage data reveal sourcing-cost structure, a plausibly proprietary category for an apparel buyer. Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) global finding that scrutiny and diffused norms — not mere exposure to reporting pressure — are what limit selective disclosure offers a complementary reading: Inditex's Living Wages gap may persist specifically where wage-level scrutiny and cross-border normative pressure on labor disclosure remain weaker than for environmental metrics such as emissions or biodiversity, which this same corpus shows Inditex quantifying rigorously under an otherwise-comparable reporting regime (Section 5.1). Three qualifications keep this reading within what the coding supports. The asymmetry is domain-specific — both firms disclose own-workforce gender-pay-gap figures, so this is not a general wage-transparency gap (Section 4.4). It is directional: the cross-case coding's explicit negative result found no category that Inditex discloses and H&M comparably withholds (Section 4.4), which strengthens the pattern precisely because disconfirming candidates were sought and rejected. And it is a disclosure finding, not a wage finding: nothing in the coded material speaks to what Inditex's suppliers actually pay, only to what the reports state.

A second family of findings concerns emphasis rather than absence. Inditex's climate narrative in 2022 and 2024 foregrounds favorable small-denominator achievements — the 100% renewable-electricity statement, the −87.7% scope 1+2 trajectory — while the corresponding scope 3 figures, though tabulated nearby, are not stated in the same or an adjacent sentence, meeting the codebook's operational DISPROPORTIONAL EMPHASIS test; in 2023 and 2025 the favorable and unfavorable figures share a sentence and the test is not met (Inditex, 2022, pp. 197, 199; Inditex, 2024, pp. 146, 163; Inditex, 2023, pp. 193–194; Inditex, 2025, p. 134). The recurring self-consumption pattern — a rising on-site generation figure narrated every year while its share of total energy consumption, under one percent throughout, goes unstated in the same passage — behaves identically (Section 4.3). This is selective disclosure in exactly the sense Nemes et al. (2022) operationalize: partial, insufficiently contextualized emphasis on positive performance, distinct from fabrication. It also corresponds to Bernini and La Rosa's (2024) passive, omission-based mode of communication-level greenwashing rather than their active, misleading-disclosure mode — the unfavorable numbers exist in the same documents, so what varies is narrative prominence, the "grey zone" of unaudited voluntary framing that Gatti et al. (2019) argue resists any bright-line classification. The denominator findings carry the same signature on the H&M side: resale activity reported with no group-wide scale figure in 2022, then 0.6% of turnover in FY2024 (flagged only as an emphasis candidate), then 0.8% stated in the same sentence as the growth claim in 2025, explicitly no longer meeting the trigger (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 53–56; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 21–22; H&M Group, 2025, p. 90) — a gap that closes over time, unlike Inditex's collection-tonnage series, which lacks a garments-placed-on-market denominator in nearly every year (Section 4.3).

The Communities divergence shows that selectivity can also be procedural. H&M retains Communities as a topic while stating that no time-bound, outcome-oriented targets exist for it (H&M Group, 2025, p. 112); Inditex's 2025 materiality assessment concludes that ESRS "S3 – Affected communities" is "not material for Inditex," removing the theme from its material-topic list altogether (Inditex, 2025, p. 121). Both reduce commitment-level disclosure relative to earlier years, but the second operates upstream of disclosure itself: under a double-materiality regime, the materiality assessment determines what must be reported at all, so exclusion at that stage is a structurally stronger form of omission than an unmet target. This extends Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) "orphan issues" logic in an unexpected direction — where their concern was material topics receiving thin follow-through, the ESRS architecture creates the possibility of resolving that tension by re-drawing materiality rather than deepening disclosure.

External triangulation, within its limits, points the same direction as the coded asymmetry. Fashion Revolution's Fashion Transparency Index scored H&M at 66% in 2022, tied with The North Face and Timberland in the report's own top-10 table (Fashion Revolution, 2022, p. 13); Zara/Inditex does not appear in that same table, placing its 2022 score below the lowest tied group shown (54%), though no exact figure could be confirmed. In 2023 — the only edition in the window scoring both firms comparably — the Index scored H&M at 71%, confirmed directly against the report's own "Highest Scoring Brands" chart (Fashion Revolution, 2023, p. 19), where H&M sits between United Colors of Benetton (73%) and C&A (68%); Zara/Inditex's reported 50% for the same year does not appear on that chart (it lists only the eleven highest- and roughly twenty lowest-scoring brands) and so remains PARTIAL, verified only at search-snippet level. The narrower climate-only successor editions score H&M at 61% in 2024 — confirmed directly against the "What Fuels Fashion?" 2024 results, with H&M among the highest-scoring brands (Fashion Revolution, 2024, p. 21) and, in the full score distribution, Zara/Inditex at 41% (Fashion Revolution, 2024, p. 20); H&M is further reported first of 200 brands at 71% in 2025, that figure still verified only at search-snippet level, with no comparable 2025 Zara/Inditex figure located. The 2024 edition thus supplies a second directly-confirmed cross-company pair — H&M 61% against Zara/Inditex 41%, a twenty-point gap in the same direction as 2023's — although its climate-and-energy-only scope makes it narrower than the full-scope 2023 Index. In sum, the H&M figures for 2022, 2023, and 2024 and the Zara/Inditex figure for 2024 are now confirmed directly against primary report pages; the Zara/Inditex 2023 figure (50%) and the 2025 H&M figure remain awaiting further primary-source confirmation, not settled numbers. With that caveat carried, the 2023 gap is at least consistent with the one-directional withholding pattern the coding identified independently. Two further nuances complicate any simple "H&M is more transparent" gloss. First, H&M's own reports cite its Fashion Revolution ranking as a competitive credential (Section 4.4), so the index is simultaneously evidence and marketing material. Second, high disclosure and external challenge coexist: the Changing Markets Foundation's Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 report notes that H&M Group "has been reprimanded for its vague claims," "has been forced to remove 'sustainability' labelling," and "faces multiple legal cases," while identifying Inditex as reporting the second-highest disclosed volume of synthetic fibres in its sample — 178,030 tonnes, behind only Nike (Changing Markets Foundation, 2023). These are the NGO's evaluative, advocacy-toned observations, not adopted here as findings of this thesis; but their coexistence with H&M's transparency ranking illustrates Dhanjee et al.'s (2026) broader point that corporate disclosure volume and externally contested claim quality are separate dimensions. Transparency indices reward disclosure; they do not adjudicate it.

The same selective-emphasis logic shows up at brand level, in the two firms' consumer-facing product labels. H&M's decade-long "Conscious" label was retired in 2022; the company itself said the decision was "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), and no successor metric was disclosed at the point of retirement. This is coded EXTERNAL CHALLENGE: sustained outside scrutiny — the kind of market and normative pressure Delmas and Burbano (2011) count among greenwashing's principal external drivers — visibly altered a communication choice mid-program. Inditex's group-wide "Join Life" label — used across its brands, including Zara — took a different path in the very same reporting year. The 2022 report states that 61% of products complied with the Join Life standard, ahead of the company's own 50% commitment (Inditex, 2022, p. 187). That figure sits on the same page as the separate "preferred fibres" terminology realignment discussed above, but it was not itself flagged under any greenwashing indicator in the coding matrix. Read together, the two labels point to genuinely different corporate responses to comparable brand-level sustainability messaging: one company pulled its label under external pressure and did not say what would replace it; the other reported a figure that beat its own target without apparent challenge in the coded material. The contrast fits Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) finding that it is external scrutiny — clearly present for Conscious, less evident in the coded material for Join Life — that disciplines selective disclosure, not disclosure volume or labeling activity on its own.

5.3 Interaction Between Institutional and Competitive Pressures

The findings suggest that competitive signaling layers onto institutional-pressure-driven disclosure during discretionary periods and recedes when coercive structure binds — but only for one of the two firms. H&M's competitive-framing and peer-benchmarking count rises from one coded row in 2022 to three in 2023 and four in the FY2024 Progress Report, then falls back to one in the ESRS-structured 2025 report (Section 4.4). The peak coincides exactly with the CSRD-anticipation window identified in Section 5.1 — the period when H&M's regulatory conformity was still rhetorical rather than architectural. Read alongside Álvarez-Etxeberria et al.'s (2023) evidence that anticipatory periods are when disclosure behavior is most discretionary and peer-oriented, the pattern suggests that the same anticipatory space that accommodates "preparing to comply" language also accommodates differentiation claims; once the standardized, compliance-scoped format arrives, with its explicit no-target admissions and boundary caveats, promotional framing loses room. For Inditex, by contrast, competitive-signal rows stay low and flat across all four years (two, two, one, two), with no spike around its own 2024 ESRS transition (Section 4.4). There, institutional and competitive pressures simply coexist at low intensity — competitive framing appears to be a stable background register, not a report-format-driven one.

The wage finding adds a second interaction mode: domain-specific insulation. Inditex's environmental disclosure was substantially restructured by the coercive ESRS transition — quantified biodiversity progress, standardized topical chapters — yet its Living Wages sub-section reports the identical reach-only metric structure in every year on both sides of that transition (Section 4.3; Section 4.4). Institutional pressure, on this evidence, reorganized the disclosure domains it directly addressed while leaving the competitively sensitive wage domain untouched; the proprietary-cost logic (Lee & Lee, 2022) and the legitimacy-seeking logic operated side by side without one displacing the other. This coexistence is what Chapter 2 anticipated in juxtaposing peer-driven convergence with competitive withholding: firms can converge on headline frameworks while guarding granular detail. Finally, the two firms' anchoring styles reveal that the infrastructure of normative pressure and the instruments of competitive signaling are often the same artifacts used in different directions. H&M's competitive instances are mostly anchored to independently verifiable third-party scorecards — a CDP A score (2024; A− in 2023), a Textile Exchange "Leading" designation (one of six overall leaders of 130 participating companies in 2023, one of just 11 companies in 2024), a named comparison with Levi's on Screened Chemistry (H&M Group, 2023, pp. 15, 31; H&M Group, 2024b, pp. 5, 20) — that is, it cites the norm-carriers as rankings in which it performs well. Inditex's instances are more often self-referential benchmark-setting ambitions, such as framing Care for Water as an ambition to make it "a benchmark for the textile and leather sector" (Inditex, 2025, p. 144): aspiring to author the norm rather than to score against it. Neither register is mimetic in the institutional-theory sense (DiMaggio & Powell, 1983) — no imitation under uncertainty is being expressed — and in absolute terms competitive language remains a secondary feature of both firms' communication (Section 4.4).

5.4 Theoretical Implications

For Institutional Theory, the case pair's principal contribution is a resolution-level correction: the substantive–symbolic distinction discriminates poorly at firm level but well at theme level. Neither company is uniformly substantive or symbolic; H&M's symbolic responses cluster in biodiversity and the no-target social themes while its climate, water, and materials disclosure is consistently quantified, and Inditex shows the mirror-image cluster, quantified in biodiversity and materials but symbolic on living wages (Sections 4.4–4.5). This extends Fraser and van der Ven's (2022) firm-level mediation argument — that internal commitment determines whether pressure becomes change — into intra-firm territory: whatever mediates institutional pressure appears to operate per theme, plausibly tracking the maturity of each theme's measurement infrastructure, which is also the reading most consistent with Papa et al.'s (2024) sub-dimension-uneven decoupling. Studies that assign whole firms to conformity types risk averaging away exactly this variation. The timing evidence adds a second refinement: anticipatory isomorphism (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023) describes one observed absorption path (H&M's rhetoric-then-structure sequence) but not the other (Inditex's structure-without-rhetoric sequence), suggesting the anticipatory-talk phase is a contingent, not necessary, stage in how coercive pressure is metabolized into reporting practice.

For the decoupling and legitimacy literature, the recurrent MIXED (self-disclosed unfavorable trend) pattern and the self-labeled target limitations complicate the implicit association between decoupling and concealment. H&M's 2025 report itself states that its wage-management target "focuses on programme coverage rather than impact" (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 108–110) — the company naming its own means–ends gap — and both firms print their unfavorable scope 3 and effluent-plant trends in the same tables as their favorable baselines (Sections 4.2, 4.4). Loko and Schiehll (2025) measure policy–practice misalignment as something researchers detect; this corpus shows firms increasingly announcing it, which suggests legitimacy management through disclosure can incorporate calibrated candor rather than only positive asymmetry, without any inference about motive being required or made here. Where the greenwashing-indicator codings did fire, they concerned emphasis, boundaries, denominators, and metric consistency — never fabricated evidence. That distribution supports the definitional position Chapter 2 distilled from Gatti et al. (2019) and Spaniol et al. (2024): the workable demarcation between misleading and merely positive communication is evidentiary — substantiation, scope, comparability — rather than tonal. It also suggests the selective-disclosure construct (Nemes et al., 2022; Bernini & La Rosa, 2024) needs a procedural extension for the CSRD era: when omission can be produced upstream, through a materiality assessment that removes a topic from the reportable set, selectivity migrates from the text to the process that generates the text. Finally, the wage finding provides qualitative, paired-case corroboration for Lee and Lee's (2022) proprietary-cost mechanism and for Marquis, Toffel, and Zhou's (2016) global scrutiny-and-norms mechanism outside their original single-country and 45-country panel settings respectively — with the added specification, unavailable to either large-sample design, that the withholding was directional and category-specific rather than general.

5.5 Practical Implications

For report preparers, the findings identify an inexpensive drafting discipline with outsized effect on disclosure quality: comparator co-location. The difference between Inditex's climate units that were coded disproportional emphasis in 2022 and 2024, and those that were not in 2023 and 2025, is not the underlying data — the scope 3 figures were disclosed in all four years — but whether the unfavorable figure shares a sentence with the favorable one (Section 4.3). The same holds for denominators: H&M's resale metric exited the emphasis-candidate category the year its turnover share appeared in the same sentence as the growth claim (H&M Group, 2025, p. 90). Placing the large-denominator or unfavorable figure adjacent to the headline claim, and attaching a scale denominator to every absolute figure, would eliminate most of the indicator codings observed in this corpus without requiring any change in performance.

For regulators and assurance providers, the ESRS transition shows a double edge worth attention. On one side, the standardized format coincided with new candor: H&M's 2025 report states explicitly which topics carry no time-bound targets (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 112, 114, 118), admissions absent from the pre-ESRS reports. On the other, the materiality assessment becomes the critical control point: Inditex's exclusion of Affected Communities as non-material (Inditex, 2025, p. 121) is formally compliant behavior that nonetheless removes a previously reported theme from commitment-level disclosure. Scrutiny of materiality determinations — not only of reported content — is therefore where review effort buys the most. The retirement of H&M's "Conscious" label, described by the company as "hastened by recent external criticism and reinforced by emerging legislation" (H&M Group, 2022, pp. 13–14), further indicates that consumer-facing green-claims enforcement does register in corporate communication decisions. For investors, NGO raters, and other report users, the practical lessons are to read tables against narratives rather than narratives alone; to check which comparator a headline uses (year-on-year versus baseline, relative versus absolute); to treat outcome-named programs without outcome metrics — a "Living Wages" area reporting only reach counts — as a specific follow-up question; and to remember that transparency rankings measure disclosure rather than performance, as the coexistence of H&M's high index scores with its own self-disclosed unfavorable trends illustrates.

5.6 Limitations and Future Research

The design-level limitations set out in Section 3.5 — self-reporting bias inherent in corporate-authored documents, the two-case restriction to analytic rather than statistical generalization, and single-analyst coding without formal inter-coder reliability — all carry forward, and the findings now allow them to be stated more precisely. The single-coder limitation weighs most heavily on exactly the codes this chapter leans on: the disproportional-emphasis operational test, the two MIXED sub-patterns, and the omission sub-tests all require judgment about sentence adjacency, comparator flattery, and internal consistency, and these are the decisions a second coder would most plausibly contest. The audit trail in the coding matrices mitigates but does not replace that check. Second, the NGO triangulation in Section 5.2 was subsequently checked against primary sources: H&M's Fashion Transparency Index scores for 2022 (66%) and 2023 (71%), both firms' What Fuels Fashion? 2024 scores (H&M 61%, Zara/Inditex 41%), and the Changing Markets Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 observations are now confirmed directly against the primary documents. Two figures remain verified only at search-snippet level — Zara/Inditex's 2023 Index score (50%) and H&M's 2025 What Fuels Fashion? ranking (first of 200 at 71%) — and are flagged as such in the text; a previously cited "96% of H&M's assessed claims" figure was found not to appear in the Synthetics Anonymous 2.0 primary and was removed. The two remaining snippet-level figures should be read as directionally suggestive rather than confirmatory until their primaries are checked. Third, several findings are explicitly provisional in the coding itself: the FY2024 resale emphasis instance is a candidate pending a page/word-share measurement method, the Inditex 2025 water-denominator omission is a candidate pending verification against that report's methodology annex, and the H&M 2025 biodiversity decoupling flag is likewise a candidate rather than a confirmed instance. Fourth, the corpus imposed comparability ceilings the analysis could not lift: the firms' fiscal years cover offset twelve-month windows, and the headline materials metrics ("recycled or sustainably sourced" versus "lower-impact fibres") are definitionally non-comparable across companies — and, for Inditex's 2025 figure, not verifiably continuous across years — so no cross-firm headline-percentage comparison was drawn. Finally, document analysis cannot observe motive: the proprietary-cost interpretation of the wage-data gap is the reading most consistent with the pattern and the literature, but alternative explanations, such as differences in data availability or internal measurement practice, cannot be excluded from public reports alone, and no claim about intent is made.

These limitations translate directly into a research agenda. A second coder applied to the existing matrices, with formal inter-coder reliability on the emphasis and MIXED codes specifically, would be the single most valuable robustness step. Direct verification of the NGO index figures against primary documents would either harden or retire the triangulation. Extending the study window into the fully post-CSRD years would test two predictions this analysis generates: that H&M's competitive-framing count remains suppressed under the standardized format, and that Inditex's wage-disclosure gap either persists (supporting the proprietary-cost reading) or closes under widening value-chain reporting obligations (supporting a regulatory-lag reading). Adding a third case headquartered outside the EU's coercive reach — or an ultra-fast online-only competitor — would help separate what in these findings is CSRD-driven from what is sector- or firm-specific. And converting the disproportional-emphasis candidates into measured findings via a quantitative page- or word-share method, following the precedent of Garcia-Torres et al.'s (2017) action-statement counting, would put the emphasis findings on firmer footing than the sentence-adjacency test alone.

This chapter has interpreted the Results through the framework built in Chapter 2: coercive pressure was absorbed through two different sequences, responses proved substantive or symbolic theme by theme rather than firm by firm, and the selective-disclosure patterns — emphasis, denominators, procedural omission, and one directional withholding asymmetry — were shown to be consistent with proprietary-cost logic operating alongside, not instead of, legitimacy-seeking conformity. It has also drawn out what the case pair contributes to institutional, legitimacy, and decoupling scholarship, what practitioners and regulators might take from it, and where its evidentiary limits lie. Chapter 6 now concludes by answering RQ1 and RQ2 directly, stating the thesis's contribution, and closing the study within the boundaries the coded evidence supports.

6 Conclusion

This thesis set out to examine the credibility of fast fashion's environmental communication at its most consequential site: the published reporting of the industry's two largest firms, H&M Group and Inditex, across the 2022–2025 reporting years, precisely as European disclosure regulation shifted from a largely voluntary to an increasingly mandatory regime. Approaching that problem through Institutional Theory and a directed qualitative content analysis of the two firms' own documents, the study posed two research questions, which can now be answered in the terms the coded evidence supports. On RQ1, coercive, normative, and mimetic pressures are all identifiable in the coded material, but the most instructive finding concerns how differently the same pressures were absorbed. Coercive pressure left the clearest trace, and its signature is timing: H&M Group absorbed the approaching CSRD/ESRS regime first as language — anticipatory regulatory rhetoric from 2022, a hedged "aligned with" rather than compliant framing by FY2024 — and only in 2025 as reporting architecture, whereas Inditex converted to the full ESRS structure a reporting cycle earlier, in 2024, with little preceding anticipatory rhetoric in its coded material. Normative and mimetic pressures surface chiefly as convergence on shared voluntary frameworks such as SBTi-validated targets and field-standard terminology, rather than in peer-ranking language, which the analysis assigned to competitive signaling instead. As to conformity type, neither firm is uniformly substantive or symbolic: the distinction discriminates at the level of disclosure themes, in near mirror-image patterns — H&M consistently quantified on climate, water, and materials yet without biodiversity targets through 2025 (H&M Group, 2025, pp. 86–87), Inditex progressively quantified on biodiversity to 1.5 million hectares (Inditex, 2025, pp. 146–147) yet reach-only on living wages. Both firms' responses are therefore best characterized as mixed conformity, resolved theme by theme rather than firm by firm.

On RQ2, the coding identified page-cited selective-disclosure patterns in both firms' reporting — disproportional emphasis where favorable small-denominator achievements were narrated apart from the larger unfavorable figure, omissions of scale denominators for circularity and collection metrics, unclear claim boundaries, and inconsistent metrics where headline definitions changed without demonstrated continuity. Two qualifications frame these patterns. Most of the emphasis findings concern placement rather than concealment: the unfavorable figures were self-disclosed in the same documents, so what varied was narrative prominence, not the presence of the data. And the patterns were distributed across both firms rather than concentrated in one. The exception is directional: across all four coded years, Inditex's Living Wages Priority Impact Area — a program named after a wage outcome — reported only a people-reached count, never a wage-level or benchmark figure (Inditex, 2025, p. 178), while H&M published wage tables, an independent supplier pay-gap study, and a self-described coverage-rather-than-impact target; the cross-case coding found no comparably supported withholding running in the other direction. This asymmetry, specific to competitively sensitive supply-chain wage data in reports that quantify most other themes in detail, is the finding most consistent with the proprietary-cost account of competitive pressure (Lee & Lee, 2022). It remains, as throughout, a finding about disclosure practice, not about wages actually paid, and no claim about either company's intent is made or implied.

The thesis's contribution follows directly from these answers. To Institutional Theory and greenwashing scholarship, it offers a resolution-level refinement — the substantive–symbolic classification performs poorly applied to whole firms and well applied to themes — together with evidence that anticipatory regulatory rhetoric (Álvarez-Etxeberria et al., 2023) is a contingent rather than necessary stage in how coercive pressure becomes reporting practice, and paired-case qualitative corroboration that selective disclosure can operate procedurally as well as textually, migrating into the materiality assessment that determines what is reported at all. To practice, it identifies comparator co-location and denominator attachment as inexpensive drafting disciplines that would have removed most of the indicator codings observed, and directs regulatory and assurance attention toward materiality determinations rather than reported content alone.

These conclusions carry the limits appropriate to their design. A two-case qualitative study of self-authored documents, coded by a single analyst, supports analytic rather than statistical generalization, and several findings remain explicitly provisional; Section 5.6 details these constraints and the research agenda they generate, including second-coder reliability checks and extension into the fully post-CSRD years. Within those bounds, the study's closing observation is a modest but usable one: over four years in which the rules changed around them, the environmental communication of these two firms proved analysable not by its tone but by its evidence — substantiation, scope, comparability — and it is on that evidentiary ground, rather than on impressions of sincerity or spin, that corporate sustainability claims can and should be judged.
