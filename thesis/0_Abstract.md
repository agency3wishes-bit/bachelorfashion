# Abstract

This bachelor's thesis examines environmental communication in the fast-fashion industry through a comparative case study of H&M Group and Inditex across their 2022–2025 reporting years, a period spanning the European Union's shift from voluntary to mandatory sustainability disclosure. Using Institutional Theory as its primary lens, the study asks how cross-border coercive, normative, and mimetic institutional pressures shape the two firms' environmental communication and how their corporate responses compare in terms of substantive, symbolic, or mixed conformity, and how selective disclosure functions as a legitimacy-seeking mechanism shaped by market competition and information asymmetry. A directed qualitative content analysis of both firms' company-published annual and sustainability reports produced 237 page-cited coded units across ten disclosure themes. The analysis finds institutional pressure was absorbed through two distinct sequences — rhetorical anticipation preceding structural change at one firm, structural change with comparatively little preceding rhetoric at the other — and that substantive and symbolic conformity resolve more clearly by disclosure theme than by firm. Selective-disclosure patterns appear in both firms' reporting, alongside one directional asymmetry in the withholding of supply-chain wage data. The thesis contributes a theme-level refinement to institutional-conformity classification and a case-based account of selective disclosure under an evolving EU regulatory regime.
